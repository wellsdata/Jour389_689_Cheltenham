CRUMP HEADS
CHELTENHAM
Negro Formally Presented By
Group To O'Conor
Annapolis, Sept. 14 (Special) -
Governor O'Conor today told 75
Negro business and professional
leaders of the State and the board
of Cheltenham School for Boys, a
Negro institution, that first steps
have been taken by his administra-
tion for the post-war construction
and equipment of a new training
school for colored juvenile de-
linquents.
The group gathered in the ex-
ecutive offices in the State House
for the installation of Royal A. B.
Crump, the new superintendent of
Cheltenham and the first member
of his race to hold such a position
in Maryland.

Succeeds Thomas
He succeeds Vance E. Thomas,
who has resigned. Before coming to
Cheltenham, Mr. Crump was con-
nected with a Negro boys school in
Hanover county, Virginia.
The Governor said Mr. Thomas
"voluntarily and graciously' has
given up his position in favor of
a new superintendency because he
was convinced that such action
would be for the good of the school
and its pupils in whose progress
he was very genuinely interested."
The post-war correctional school,
the Governor said, will take the
place of Cheltenham, which will be
turned into an institution for the
care of Negro mental defectives
under the Board of Mental Hygiene.

Post-War Cheltenham
"Intensive consideration has
been given by the Department of
Public Welfare and the Cheltenham
School board concerning the selec-
tion of a site for the post-war
school," the Governor explained.
"Provision is being made in the
program of the Maryland Post-War
Commission for the necessary
buildings."
The Governor reviewed the
money spent on Cheltenham since
1939, when the Legislature pro-
vided $85,000 for new facilities.
Two years later, he said, an addi-
tional $131,000 was provided and
since then the Post-War Commis-
sion has programed approximately
$900,000 for post-war construction
when conditions permit.

$2,000,000 Since 1938
Besides, he said, $900,000 for
operating expenses has been pro-
vided, making a total of $2,000,000
"that has been put into the insti-
tution and its operating expenses
since this administration came into
office."
Nothing could be more hearten-
ing to Mr. Crump and his associ-
ates, the Governor said, than "the
knowledge first of all that the State
administration was heartily in ac-
cord both with his appointment and
with the new policy which such an
appointment indicated."
"Your presence here today in the
executive offices, at my invitation,
would certainly give ample evi-
dence of the favorable view which
this administration has of condi-
tions at Cheltenham at the mo
ment," the Governor continued.

"Favorably Impressed"
"We have been most favorably
impressed with the selection of the
board because of Mr. Crump's ad-
mitted experience and proven capa-
bilities in previous work of this
kind.
{ "The very fact that the installa-
tion is being held here would cer-
tainly give emphasis to our desire
to impress upon all concerned that
we are heart and soul in line with
Cheltenham's new policies and de-
lighted, indeed, to see the school at
last on the road toward permanent
solution of its problems."

"Program Of Education"
Mr. Crump was appointed in an
announced effort by the Governor
to place the entire management of
Cheltenham under Negroes. Mr.
Crump, who was introduced by Dr.
Frank S. Isaac, president of the
school's board, said he will advance
"a program of education for boys
who have not had opportunities."
Dr. Isaac described the condi-
tions at Cheltenham to those at-
tending the meeting and asked
that a new dining room, apartments
for employes and an intake cottage
be constructed as soon as possible.
He said the intake cottage might
be used to house boys still awaiting
sentence by the courts.

"Almost A Hog Pen"
He said that at present the kitch-
en at Cheltenham "is almost a hog
pen" and that the school has been
"indoor sport for one grand jury
after another in making investiga-
tions and reports."
Following short statements by
architects to the effect that priori-
ties could be obtained for the con-
struction of the new kitchen imme-
diately, the members of the board
were introduced to those present
by Dr. Isaac and the meeting was
adjourned.
Cheltenham board members who
attended included, in addition to
Dr. Isaac, Joseph P. Healy, Jr., Dr.
G. Canby Robinsoh, Junius Gray,
Howard C. Hill, Joseph C. Briscoe,
John L. Berry and Bertha в.
Proctor.
