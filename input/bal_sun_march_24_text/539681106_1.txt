To
The Editor
Cheltenham Schoo
TO THE EDITOR OF THE SUN-Sir:
As a member of the board of man-
agers of Cheltenham I would like
to express my reaction to the re-
port of the grand jury on that insti-
tution.
Grand juries have been dragging
the skeleton out of the closet at
Cheltenham and exposing it to the
public for twenty years without re-
sults. I have shared their disgust
and have been on the brink of resig.
nation. I am fully aware that after
the publication of this letter my
resignation may be a must,
The grand jury did a good and
thorough job with brutal frankness
in condemning the physical plant
and the substandard efficiency of
the staff. Your editorial likewise
exposed the plausible weakness of
a normally good board of directors.
But constructive criticism should.
be followed by practical solutions,
long overdue at Cheltenham.
A careful survey. and estimates
have been made of the cost of reno-
vating the present location, and the
figures approximate those for build-
ing a new school on a new site. Onę
possibility is that a new school for
Cheltenham could be built on the
State-owned land adjacent to the
Maryland School for Boys at Loch
Raven. Several other sites have
been offered, including one on the
Washington boulevard.
Several major problems cannot
and will never be solved as long as
Cheltenham remains at its present
location, 55 miles from Baltimore.
This means as high as 15 and 20
per cent vacancies on the staff with-
out much choice in getting better
qualified help. Cheltenham should
be moved to within 10 or 15 miles
of Baltimore. Ninety 'per cent of
the boys are from Baltimore city.
There is a plan pending for the
erection of a new dining room and
building at Cheltenham at the cost
of about $270,000. This, I think, is a
mistake and extravagant if the
State is sincere in giving us a new
school at a new site. This large sum
of money should go immediately
into the construction of a new
school.
A superintendent for Chelten-
ham should, for obvious reasons, be
appointed at the earliest possible
time. And a rational purge should
be made of the entire board of trus-
tees, let the chips fly wherever they
may. This, if done without any per-
sonal feelings, will go a long way in
solving one of Cheltenham's major
problems.
I also agree with The Sun that,
because of circumstances and for
the present at least, the school's
best chance for getting out of its
deep rut is to have a mixed board.
That is Maryland's pattern as ex-
emplified at Morgan College. I wish
it to be clearly understood that I do
not believe in or defend Maryland's
policies in making a distinction be-
tween races. We simply have no
practical alternative for Chelten-
ham except to fall in line with
Morgan. That is to say, to cut our
cloth to fit the pattern.
JAMES A, WHITE,
Baltimore, Dec. 22.
