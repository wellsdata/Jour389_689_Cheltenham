CARAVAN ON LAST LAP
Will Leave Upper Marlboro Today
For Washington.
Weary from a toilsome journey over
steep, dusty roads, four sunburned suf-
fragists arrived in Upper Marlboro last
night in their travel-stained prairie
schooner. So steep had been the hills
over which they had come, the women
had found it necessary to keep two of
their members at the brake in order to
keep the wagon from tumbling on top
of the trusty horses Margaret B. and
Susan B.
Twelve miles were covered by the suf-
fragists yesterday. They left Brandy-
wine early in the morning and arrived
at Cheltenham a little after noon. Here
they were entertained at dinner by John
Pyle, superintendent of the Cheltenham
Reformatory for Colored Boys. After
dinner about 60 people came over from
the town to attend a meeting, which was
held on the grounds of the school. The
300 boys of the school were all in dress
uniforms in recognition of the event
and a special speakers' stand was ar-
ranged. Wilfred H. Townsend, a resi-
dent of the town, who is a warm suf-
fragist, made one of the addresses.
The letter of introduction given them
by Mayor Preston was formally pre-
sented to John H. Traband, Mayor of
Upper Marlboro, by the caravaners Inst
night, preceding an open-air meeting
held in front of the Courthouse. Today
the caravan will move on to Washington
on the last lap of their journey before
the one that brings them back to Balti-
more. An elaborate welcome is being
planned for them by the Washington suf-
fragists, who will go out in decorated
automobiles to greet the cavavan in that
city this afternoon. The suffragists ex-
pect to make their triumphant entry at
5 o'clock.
