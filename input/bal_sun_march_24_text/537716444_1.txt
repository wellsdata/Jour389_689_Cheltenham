INSTITUTION
FOR 'UNSOCIAL'
YOUTHS URGED
Training School Heads
Favor Intermediate Unit
Of Correction
Establishment of an "intermedi-|
ate" correctional institution for the
detention of juveniles too young to
be sent to penal institutions for
adults, yet adjudged too "unsocial"
to associate in correctional juvenile
institutions with delinquent and de-
pendent youths, was urged yester-
day by heads of the State's four
training schools.
On the other hand, Harold E.
Donnell, State superintendent of
prisons, took the position that ex-
isting institutions created for de-
tention of delinquent and depend-
ent juveniles "should and can be
run to deal effectively with all
types of juvenile delinquents below
16 years and possibly some cases
between 16 and 18."
Figures Presented
Mr. Donnell presented figures
showing that in the year ended last
June 30, a total of 99 youths of 16
years and under were committed to
the House of Correction and the
Penitentiary, compared with 97
youths committed in the preceding
twelve months to the same penal
institutions.
Mr. Donnell further disclosed
that last year seven girls of 16
years and under were committed
to the Maryland State Reformatory
for Women at Jessup.
Although
each of the seven girls gave her age
as 16, the superintendent expressed
confidence that "several were not
yet 16."
Favored By Pratt
James A. Pratt, superintendent
of the Maryland Training School
for Boys at Loch Raven, is in favor
of immediate erection of a "spe-
cialized institution."
He further disclosed that the
board of managers for the Loch
Raven institution has approved
transfer of sufficient land for the
proposed institution, but "is averse"
to having it connected in any way
with the existing buildings, believ-
ing that it should be "operated en-
tirely separately from the regular
training school for average boys."
Basic or tentative drawings for
the buildings to comprise the sug-
gested institution to accommodate
about 125 have been made, Mr.
Pratt said, adding that plans called
for an administration building,
isolation section, hospital, shops,
dormitory and classrooms, esti-
mated to cost $400,000.
Mr. Pratt said such an institution
should not be run with the purpose
of handling any specific age groups.
Instead, he held it imperative that
it should be of the intermediate
training type with detention deter-
mined solely by "the behavior of
the individual."
"In other words, we need an in-
stitution that can handle difficult
psychopathic cases that cannot be
adapted to training for normal

such as the State training schools,"
Mr. Pratt said.
He pointed out that such an insti-

and heads of regular training
schools were authorized to commit
difficult-to-handle cases to it with-
out. first seeking court approval.
"When such an institution is
created, we ought to have careful
psychological and educational tests
of juvenile delinquents, so that we
can commit to it those unsuited to
open-type training school life,"
Mr. Pratt continued. "Such a unit,
of course, should be carefully.se-
cured, under constant guard, and
made difficult to get out of, as con-
trasted with our unfenced, open
institutions. Also, it should have
especial education and training fa-
cilities suited to the type of indi-
viduals that will be committed
to it.
"Such an institution could care
for the disturbers, those who can't
get along with the normal run of
individuals until they are trained
to do so. Most of those that would
be committed will be found to be
psychopathic cases.
"It is very, difficult to handle
such cases in the open-type school.
At Loch Raven, for example,
we have to put the worst cases
in a special detention cottage. That
requires that we have extra in-
structors, extra guards. The play-
ground is fenced; the door of the
cottage is kept under lock and.key.
"We find that very often, after
two to five months of correctional
training, the offender can be trans-
ferred to our open-type institution
and go on from there."
Not Equipped To Handle Type
Royal A. B. Crump, superintend-
ent of the State School for Colored
Boys at Cheltenham, discussing the
difficult-to-handle juveniles, said:
yesterday:
"We actually are not equipped.to
handle that type. We hesitate to
have them come in contact with our
regular boys of 11 years and up;
the influence is not good. But on
the other hand, we hesitate before
refusing to handle them because
there isn't any other place save
the penal institutions open to them
and they certainly should not be
sent thero.
"So often one of the difficult-to-
handle lads runs away because we
haven't the minimum security place
to maintain him and he frequently
gets into trouble with the law, so
that he commits a criminal offense
and thus is committed to a penal
institution.
"We feel that a separate unit
should be built for such boys, but
it ought not to be directly con-
nected with the established train-
ing school where we handle delin-
quents and dependent boys as well.
Many of the boys are ill-tempered,
high-strung, emotionally unstable,
and generally unsocial.
"They should, therefore, be iso-
lated from the rest of our plant.
But there is definitely a place
for such an intermediate training
school in the comprehensive over-
all training school program, yet it
certainly has no place in the penal
system."
• Favors Special Institution
Mrs. M. J. Spellman, assistant
superintendent and acting superin-
tendent of the Maryland Training
School for Colored Girls at Glen
Burnie, said:
"Difficult-to-handle cases can best
be accommodated in a special in-
stitution. If there was such a place
of detention it would make our job
of rehabilitating girls for society
much easier. But we must caution
that the hard-to-handle ought not to
be sent to penal institutions.
"Many of these juveniles are re-
claimable. They are simply out-of-
hand. Putting them with normal
juveniles makes it difficult to han
dle the latter, and reformation of
the hard-to-handle is impeded. If
these unfortunates are sent to penal
institutions, they will swing to the
worst patterns of life invariably."
Special Treatment Required
Mrs. Edith Gardner, superin-
tendent of the Montrose School for
(white) Girls, said:
"Fortunately we don't have too
many of the hard-to-handle cases,
but those we do get are generally
psychopathic cases requiring spe-
cial treatment to adjust them to
conventions of society.
"In institutions like our own.
it is hard to segregate them and
give them the special attention
they deserve.
"There should be a school be-
tween ours and the State Reforma
tory School for Women. The offend-
ers should not be placed with the
young children of our school, nor
should they be thrown with adult
offenders.
"They demand specialized train-
ing, and they can get it only in a
separate institution,"
Cites Loch Raven Cottage
Mr. Donnell pointed to establish.
ment of the minimum security cot-
tage at Loch Raven as "evidence of
what can be done by operating our
existing institutions to handle all of
the juveniles committed to them."
"Naturally some special facilities
may have to be provided," he said.
"But I don't think the youths with
good minds have to be handled in
a distinctly separate institution. It
may be that the feebleminded and
those of very low mentalities may
have to be housed separately. But
that is a problem for the mental
authorities. In recent years there
have been many of this type. For
example, a recent murder at the
Hagerstown reformatory was com-
mitted by a lad who had been in
Crownsville for four years."
Expressing the belief that sep-
arate institutions would have to be
provided for white and colored
boys, if it is decided to segregate
the difficult-to-handle youths in the
training schools of the State, Mr.
Donnell said:
"I don't see how we could afford
to do this. Improved facilities at
the respective institutions may be
the answer. Institutions must adopt
the policy of not trying to get rid
of an offender because he is hard
to handle; instead they should try
to save a child from becoming a
confirmed criminal through wrong-
ful associations in a penal insti-
tution."
