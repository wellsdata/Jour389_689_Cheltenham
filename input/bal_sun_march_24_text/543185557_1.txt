Board Announces Purpose
After Parley With Jones
On Personnel
Commissioner

Howard C. Hill, member of the
board of managers of the Cheltenham
School for Boys, said last night the
board would take its controversy with
Harry C. Jones, State Employment
Commissioner, over personnel issues
into court.
Hill said the decision was reached
at a conference between a committee
from the board and Jones. He an-
nounced:
"The seeming conflict between the
merit system act and the act making
Cheltenham a State institution will be
taken to court for settlement. The
procedure has not been finally de-
cided."
May Center On Pay Roll
He indicated the court test possibly
would be precipitated by submitting a
pay roll to Jones, including the names
of twenty-six employes Jones has
ordered dismissed, then seeking a
mandamus action when Jones refuses
to sign it.
At the start of the conference, Jones
told the committee that the "lack of
perspective and initiative" character-
izing private management of the in-
stitution could not continue under
State direction.
Jones, who has recommended dis-
missal of half of Cheltenham's per-
sonnel as "unfit for any State serv-
ice," warned a committee of board
members that the board must accept
the State's plan for reorganizing the
school or contest it in court.
Clause In Act At Issue
Cheltenham became a part of the
State penal system last October 1, by
an act of the 1937 Legislature. At
issue yesterday was a section of the
1937 act requiring Cheltenham em-
ployes to be blanketed into the State
merit system without examination.
"This is impossible," Jones told the
committee, at a conference called to
discuss "technicalitics" in a reor-
ganization plan Jones offered the
Cheltenham board last week. The
board asked for the conference.
He asserted that "whoever was re-
sponsible" for drawing the legisla-
tion transferring Cheltenham to the
State was not "entirely candid."
"They concealed from both the Gov-
ernor and the Legislature what they
must have known," he contended, re-
ferring to the authors of the bill,
"about the obvious unfitness of the
employes
an unfitness which
caused them to reserve the sum of t
$30,000 to take care of such of the

employes as might be rejected in a
contingency such as now has arisen."
Under private management, Jones
said, the Southern Maryland reforma-
tory, "like many other similar insti-
tutions, has failed in its mission"
chiefly because of lack of funds, lack
of experienced technical direction and
the "virtual admission by all con-
cerned that the institution was a local
project fostered by local interests and
having little to do with the State as
a whole."
Cheltenham's development since its
founding sixty-seven years ago has
been retarded, Jones said, by divi-
sion of responsibilities and "well-
meaning but inadequate efforts." Its
managers must "make a clean break"
with past practices under the State's
direction, he warned.
Should the board contest his posi-
tion in court, he added, "the Com-
missioner doubts exceedingly whether,
in the well-known state of public
opinion as regards Cheltenham, any
Maryland court will assume a respon-
sibility which the State Employment
Commissioner has definitely declined."
He asserted the institution's future
depends on its personnel as well as
its management and he felt the con-
struction he placed on the Chelten-
ham legislation was "sensible and
practicable."
Urges Acceptance in Principle
He said he "refused to believe" the
Legislature "intended that Chelten
ham should be continued as it is now
being run for the next generatior
under State auspices and at the
State's expense."
Jones advised the committee that,
in his opinion, the board should ap-
prove his recommendations in prin-
ciple and appoint another committee
to confer with him in connection with
placing them in operation as "promptly
as circumstances will permit."
Jones said he wanted to "make one
thing clear"-that "nothing in my
survey or in this memorandum sanc-
tions the continuance of the super-
intendent, assistant superintendent,
executive secretary, director and
parole officer" of the institution in
their present jobs.
Considers Contributory
State Retirement System
Maryland is contemplating shifting
some of the burden of its State em-
ployes' retirement system onto the
employes, Harry C. Jones, State Em-
ployment Commissioner, disclosed
yesterday.
Jones, supervising head of the
State's present pay-as-you-go retire-
ment plan, said the system's board of
trustees was investigating a "contribu-
tory pension system set up on actuarial
lines."
Under the pay-as-you-go policy the
State carries the entire pension load,
appropriating fixed sums annually to
care for the pension fund. Under the
contributory system, the State would
match contributions from employes,
whose portion would be deducted
from their salaries.
Jones, ex-officio secretary of the
board, said he and the chairman of
The board, William S. Gordy, Jr., State
Comptroller, had held a preliminary
conference with George Buck, actuary
of the State teachers' retirement sys-
em and the Baltimore city employes'
Dension system, on the contributory
pian.
Pending presentation of the new
system to the Legislature, Jones said,
the, pay-as-you-go system would
"doubtless" be continued. The plan,
marking the State's first retirement
system for classified employes, was
set up last year with a $40,000 appro-
priation for 1938.
