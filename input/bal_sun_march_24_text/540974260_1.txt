Private Bids Few
Participation of private citizens
in bidding for items offered at an
auction at the Appraisers' Stores
reached a new low when Federal
restrictions made many items sale-
able only to wholesalers, others out
of reach because of the large num-
ber of ration points required.
Flowers from all over the world,
via cable, radio and telegraph were
ordered by hundreds in the services
for delivery to Baltimore homes
today, florists reported.

the Baltimore Transit Company an-
nounced it would discontinue serv-
ice on the Federal street branch of
the No. 27 trackless trolley line,
effective next Sunday.

Letters To The
Service Sun
Baltimore, Md., Sunday, April 25, 1943
"Even though we are only in
Louisiana, Baltimore seems a long
way from here. The service paper,
bringing happenings in Baltimore
to us, is just like a magic carpet,"
wrote Private Ambrose O. Miller,
4008 Woodbridge road, from Camp-
Claiborne.
Private M. F. Murkey, serving
with a marine unit in the South
Pacific, whose home is at 822 North
Monroe street, wrote that "you
can't realize what a boost it is to
my spirits, also a source of pleasure
for the other four fellows in my
company from Baltimore. Many of
the men not natives of Baltimore
read it also and get almos as much
kick out of the Service Sun as we
do."
Relayed by his sister was this
comment of Private Bernard Johan-
nesen, somewhere in New Guinea,
who lived at 2102 Orleans street:
"The Service Sun is my means of
knowing what is going on in the
big town. There have been so many
changes that I will feel like a Rip
Van Winkle when I return."
"I received the copy of the ser-
vice edition of The Sun and it was
swell," Capt. Daniel L. Drake, on
duty overseas, said in a letter to
his wife here. "Gosh, that small
paper had more news in it than half
a dozen local papers. Are they in
the daily paper? How about send-
ing them more often if they are?
I really did enjoy it and thanks
loads."
Private Francis X. Myers, a
World War veteran in the army in
North Africa, said: "Thanks a mil-
lion. You can't imagine what a lit-
tle news from home does for a
guy."

Blandford Resigns
After the Cheltenham School for
Boys board of managers voted to
abolish segregation of white and
Negro employes of the institution,
Joseph H. Blandford, board presi-
dent, sent his resignation to Gov-
ernor O'Conor and twenty-orne
white employes said they'd resign.
The board replied that an all-Negro
staff for the Negro institution was
its aim.
