CHELTENHAM
BOARD MEETS
Interviews Four Candidates
For Superintendent
The board of managers of the
Cheltenham School for Boys yes-
terday interviewed four candidates
without selecting a new superin-
tendent for the State training
school for Negroes.
They also asked two State agen-
cies for help in determining what
can be done to improve the school,
which cares for 215 to 235 boys
assigned there by juvenile courts.
Governor Lane several weeks
ago said his attention had been
called to conditions which needed
immediate correction.
A joint conference between the
board of managers and State Wel-
fare Department officials decided
the first step to be taken was ap
pointment of a new superintendent.
The institution has been admin.
istrated for more than eighteen
months by H. R. Harris, acting
superintendent.
Candidates Considered
He was one of those considered
yesterday by the board of man
agers.
The others were:
Calvin L.. Kiah, a school prin-
cipal from Cambridge, Md.
Benson C. Tipton, an employé of
the National Traninng School for
Boys at Washington,
Garland J. Gilliam, principal of
Bet Alton High School in Charles
county, near La Plata,
A fifth candidate asked that his
name be withdrawn. He was George
M. Crawford, principal of Robert
Moton High School at Westminster.
The board accepted the resigna-
tion of James W. Childs, who for
less than a year had been director
of security and acting assistant
superintendent.
Decisions Reached
After discussing conditions
brought to light in the joint con-
ference with the Welfare Depart-
ment, the managers decided to:
1. Ask the Department of Public
Improvements and John B. Funk,
chiet engineer, to make a survey
of the physical plant at Chelten
ham and suggest what new con-
struction is needed.
2. Ask Walter Kirkman, director
of the State Department of Budget
and Procurement, to make a simi-
tar survey on the condition of
present facilities.
The board also decided to hold
regular monthly meetings the first
Tuesday in each month, beginning
August 5.
