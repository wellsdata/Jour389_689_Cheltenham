Coal Miners And Their Work
TO THE EDITOR OF THE Sun-Sir:
In your editorial on John L. Lewis
April 14, you dogmatically state
that the miners "certainly are not
working harder."
Unless someone has made a com-
plete survey with a gadget which
measures the exact amount of
physical as well as nervous energy
expended by each individual miner,
I don't see how you can be certain
that they are not working harder.
I, personally, agree with Mr. Lewis.
MRS. PETER B. OLNEY 2D.
Baltimore, April 16.
The two major operations in
soft-coal mining are cutting the coal
out of the coal veins and loading
the stuff on cars for transport out
of the mines. In 1918 mechanical
labor-saving devices for loading
coal accounted for less than one
per cent. of the total output. In
1942 40.7 per cent. of total out-
put was loaded by labor-saving me-
chanical devices. In 1918 only 56.8
per cent. of total output was cut by
means of labor-saving mechanical
devices. In 1942 89 per cent. was
cut by such devices.
In 1923, the earliest year for
which the statistical abstract of the
United States has such figures, the
prevailing hours of labor per week
for the overwhelming proportion of
bituminous wage earners were 44
to 52 hours, and some worked up
to 62 hours and more. It is unlikely
that working hours were shorter in
1918, a war year. But in February.
1943, average weekly working hours
were 37.3-Editor, The Sun.
On The Cheltenham Case
TO THE EDITOR OF THE SUN-Sir:
I was shocked beyond words when
I read the statement which accom-
panied the resignation. of Mr.
Joseph H. Blandford as president
of the board of managers of the
Cheltenham School for Boys.
I have always respected the views
and opinions of others even when
they differed from my own and I
am willing to concede that Mr.
Blandford had the right to express
himself as he felt about the matter
of racial discrimination at the in-
stitution which he served for so
many years.
I write this letter to you in the
hope that Mr. Blandford will ex-
amine himself, his ideas, and his
philosophy of life, in the light of
the generic story of the creation,
La part of which story was used by
that great American, Thomas Jef-
ferson, in writing the Declaration
of Independence which states
among other things, "that all men
are created equal...."
WALTER T. DIXON.
Baltimore, April 15.
