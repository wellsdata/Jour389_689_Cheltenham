WHY THE DELAY?
The bill by which the reformatory for
colored boys at Cheltenham is to be taken
over and administered by the State has been
in the State Senate's Finance Committee for
several weeks and, although other bills have
gone through the committee, is still un-
reported. In view of the fact that the bill
is the result of an agreement between the
present Cheltenham trustees and the Board
of State Aid and Charities, and was intro-
duced by fourteen members of the Legis-
lature representing both parties, this delay
is strange and disturbing.
It is doubtless true, as it is always true
when changes of this sort are contemplated,
that various interests are not entirely satis-
fied, and it is sometimes true that unworthy
political motives intrude to block measures
which of themselves are not criticized. The
arguments on this matter have been in
progress for a long time, however, and the
bill now before the Senate committee at
Annapolis represents thought and investiga-
tion by M. Hampton Magruder and Enoch
Harlan for the Cheltenham trustees and
Morris A. Rome for the State, and the
willingness of all concerned to concede a
point here and there for the sake of agree-
ment on the proposal as a whole. That agree-
ment has been reached by the parties in in-
terest, and the agreement is represented in
this bill. It ought to be reported promptly.
If the delay is occasioned by genuine
doubts about the bill itself, those who enter-
tain such doubts should reveal them at once.
The situation at Cheltenham calls for frank-
ness and a willingness to subordinate any in-
stinct to spar for political advantage to a
greater necessity. Too much hesitation will
convince the State that these qualities are not
sufficiently represented at Annapolis.
