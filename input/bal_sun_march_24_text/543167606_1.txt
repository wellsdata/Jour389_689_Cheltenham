LINKS POLITICIANS
WITH CHELTENHAM
Rev. Dr. Frederick Douglas
Explains His Resignation
From Board
Continues In His Post As
Chief Parole Officer,
However
Hints at political maneuvering at the
Cheltenham School for Boys were
made last night by the Rev. Dr. Fred-
erick Douglas, pastor of Douglas Me-
morial Community Church, in explain-
ing his resignation and that of three
other Negro members of the school's
board.
Dr. Douglas, however, will continue
as chief parole officer at the institu-
tion.
Called "Wonder Workers"
The four who resigned, including
Willard W. Allen, Joseph Briscoe and
Dr. George McDonald, quit in order to
make room for "wonder workers" to be
appointed by Governor Nice, Dr.
Douglas said. The Governor recently
demanded the board accept reorgani-
zation plans, submit plans of its own,
or resign. The board has refused to
comply.
"There are pioneers in every great
movement, and I am profoundly grate-
ful to our good Governor for making it
possible for me to be one of the
pioneers in initiating the much-needed
reforms that are being so strongly
urged at this time," Dr. Douglas said
in a statement.
Competent Man Needed
"Together with the Messrs. Mc-
Donald. Briscoe, Allen, Spencer and
Hill, I have helped make Cheltenham
a State institution, and when the mat-
ter came before the board for the
selection of a superintendent, six
members, namely, Dr. George Mc-
Donald, Prof. Joseph Briscoe, Willard
W. Allen, Dr. John O. Spencer, Howard
C. Hill and myself, contended that the
institution needed a competent super-
intendent.
"We felt that it ought to be the
chief concern of the board to select
a man specifically trained for that
work, and that was the most impor-
tant thing to be considered. Hence,
we went on record as being opposed
to the present incumbent, but, being
in the minority, our wishes were de-
feated.
Cites "Political Leaders"
"We had been told by some so-called
political leaders of our own group that
we ought to contend for an entire
Negro personnel.
"We should like to see the wish of
these folk gratified, but we fail to find
a way in which this can be accom-
plished immediately, because anybody
with two grains of common sense
ought to know that six men can never
outvote nine, and, even if it were pos-
sible to so maneuver Roberts' rules of
order to the effect that a minority vote
should have precedence over the
majority, there are any number of
things entering into the situation down
there that would not make it a wise
thing to try to accomplish overnight."
Five Vacancies Next Month
Dr. Douglas explained there were
five vacancies to occur on the board
next month-the posts of the four re-
signed members and that of Dr.
Spencer, a white member.
"We have been told on good au-
thority that our good Governor has
promised to reappoint Dr. Spencer and
to replace the four colored men, who
have labored faithfully and well, with
four other colored men who shall be
able to get things done at Cheltenhan
in shorter order than we have been
able to do; and, since he has promised
our positions to these wonder workers,
we folk who have been there all these
years and have had every opportunity
to do what they are now anxious to
do, we thought it might be a good
idea to let Governor Nice have the
pleasure of appointing them to the
board at the earliest possible moment."
Dr. Douglas added that "it must be
remembered that Cheltenham has been
under fire for years and that the con-
ditions that obtain there are not of
recent development, but the result
of years of inactivity on the part of
the colored people of the State and
neglect on the part of the dominant
group who largely, if not entirely,
dictated the management of the in-
stitution."
Resignations Voluntary,
Governor Nice Asserts
Commenting on the statement of
the Rev. Dr. Frederick Douglas on
conditions prevailing at the Chelten-
ham School for Boys, Governor Nice
last night said:
"The resignations of the four mem-
bers that I have received came to me
voluntarily, and I quite agree with the
Rev. Dr. Douglas that six never can
outvote nine. I know that the four
who have resigned have done the best
that they could under trying circum-
stances and insurmountable handicaps.
"I know that they resigned in order
that their people might be furnished
the opportunity of learning that their
best efforts in the face of these handi-
caps could not prevail.
"I do not for a moment think that I
can replace them with wonder work-
ers. There can be no correction of the
!
terrible conditions short of majority
appointments to the board. I feel that
the four have done to the limit the
best they could under the handicap-
ping circumstances."
