Cheltenham-
By Any Other Name
Beginning next month there will be no
Cheltenham School for Boys, with all the
memories of filth, brutality and bad man-
agement that name conjures up. The in-
famous reformatory school for Negro boys
will be known officially as "Boy's. Village of
Maryland." A full-time psychologist and a
part-time psychiatrist will be added to the
staff at that time to carry out the "Boy's
Town" tradition of close personal guidance
and moral rehabilitation.
Perhaps the most significant change that
is taking place at Cheltenham is a shift in
the age group being handled by the institu-
tion. The State Reformatory at Breatheds-
ville is being used for older delinquents
from. 16 to 23 years of age, while the
younger boys-including those only 8 and 9
years old are being sent to Cheltenham.
Under the guidance of the new Cheltenham
superintendent, Mr. Lawson J. Vency, the
younger boys are being encouraged to com-
pete on a friendly cottage-to-cottage basis
in housekeeping, athletics and academic
progress.
This is not the first time that there have
been reports of reform measures under way
at Cheltenham. There have been many
such reports in the past, and always they
have been followed by later reports that
the institution was too antiquated and
poorly located to make it an effective train.
ing school. Mr. Veney gives at least one
hint that the basic things wrong with the
school have not changed much for the better
when he speaks of his "terrific problem
getting an adequate staff."
The public, periodically shocked by bad
reports on Cheltenham, will certainly be on
Mr. Veney's side if he can make Cheltenham
by any other name smell better than it
used to. But, while wishing Mr. Veney the
best of luck, the public has justification for
keeping its fingers.crossed.
