No Increase Sought
In State Milk Price
No immediate increase in the
price of milk in Maryland is fore-
seen by the Maryland Co-operative
Milk Producers, Inc., W. Charles
Rutledge, a co-operative director
from Jarrettsville, declared list
night.
The co-operative has agreed, Mr.
Rutledge stated, to "stand pat" on
milk prices, which were raised by
1 cent last July. Shippers to the
co-operative have adopted a "hold-
the-line" policy and enjoy a high
sales volume, he reported.
Mr. Rutledge's statement fol-
lowed a report from Washington
that a co-operative of Maryland and
Virginia producers which supplies
Washington and Virginia had asked
for an increase in milk prices by
1 cent a quart.
A petition was filed with the Vir-
ginia Milk Commission by Mary-
land and Virginia Milk Producers,
Inc.. asking the price rise. It was
set for hearing in Alexandria, Va.,
October 17.
In the last director's meeting of
the co-operative supplying Mary-
land. Mr. Rutledge said, it was de-
cided "not to try to ram another
increase down the consumers'
throats at this time."

Near 100% Co-Operation
Officials at a number of hospitals,
Including the Hopkins, described
their plans as both conservation
and economy measures.
P. J. McMillin, superintendent
of the City Hospitals, said co-
operation there would be virtually
100 per cent.
He said he had instructed the
dietary staff to put the recom-
mendations in effect "except as
doctors may require."
He added, however, he had been
assured by physicians that, except
for an almost negligible number,
patients could be provided a
"nourishing and adequate,diet
under the President's restrictions."
Not An Innovation
A meatless day will not be an
innovation, Mr. McMillin sald.
Budget restrictions, coupled with
soaring meat costs, already had
caused the hospital to observe a
meatless day each week for some
three months, he declared.
Harold A. Sayles, acting director
at University Hospital, said he can-
celed an order of fowl which
would have been served at the hos
pital Thursday, and that a vege-
table plate would be put on the
menu instead.
Making the single exception of
patients whose health requires cer-
tain foods, Mr. Sayles asserted:
"We intend to follow the Presi-
dent's directive to the letter."
Some Misgivings On Eggs
Union Memorial Hospital also
laid plans yesterday to comply with
the food-saving plea, according to
Carroll D. Hill, hospital director.
There were some misgivings
about cutting out eggs, Mr. Hill
said. "With people who are sick."
he added, "it's hard to find a sub-
stitute for eggs for breakfast."
Mr. Hill declared, however, that
Union Memorial "is going along as
closely as possible to the Presi-
dent's plan."
Both St. Agnes and Bon Secours
hospitals said that, although plans
had not been completed, they
planned to carry out President
Truman's recommendations.
