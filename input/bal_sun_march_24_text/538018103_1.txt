NICE DENIES
ROBERTS STORY
ON CHELTENHAM
Insists Board Was Con-
sulted About Move For
State Control
CLAIMS CHAIRMAN
WAS "MISTAKEN"
Refers To Conference On
Acquisition Of Colored
Boys' Reformatory
Governor Nice yesterday said that
the board of directors of the House
of Reformation for Colored Boys, at
Cheltenham, had been consulted when
legislation looking to the acquisition
of the reformatory was being dis-
cussed at the last General Assembly.
The Governor made this statement
after Robert F. Roberts, president of
the board, said "we have not been
asked to state our position" on a move
by the State to take over the operation
of the institution.
Roberts "Mistaken"
"Mr. Roberts is entirely mistaken,"
the Governor said. "A number of the
members of the board were in consul-
tation with me and Thomas J. S. Wax-
ter, director of the Municipal Depart-
ment of Welfare, and Sidney Hol-
lander, member of the Board of State
Aid and Charities, during the last ses-
sion of the Legislature.
"The conference was held before
legislation which would have set up
the reformatory as a State institution

Situation Weighed
"The situation as it exists was gone
into very thoroughly and the con-
sensus of the members present-and
more than half were present-was to
the effect that the board would neither
approve or disapprove legislation
looking to State control.
"My personal reaction and memory
is that the board was very decidedly
of the opinion that the State should
take over the institution.
"And I am wondering how many
visits, if any, Mr. Roberts has made
to that institution since he has been
president or even a member of the
board."
Mr. Roberts, before leaving for a
three-month trip to the West Coast,
said that in his opinion the "board will
vote against turning the home over
to the State."
The last session of the Legislature
passed a bill empowering the State to
accept the property of Cheltenham if
offered by the board of that institu-
tion. A companion measure, which
would have set up the home as a State
institution, failed to pass.
Mr. Waxter, while Juvenile Court
judge, made an investigation of the
institution and recommended at that
time that Cheltenham be taken over
by the State.
In his capacity of Director of Wel-
fare for Baltimore, Mr. Waxter reiter-
ated his stand of several years ago.
Cheltenham, a privately owned and
operated institution for Negro boys
between the ages of 10 and 21 years,
is supported by a State appropriation
of $20,000 a year and by payments of
$180 a year from the counties and city
for each boy committed.
Baltimore contributes more than
$60,000 a year to the institution under
this system.
