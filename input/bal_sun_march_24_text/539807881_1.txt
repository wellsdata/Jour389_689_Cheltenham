MENTAL HOSPITAL
NEED FOUND VITAL
Fire Hazards, Lack Of Proper
Segregation Facilities
Are Reported
| The Maryland Commission on
Post-War Reconstruction and De-
velopment had before it yesterday
a story of fire hazards, lack of
proper segregation for patients and
unsuitable housing facilities for
attendants at State mental hos-
pitals.
Walter N. Kirkman, State direc-
tor of budget and procurement,
said projects to remedy the condi-
tions should be placed high on the
schedule for post-war construction.
In the fire-hazard class he par-
ticularly noted the industrial build-
ing at Spring Grove State Hospital.
The present building, he said,
would offer little resistance to con-
Nagration. Mattresses and other in-
flammable objects are made there.
He submitted a proposal for a new
building to cost $120,000.
Cottages Wanted
Mr. Kirkman said there is a lack
of proper segregation for patients
at Springfield State Hospital. Re-

quests are on file, he said, for cot-
tages for one hundred women; at
a-cost of $150,000, and a cottage
for one hundred men patients, also
at $150,000.
An attendants' home for fifty
employes is asked at the same hos-

Mr. Kirkman said the Legisla-
ture, often sympathetic with the
needs of patients, is inclined to re-
ject proposals for the comfort and
convenience of hospital employes.
This, he contended, is one reason
for the difficulty in keeping proper
personnel at tire institutions.
Equipment Limited
When, the cost of lighting, re-
frigeration and sewerage for an at-
tendant's home comes into the pic-
ture, he said, the Legislature will
say: "You have a dynamo at the
main plant, you have a sewer. Just
hook them up with the new build-
ings."
"They don't realize," he said,
"that those pieces of equipment
have limitations."
An admitting building for 120
women patients at Spring Grove
would cost $220,000 according to
the proposal. The building, he said,
is needed to make it possible to
properly assign patients for treat
ment. Such assignments, he de-
clared, offer the best opportunities
for recovery.
More farm land-an estimated
$28,000 worth-also is needed at
Spring Grove, he said, because
much of the land there now is not
suitable for farming and doesn't
provide the prescribed half acre a
patient.
Building For Alcoholics
The Eastern Shore State Hos-
pital, he said, is badly in need of
a building for alcoholics. The pro-
posal calls for a structure to house!
eighty patients at a cost of $100.-
000. At present, he said, it is neces-
sary to house them with psycho-
pathic cases.
"They are not psychopathics,"
■ he said; "and it would be to the ad-
vantage of both the psychopathics
and alcoholics if they were treated
in separate hospitals."
A total of $480,000 is asked for
improvements at Cheltenham
School for Boys.
The school. he pointed out, has
been the subject of controversy for
years. The buildings are all very
old and a complete vocational and
educational program is needed.
Environment Important
"To train those boys," he said,
"you must provide a proper en-
vironment and a proper staff."
The proposals call for a voca-
tional building and equipment, re-
modeling and reconditioning of
four old cottages, additional facili-
ties for the staff and supplemen-
tary services.
A new women's dormitory, he
said, is needed at Morgan State
College.
Improvements sought at the
tuberculosis sanitoriums would
cost $262,000 at Maryland State
Sanitorium: $3,000 at Salisbury
and $65,000 at Henryton,
$6,889,396 Urgent
All the proposals listed as urgent
total $6,889,396. Listed as slightly
less urgent are projects totaling

Members of the commission
pointed out that the $2,000,000 set
aside by the State for post-war
improvements is "only a drop in
the bucket" and would be so even
with most of the $4,000,000 in the
current emergency funds.
Dr. Abel Wolman, vice-chairman
of the committee, pointed out there
have been years in the past-even
with no unemployment emergency
