The Rich
Harvest
At Solomons
This is the first of two short
articles on the remarkable bar-
gain which the State got when it
acquired the amphibious training
base near Solomons Island from
the Navy last fall. The second
article will describe some of the
potentialitics of this base for the
State when the present big sal
vage operation is finished.
The State government is bring-
ing in a rich harvest from the 118-
acre peninsula between Back and
Mill creeks near Solomons Island.
The harvest consists of an immense
quantity of building supplies-
finished building supplies: electric
wiring and lighting fixtures, oak
flooring, radiators, windows and
doors and their frames, wood joists
and heavy roof beams, pipes of
many kinds and sizes. There is
just about everything required to
build a house, a small office build-
ing or any other relatively simple
structure. And each item is there
to be harvested in abundance.
The materials are being taken
from what is known in official rec-
ords as the United States Naval
Operational Development Center
(Atlantic). But the tract is better
known as the amphibious training
base. It was there that many of the
men who fought in the Pacifie
learned about beach warfare.
In its heyday five years ago the
base could house thousands of
troops. It was a small but complete
city, with its own modern water
a
system, its own electric power and
heating plants, its own sewage-
disposal plant. In addition, the base
had numerous barracks, a large
hospital, recreation centers,
series of substantial piers, a marine
railway-all the facilities of a
thriving community and an armed
services center. But when the war
ended the Navy had no further use
for the base..
-0-
It was then that an agreement
was reached between the Navy and
the State government. On payment
of $5,000-just $5,000-the State
was given the authority to strip
the peninsula of the improvements
made by the Navy. The only ex-
ceptions are the sewage-disposal,
water and power plants and a few
other selected items that will not
readily deteriorate and could be put
to use more or less swiftly if the
base were needed in the future.
The State began its stripping
operations last fall, By the first of
this month building materials with
a value of $200,000 had been re-
claimed and segregated. The total
will be increased to $250,000 before
this phase of the job is completed,
To date, materials valued at more
than $106,000 have been delivered
to State institutions for public-
building, projects. Reference to the
minutes of the State Board of Pub-
lic Works provides evidence of the
many uses to which these materials
from Solomons are being put.
Last month, on the recommenda-
tion of Mr. John B. Funk, the State
chief engineer, the board author.
ized the Cheltenham School for
Boys to convert a farmhouse into
two apartments for school em-
ployés. There will be no expendi-
ture of State funds; the materials
will come from Solomons and the
labor will be furnished by the
personnel at Cheltenham.
The construction of twelve small
apartments, one large apartment
and twelve single bedrooms at
Princess Anne College also was
authorized on the recommenda-
tion of Mr. Funk. The use of ma-
terial from Solomons will make an
appreciable reduction in the cost
of the new living quarters.
At the same time the board
authorized the use of materials
from Solomons for the construction
of employés' cottages at Crowns-
ville State Hospital and for a ga-
rage at the Eastern Shore State
Hospital. And slop tanks were al
lotted to the Spring Grove State
Hospital. At every board session
there is some talk about materials
from the old Navy base.
-0-
Needless to say, the stripping of
the base and the distribution 'of
the materials are not being done
for nothing. Both jobs are being
done under the direction and super-
vision of Mr. Funk. The actual
work of tearing down the build-
ings and the segregation of the
many items is being done by prison
labor, The prisoners-usually there
are about 80 of them, including
cooks and helpers-live at the base
in a partly reconditioned building.
As of June 1 the prison labor bill
had mounted to $38.500. The total
includes the pay for the guards and
the $3 per month given each pris-
oner for his work. But all of that
$38,500 is not lost to the State.
About half of it goes into the State
industries account of the Depart-
ment of Correction and will be
used to help finance the penal in-
stitutions. So, while the State has
been paying out $38,500 on one
hand, it has been receiving $20,000
back on the other hand. Just a
bookkeeping item, in part.
There is also an item of $20,000
for operating costs, which includes
the financing of actual operations
at the base and the delivery of ma-
terials to the sites of construction.
That $20,000 is money spent. It
isn't going back into another
pocket. of the State. But, all in all,
the State has not spent more than
$45.000, including the $5,000 pay-
ment to the Navy. That means that
for every dollar the State has
paid out for the stripping opera-
tions, it has received $4 in return.
That is the kind of business trans-
action one rarely if ever finds in
the operation of public enterprise.
E. V. BAUGH, JR.
