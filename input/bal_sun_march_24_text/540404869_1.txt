TRACK IS HALF-MILE WONDER
Large Crowd Gathers About Course
To See Events On Program Of Big
Four-County Fair.
The Laurel (Md.) Four-County Fair and
race meeting began yesterday under most
encouraging auspices. For an opening day
the attendance was large. The race track
was a curiosity to the horsemen. It is a
full half-mile course. The building of the
track was started August 19, and it was
finished, last week. Forces worked night
and day for 40 days. They accomplished a
work which was thought by many to be
Impossible.
Time made in the races yesterday showed
the capabilities of the course. The many
horsemen present had no fault to find
with the track, and as there are 178 horses
entered for the harness races alone this
is the best evidence of the track being a
good one. It is a Fuler's earth base and
mixed loam topping.
Both harness horses and runners took
to the going kindly. The track has good,
long stretches, broad turns and is 60 feet
wide on the homestretch. It is nearly as
wide on the backstretch. The association
has land enough to extend the homestretch
by a chute and have seven-eighths of a
mile straightaway if such is necessary.
Fast Time By All Classes.
Besides harness and flat races there will
be hurdle races this season, and next fall
a steeplechase course will be added.
Yesterday the 2.25 class pacers stepped
in 2.23, the 2.24 class trotters worked in
2.234 and the 2.35 class (county horses)
in 2.28. In the one running race, an inau-
gural at one-half a mile, the distance was
run in 52 seconds, and had the winner had
occasion he could have won in 50 seconds.
That the races were hotly contested
was proved when it took six heats under
the new elimination rules to settle the 2.24
trot. The first event was for the 2.25
pacers. Nine started. The first heat was
the feature of the race. Elegant Boy, en-
tered from Baltimore, Happy May, from
Mount Vernon, and Helen, from Townsend,
Del., raced home in the lead. There were
not 24 inches between these three, who
finished in the order named.
While the second heat was faster, it
did not result in so grand a finish. Ele-
gant Boy was forced out in the final heat
In fast time.
The 2.24 trot had 17 entries and 11
scratched. It was a race of uncertainties
to the finish. The first three heats were
each won by different horses. One was
distanced, one was out under the rules,
but four were left to race three more heats
before Alexander I., owned and driven by
Gipsy Haight, of Savannah, Ga., landed
a winner. Miss Kushan, a Washington
mare, was first under the wire in the fin-
ish heat, but was set back one position
for running.
Race For Horses Of Four Counties.
The 2.35 pace was for houses owned in
the four counties-Prince George's, Mont-
gomery, Howard and Anne Arundel. It
was a better race than such specials usu-
ally result in. It was won by Cora Wig-
gens, from Bladensburg, in straight heats.
Tuscarora, a gelding entered from Sandy
Springs, was second in the first heat, but
as he belongs in Washington, he had to be
drawn after the heat.
The inaugural running dash, at half a
mile, was won handily by Pio Pico, a horse
which had previously won 13 races this
season. Despite this the books laid 3 to 1
against him.
The management decided to offer a $50
bonus to the horse making the fastest
mile, on a trot or pace, at this meeting.
