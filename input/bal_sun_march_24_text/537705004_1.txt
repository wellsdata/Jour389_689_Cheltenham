JUDGE MOYLAN
OPPOSES NEW
JUVENILE HOME
Says Only 11 Of 2,211
Delinquency Cases Went
To Court In 1944
Judge Charles E. Moylan, of the
Supreme Bench of Baltimore City.
said yesterday that he felt there
was no need for another institu.
tion for supervision of serious juve-
nile delinquents.
In a statement, he noted that
nineteen boys under 16 years of
agé had been sent to State penal
institutions during the year ended
last June 30.
- "From my experience in dealing
with more than 4,500 delinquent
boys and girls in the Circuit Court
of Baltimore City, Division for
Juvenile Causes, I definitely feel
that there is no need" for an addi-
tional institution for serious
cases, said Judge Moylan, who has
been assigned to the juvenile court
since September, 1943.
Handled 2,211 Cases in 1944
"The Division for Juvenile
Causes, as pointed out in its last
annual report, handled 2,211 de-
linquency cases in 1944-about half
of which involved burglaries, rob-
bery, larceny, or serious assaults-
and waived jurisdiction in only
eleven of them involving nine boys,
five white and four Negro boys.
"The dockets of the Criminal
Court of Baltimore City show that
only four of these nine boys were
sentenced to penal institutions; one
was placed on probation; one was
found not guilty, and three were
committed to training schools.
"During the first six months of
1945, the Juvenile Division, out of
approximately 1,300 cases of delin-
quent boys and girls under 16 years
of age, has waived jurisdiction in
eleven instances, all involving boys.
10 Under 16 "Sent Up"
- "Of these eleven boys the Crimi-
nal courts of Baltimore city sen-
tenced three to penal institutions,
committed one to the Spring Grove
State Hospital, and the remaining
seven were sent by the Criminal
Court to one of our training
schools.
"The Criminal Courts retained
jurisdiction over the ten boys it
had committed to the training
schools. Subsequently, three of the
ten were returned to Criminal
Court for a subsequent offense
committed at the school or while on
escape from the school, and were
sentenced to a penal institution.
"Accordingly, during 1944 and
the first six months of 1945, the
Criminal courts of Baltimore city
sentenced ten boys under 16 years
of age to a penal institution. Ob-
viously, Baltimore city needs no
additional institution for this hand-
ful of boys."
15 In State Sent To Prisons
Judge Moylan said Harold E.
Donnell, superintendent of prisons,
had told him that during the fiscal
year ending June 30, 1944, fifteen
boys under 16 had been sent to
penal institutions by all the courts

Case Of One Boy Cited
"Pending such amplification by
the State Department of Public
Welfare of our training-school pro-
grams and facilities, should the
Juvenile Division ever waive
jurisdiction in any instance? An
intelligent answer can be supplied
only by reference to concrete cases.
"A 15-year-old Negro boy. from
Harford county ran away from
Cheltenham School, came to Balti-
more, attacked women on our
streets and engaged in purse-
Isnatching, and was remanded by
the Juvenile Division to Chelten-
ham on June 12, 1945. After being
remanded, he again ran away from
the school and engaged in burglary
and pocketbook-snatching and was
again brought to the Juvenile
Division.
"The superintendent of Chelten-
ham advised the court that this
boy had run away on many previous
occasions and had been one of the
worst problem boys in his many
years of experience as administra-
tor in training schools in Virginia
and at Cheltenham, and was a
disrupting influence generally.
Despite this plea, I declined to
waive jurisdiction and remanded
him again to Cheltenham on July
3, 1945...
Attacked Sleeping Boy
"On July 15, 1945, this boy ma-
liciously attacked a sleeping boy
at the school with a wagon spoke.
The unprovoked criminal assault
required that the injured boy be
sent to Freedman Hospital in Wash-
ington, where he remained in a
semi-conscious condition for sev-
eral days.
Three days after the assault this
boy again ran away from Chelten-
ham, and a warrant has been signed
for his apprehension. He is now
thought to be again in Baltimore
and will probably be soon brought
to court on a new offense.
"Query? If this boy, although
only 15 years of age, is returned
to the Juvenile Division, should he
again be sent to Cheltenham and
afforded an opportunity to en-
danger the lives of other boys now
being rehabilitated at this school,
over half of whom are wards of this
court?, Should the court waive juris-
diction to the Criminal Court?
Judge Asks Question
"What should be done in such a
case until the training schools ad-
just their facilities to meet just
such immediate needs? Is it an an-
swer to declare that the judge can
force the school to accept the boy
if he will almost surely again run
away at will and be back on Balti-
more streets within a week, thereby
affording no opportunity for his
own rehabilitation and no protec
tion to the community?"
Meanwhile, James A Pratt, super-
intendent of the Maryland Training
School for Boys, yesterday objected
to Governor O'Conor's proposal
that the school's gymnasium be con-
verted into a dormitory for "chronic
runaways" and that the automotive
instruction shop now unused be-
cause a vocational instructor is not
available be made into a kitchen
and dining room..
Governor's Suggestion
The Governor's suggestion had
been put forward with a view
toward using the security unit at
the school exclusively for boys com-
mitted by the criminal courts and
"difficult repeaters" sent by the
juvenile courts.
Mr. Pratt protested that conver-
sion of these facilities would in-
terfere with the recreation pro-
gram for other boys and make im-
possible a later reopening of the
machine shop. He said the gymnasi-
um offered no security-sometimes
needed in dealing with "chonic
runaways"-and could serve at
best only as a stopgap.
