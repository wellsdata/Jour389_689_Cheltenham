Filth And Brutality
At Cheltenham
The latest report of filth and brutality at
the Cheltenham School for Colored Boys
follows a pattern that has been going on for
twenty years. Cheltenham has long been one
of those civic disgraces that everyone talks
about and no one does anything about. Now
Thomas B. Sprague, the current president of
the board, has ordered a thorough investiga.
tion of reports that the boys are being
whipped and abused. It is to be hoped that
he can do something to improve conditions
in that medieval rattrap that Maryland so
graciously calls a reform school for young
Nezroes.
Mr. Sprague has been told by the inmates
that whipping with a strap and a cat-o-nine-
tails is common practice at Cheltenham.
Mr. Sprague himself, on a surprise visit to
the institution, found a large leather lash
in a supervisor's drawer, Mr. Sprague also
found that three of the five cottages were
"inexcusably filthy." Worst of all, he found
& young boy locked and unattended in a wire
storage cage who was just recovering from
an operation and was still running a fever.
Such abuses Mr. Sprague should be able
to correct. There is no excuse for brutality
or filth. At the same time, the State should
not entertain the notion that the expenditure
of large sums of money will do much to
Improve the situation at Cheltenham. The
school is hopelessly outmoded, overcrowded
and useless. Nothing except a new school
will cure the basic things wrong with
Cheltenham. And the funds for a new school
were set aside by Governor O'Conor as a
part of Maryland's postwar construction pro-
gram. Governor Lane wiped out the funds
for a new Cheltenham, and it is now up to
Governor Lane to see to it that those funds
are restored.
