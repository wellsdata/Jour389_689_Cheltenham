SOCIAL HELP SEEN
IN FREUD'S THEORY
Psychiatrist Says Study Of
Conflict Of Tendencies
Will Aid Workers
Dr. Karl Menninger. Ad-
dresses Meeting Of State
Conference Of Welfare
Sigmund Freud's theory of the con
flict in every human being between
constructive and destructive tenden-
cies was extolled to 400 maryland so-
cial workers last night as the means
by which they might raise the stand-
ards of mental hygiene.
If you can think of your beings as
interactions of these two, then you can
work out a mental-hygiene program,"
Dr. Karl Menninger, author and head
of a psychiatric clinic in Topeka, Kan.,
told a meeting of the Maryland State
Conference of Social Welfare in the
Lord Baltimore Hotel.
:
Some Do, Some Don't
"Some master their destructive or
aggressive inhibitions," he continued.
"Some don't; and externally, they may
become criminals; or internally, they
may evidence it by suicide or drinking
themselves to death'."
Social workers, he said, could strive
to help those who have little mastery
over their inhibitions to sublimate
their destructive energy into construc-
tive goals. In this connection he men-
tioned President Roosevelt as one who
has applied "psychiatric principles to
sociological problems" as no Presi-
dent has ever done before. The Civilian
Conservation Corps he called "a won-
derful example" of this.
Games And Dances Can Help
"Play is another thing in which a
psychiatric approach can help mental
hygiene," Dr. Menninger declared.
"The furtherance of games, socials and
dances at institutions is a scientifically
justified part of mental hygiene. They
get rid of destructive energy and
build up constructive energy,"
People often complain that pris-
oners or those in mental instiutions
are being "coddled" when they find
such programs arranged, the psychia-
trist said. Such feelings he called
"foolish" and added that "it is about
the only kind of mental hygiene I
know much about."
Dr. Menninger was introduced by
Dr. George H. Preston, State Commis-
sioner of Mental Hygiene. Governor
O'Conor, delayed by another engage-
ment, appeared at the end of the
meeting and welcomed the social
workers as engaged in work of "im-
measurable benefit to the State."
Building At School:
Improvements in, personnel and
buildings at Cheltenham School for
Colored Boys are well under way, but
the "very bad problem" presented by
the mixture of feeble-minded and
normal inmates is yet to be worked
out, Dr. Thomas G. Pullen, Jr., said
ina luncheon address to the con-
ference.
"The fundamental problem," con-
tinued Dr. Pullen, who is assistant
State superintendent of education, "is
the wide range of intelligence among
the boys. Sixty per cent. are feable-
minded and only ten per cent. of su-
perior intelligence.
The position of supervisor of educa-
f'on at the school, created under
recommendations made by an in-
vestigating group last August, will
be filled shortly, Dr. Pullen said.
Program Of Training
Two cottages and a new auditorium-
gymnasium have been constructed
on the campus, the speaker added, and
a program of inservice training for the
school personnel nearly completed.
In an address at one of the confer-
ence's numerous seminars, Judge
Charles W. Hoffman, of the Court of
Domestic Relations, Cincinnati, Ohlo,
branded as "the merest lies" stories
that family life is disentegrating.
"The family has withstood the rigors
of modern civilization as well as any
institution we know and cherish," he
declared. "The best available figures
indicate that only one marriage in one
hundred and twenty-six is a failure."
Incapacity To Work
Among the afternoon seminars was
one discussing the problem of inca-
pacitation for work at which J.
Douglas Coleman, executive director
of the Associated Hospital Service of
Baltimore, presided.
There is no organized group, it was
pointed out, to determine the exact
status of a person's "incapability to
work."
