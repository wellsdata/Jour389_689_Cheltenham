NATIONAL LEAGUE
HITS "RIPPER BILL"
Civil Service Reform Group
Asks Governor O'Conor To
Kill Measure
Jones' Work Defended 'As
Best Merit System Has
Ever Had
An organization outside of Mary-
land yesterday aligned itself. with the
opposition to the bill that would legis-
late Harry C. Jones, State Employment
Commissioner, out of office.
The National Civil Service Reform
League urged Governor O'Conor in a
letter to disapprove House Bill 240,
the so-called "ripper bill."
The league described the three-man
board which the bill proposes to sub-
stitute for the employment commis-
sioner as a "dubious experiment" and
a threat to the Merit System.
Fears Much. Loss
E
Stressing the advantage of a paid
employment commissioner over
three-man board, the letter said: "To
abandon the present form of adminis-
tration seems to us a change liable to
result in much loss and no apparent
gain."
The letter, which was signed by
H. Eliot Kaplan, executive director
of the league, said States where there
is a single commissioner or director
have "won places in the scale of civil
service administration far higher than
most of the States with multi-mem-
bered civil service commissions."
Bill Passed By House
Attacking the setup provided by the
bill, which has been passed by the
House and has been reported favor-
ably in the Senate, the letter con-
tinued:
"A bipartisan board is all too fre-
quently exactly what the adjective
implies-representative of two politi-
cal organizations, and its deliberations
are apt to bow to political expediency.
"Multi-membered control,.
over, tends toward delay, uncertainty
of action and the spreading of re-
sponsibility which ought to be defi-
nite."

Mr. Kaplan will be the speaker at

Uphold Jones' Work
Local officers of the Civil Service Re-
form League from time to time have
upheld Mr. Jones, saying that he "is
giving the Merit System the best ad-
ministration it ever had in Maryland."
At the very time the "ripper bill"
was introduced, it was pointed out
yesterday, legislation prepared under
Mr. Jones' direction to standarize the
salaries of thousands of Merit System
employes was being introduced in both
houses of the Legislature.
The bills represented an aim of the
Unemployment Commissioner since
1935.
Walter H. Buck, a pioneer in civil
service work, charged that the bill
was aimed solely at Mr. Jones, and its
purpose was to "get rid of" him "in
the middle of his term when he is
doing a good job.",
Mr. Jones' attempts to raise the
standards of employes at Cheltenham
School for Boys, defeated by a legal
decision, have been cited by several
of the organizations that are protesting
the move to legislate him out of office.
The commissioner ordered the board
at the school to dismiss twenty-six
members of the staff as "physically and
otherwise unfit" for duty. The board
resorted to the courts and it was ruled
- the staff at Cheltenham had been
blanketed into the Merit System
when the school came under State
cirection.
Other Efforts Mentioned
Further mention was made of the
commissioner's successful efforts last
fall to prevent Merit System employes
from engaging in political activity.
Though the "ripper bill" was intro-
duced by Delegate John S. White,
majority floor leader in the House,
Governor O'Conor has said it is not
an administration measure, and has
declared a hands-off attitude.
Repeatedly on the floors of both
houses the charge has been made that
the bill is nothing but a "political
maneuver" to shoot holes through the
Merit System and open patronage
fields to Democrats.
