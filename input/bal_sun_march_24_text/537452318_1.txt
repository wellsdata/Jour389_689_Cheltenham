Angel Topples
The angel-topped Centennial
Fountain, between McMechen and
Wilson streets, in Eutaw place,
which several generations of Balti-
moreans have liked to stroll
around, crashed irreparably as the
result of a one-sided burden of ice
and snow.
The 50-foot granite structure,
which was first put into use May
19, 1877. had three basins, the outer
one 48 feet in diameter. Standing
in the top shell was a female figure,
apparently an angel, and four
smaller figures decorated the base.
Curving walks and benches sur-
rounded the fountain, making the
spot a favorite one for Eutaw place
residents.

Major Projects Planned
Four major projects-planned at
a cost of approximately $3,500,000
-head a list of postwar improve-
ments announced by G. H. Pouder,
executive vice president of the
Association of Commerce, and Col.
Henry G. Perring, harbor engineer.
The projects are expected to better
the port facilities so that Balti-
more's annual water-borne com-
merce, which has aggregated as
much as $1,000,000,000 in one year,
will thrive and the port's position
as second in the nation for foreign.
trade tonnage be sustained.
The projects consist of improving
the main ship channel from the
Chesapeake Bay to Fort McHenry,
making this channel 39 feet deep
at mean low water; enlarging
Anchorage No. 3, better known as
Riverview Anchorage, by extending
it 2,400 feet in length for a depth
of 30 feet the present anchorage
is 1,500 feet wide, 4,500 feet long
and 35 feet deep; constructing
a new deep-water channel from the
Brewerton Channel below Spar-
rows Point to the upper Chesa-
peake Bay, in order to shorten by
12 nautical miles the distance be-
tween Baltimore and the Chesa-
peake and Delaware Canal and re-
duce vessel steaming time and ex-
penses and building a new Curtis
Creek Channel, to serve the grow-
ing industries of that section.
