IMPROVEMENT
PLAN URGED AT
CHELTENHAM
O'Conor, 2 Special Com-
mittees And School Board
Back Program
Appointment Of Quali-
fied Educational Director
Among Proposals
A far-reaching program to improve
conditions at the Cheltenham School
for Negroes was unanimously recom-:
mended yesterday at a conference
among Governor O'Conor, two spe-
cial committees and members of the
school's board.

The board of managers of the re-
form school was requested to "lake
initial steps to put into force" the
recommendations which are based on
studies made of the institution by a
committee of the State Department of
Education and by a committee ap-
pointed recently by the Governor.
Board To Get Report Thursday
Yesterday's recommendations and a
complete report on the school, com-
piled by the education committee
after a year's study, will be placed
before the board of managers for
action at its meeting Thursday, the
Governor announced.
Studies of Cheltenham were under-
taken after repeated reports of bru-
tality, starvation and mismanagement
emanated from the school. The recom-
mendations made at the conference
follow:
1
Appointment of a qualified educa-
tional director.
Appointment of an advisory com-
mittee on education, consisting of
at least five members.
Establishment of a program of in-
service for present employes.
Expansion of the testing program to
determine the abilities of present
trainees and those, entering the
school.
Regrading of the school and pro-
vision for an opportunity class.
Coordination of practice and related
information in practical arts train-
ing.
Installation of a graded system of
credits in habit training.
Installation of an adequate system
of physical examination and rec-
ords thereof.
Establishment of a plan of intake
control based on the objectives
which may properly be assigned
to a training school.
Construction of a building to serve
as a gymnasium, school building,
assembly hall and industrial build-
ing.
Establishment of an optional recre-
ational program and purchase of
the necessary equipment for its
operation.
Governor O'Conor, who released a
statement on the program, declined to
enlarge on it or make any further
comment on the institution. However,
he said the program, if adopted, would
modernize the handling of the reform
school.
Governor's Statement
The Governor's statement follows:
"Governor O'Conor met today with
six members of the board of managers
of the Cheltenham School for Boys,
a committee of the State Department
of Education, appointed in March,
1938, at the request of the board of
managers of Cheltenham, to study the
educational situation at the institu-
tion, and the committee appointed re-
cently by him at the request of the
president of the board of managers
of the Cheltenham School.
"The board of managers of the
Cheltenham School for Boys in March,
-1938, asked the State Department of
Education to study conditions at the
school and to make recommendations
in respect to the educational program.
"In May, 1939, the president of the
board of managers of the Cheltenham
School had an interview with the
Governor and requested him to ap-
point a committee to study the con-
ditions at Cheltenham and to report
its findings to the Governor.
Educational Director Urged
"The committee of the State Depart-
S ment of Education has finished its
study of the institution. The commit-

tee appointed by the Governor had
access to this report and had con-
ferences with those who drew it.
"The meeting today was an amicable
one and everyone present was actuated
by the desire to do whatever is best
for the school.
"Cheltenham is the institution to
which colored boys who are homeless,
who have no proper care and who
have other problems, are sent. The
State Department of Education has
recommended that an educational di-
rector be appointed immediately to
take charge of all matters connected
with the training and education of
the boys and has outlined a long-term
program of general improvement."
Secret Committee
Governor O'Conor declined to re-
veal the identities of the three mem-
bers of his special committee, which
he named early in June. He did say,
however, that their work was now
completed.
Unfavorable reports have emanated
from Cheltenham ever since it was
taken over by the State in 1937.
Last year former Governor Nice
asked the board of managers of the
school to adopt a plan of reorganiza-
tion advanced by Harry C., Jones,
State Employment Commissioner, pre-
sent a satisfactory alternative plan
or resign. The board then informed
him. it would not resign or comply
with his demands.
The school's board consists of fifteen
members. There will be five vacancies
on the board this summer for Gov-
ernor O'Conor to fill.
