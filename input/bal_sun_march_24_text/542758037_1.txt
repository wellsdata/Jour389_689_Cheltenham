CHERRY HILL
RECREATION
CUT ATTACKED
Moylan Joins In Asking
Board To Restore Funds
In New Budget

As one of the speakers who
yesterday criticized the Board of
Estimates for cutting recreation
budget requests, Judge Charles E.
Moylan said that one boy sent to
Cheltenham, possibly because of a
lack of recreation facilities, costs
the taxpayers $400 more in a year
than the cost to parents of sending
a boy to the University of Mary-
land.
Discussing generally the board's
reduction of a $96,000 request for
an expanded playground program,
and particularly the deletion of
$5.000 asked for staffing a com-
munity recreation center at the
Cherry Hill Negro housing devel-
opment, Judge Moylan told the
board:
"One of those youngsters from
Cherry Hill will come before the
court tomorrow, or the next day, or
next week, and he will be sent to
Cheltenham. That will cost the tax-
payers more in one year than it
would cost you to educate your boy
or girl at the University of Mary.
land."
Cost About $1,300 A Year
Questioned later, he said he is
advised that the annual cost for a
boy at Cheltenham-State training
school for Negro boys-is between
$1,300 and $1,400 a year,
Head of the Division of Juvenile
Causes, before which such boys are
tried. Judge Moylan was before the
Board of Estimates yesterday as
chairman of the Baltimore Youth
Commission.
He was preceded by several other
speakers, including Otho Pinkett,
project manager at the Cherry Hill
housing project, who surprised
some board members by reporting
that the comparatively new, Gov-
ernment-built Cherry Hill school
already is overcrowded and that
next year at least eight classes will
be on part time.
Questioned By Kelly
He cited the overcrowded condi-
tion of the school as another reason
why the Negro children of the area
will need more in the way of rec-
reation facilities.
"Do you mean to tell us," asked
C. Markland Kelly, president of the
board, "that that new school is al-
ready overcrowded?"
"It has been overcrowded since
the day it was opened," Mr. Pinkett
replied,
"How could that have hap-
pened?" asked Mr. Kelly.
"It appears," he was told, "that
proper survey was not made before
it was built. It was built only for
the housing project but must serve
the entire Cherry Hill area."
Can't Place The Blame
"You mean," Mr. Kelly pressed.
"that the Federal Government
slipped up in planning the school?"
"I'm not in a position to place
the blame," replied the project
manager.
Although built by the Federal
authorities, the school is operated
by the city.
Clarence W. Burrier, director of
project services for the Housing
Authority of Baltimore, said
grounds have been laid out for a
playground, and are awaiting the
equipment to be delivered by the
Federal Public Housing Admini-
stration.
"But with no supervisors," he
said, "the equipment will be only
a problem."

tary of the Baltimore Urban
League, acting as chairman for the
group protesting the cuts, pointed

Dropped From Budget

city now is covered by two such
supervisors.

Cherry Hill development now has

"We are not completing the job
at Cherry Hill," he said, "if we
don't provide it with recreation
facilities."
