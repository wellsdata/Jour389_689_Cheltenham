CURRAN RULES
Reformitories Must Re-
ceive Youths Under
16, He Says
William Curran, Attorney Gen-
eral, said last night he saw "no
reason why any male offender
under the age of 16 should be sen-
tenced to the House of Correction
or to any other penal institution."
Declaring that the State Board of
Public Welfare has complete con-
trol over policies of admission to
the four State training schools, he
said that, if the board were willing,
no legal obstacle would stand in the
way of an institution's accepting a
youthful offender committed to it.
If contrary institutional regula-
tions exist, he said, the law calls
for alteration of those rules.

Cites Training School
Explaining that he was convinced
the reformatories were ir a situa-
tion which might call for some over-
crowding, he said the same situa-
tion existed in other institutions
and in hospitals, hotels and homes,
and that the question would have
to be worked out accordingly.
He pointed out that a minimum
security unit for serious offenders
with a capacity of 40 is in opera-
tion with 44 inmates at the Mary-
land State Training School for
Boys.
He said additional space could
be provided at the school, inmates
could be rotated from that unit to
other departments and similar
units could be established at the
Cheltenham School for Boys-all
under existing authority of the
welfare board.

Mr. Curran's statement follows:
"Mr. J. Milton Patterson, di-
rector of the State Board of Public
Welfare, has asked the attorney
general's office to advise whether
the recently enacted Juvenile
Court bill calls for a change in
the department's rules adopted for
training schools.

Request From O'Conor
"This office has also been re-
quested by Governor O'Conor to
determine what institutions are,
under the present laws, obligated
to take care of the more difficult
types of juvenile offender.
"Our laws over the years and
particularly in recent years have
become more progressively en-
lightened as they concern the juve-
nile offender.
"The State Board of Public Wel-
fare now has complete control over
the policy of admission to the four
training schools: the Maryland
Training School for Boys, the
Montrose School for Girls, the
Cheltenham School for Boys and
the Maryland Training School for
Colored Girls,
"The sentencing judge has now
an almost complete freedom of
choice in sentencing a juvenile
offender.

Intermediate Place Cited
"Mr. Hall Hammond, the deputy
attorney general, is advising Mr.
Patterson that the right given to
the court to sentence a juvenile
offender to any public institution
imposes a correlative right on the
public institution to receive the
child when sentenced to it.
"If any of the rules or policies of
admission of these institutions be
to the contrary, the law as it has
been developed calls for a change
in the rules of the institution.
= "Recently there has been much
discussion as to the need for an
intermediate institution to handle
juvenile delinquents. In 1944 the
so-called minimum security unit
was established at the Maryland
Training School to house youthful
offc.ders convicted of serious
crimes.

Now In Operation
"That unit is now in operation
with 44 inmates, 35 of whom are
confirmed runaways. The remaining
nine were committed by the Crimi-
nal Court of Baltimore city. The
unit's capacity is 40 boys.
"Within the powers entrusted to
the board of welfare by the Leg-
islature, additional space could be
provided in co-operation with the
management of the Training
School or some system of rotation
worked out whereby certain in-
mates of the security unit could
be sent back to other departments
of the school.
"Similar units could be estab-
lished at Cheltenham. There would
seem to be no reason why any male
offender under the age of 16
should be sentenced to the House
of Correction or to any other penal
institution.".
