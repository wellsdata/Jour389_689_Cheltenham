Again, Not A Word
About Cheltenham
Cheltenham, the State's school for de-
linquent Negro boys, has long struggled
under a double burden-that of caring
for defective Negro boys and that of
caring for delinquents capable of reha-
bilitation. It is generally agreed that
these two functions should be taken care
of by separate institutions.
Back in January of 1943 the Maryland
Commission on Juvenile Delinquency
recommended just such a separation. It
recommended that an entirely new insti-
tution be built for trainable Negro delin-
quents and that the present Chelten-
ham, after suitable alterations and im-
provements, be turned over to the State
Board of Mental Hygiene as a school for
defective Negro boys. The General As-
sembly approved this recommendation.
Yesterday Governor O'Conor an-
nounced the State's post-war program
for medical-care institutions, including
mental patients.
The Governor's list of projects in-
cluded badly needed work at Spring
Grove, an expansion program at Spring-
field State Hospital and programs of
expansion at Crownsville State Hospital,
Eastern Shore State Hospital and a
number. of other institutions.
Nowhere in the list is any reference
to the new rôle of the present Chelten-
ham. Cheltenham is absent from the list
despite the fact that the post-war pro-
gram for Cheltenham is supposed to be
settled public policy.
Why is it that the program for Chel-
tenham is so consistently disregarded?
Only last week the Governor's imposing
Commission on Post-War Reconstruction
and Development published a list of
post-war projects which made no refer-
ence to the new Cheltenham program.
Now Cheltenham has been skipped
again. The Governor, or the State Board
of Mental Hygiene, or the Commission
on Post-War Reconstruction and Devel-
opment, owes the public an explanation
of this.
