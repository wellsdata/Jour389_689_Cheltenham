Butcher was convicted for failure

Marion, N. C. The jury deliberated
less than five minutes before re-

Wilfred T. McQuaid, defense
counsel, immediately filed an ap-
peal, but his request for the release
of Butcher on bail pending a de-
cision on the appeal was refused
by Judge Coleman.
Called Test Case

Butcher had been working as a
counselor at the Cheltenham
School for Boys when he received
the order to report at Marion.
"You are an agitator." Judge
Coleman told Butcher before im-
posing sentence. "You were trying
to tie up racial prejudices with the
question of military service and re-
ligious beliefs. When they tried
o remove you from the place where
you caused trouble, you complained
that you couldn't be removed."
The testimony disclosed that
Butcher had been assigned to two
other camps before he reached
Cheltenham, but the defendant con-
tended that it wasn't until recently
that he learned the camps were
being directed' and controlled by
military personnel.
Signed By Hershey
In support of his contention, he
pointed out that his orders to pro-
ceed to another camp were signed
either by Maj. Gen. Lewis B. Her-
shey, Director of Selective Service,
or by a military attache of the
camp operations division of selec-
tive service.
Mr. McQuaid argued to the jury
that the draft law specifically states
that objectors shall be assigned to
work of national importance under
civilian direction.
"The court considers your case a
very extreme one," Judge Coleman
asserted in remarks made before
the imposition of sentence. "The
court feels that the verdict of the
jury was fully justified by the evi-
dence. You have had a fair, im-
partial trial.
