The Cheltenham Plan
Is Still A Valid One
in the miast of so muen new planning for
the expansion of Maryland's institutional
care, one old plan should not be overlooked.
The old (1943) plan concerns the building of
a new Cheltenham School for Boys at a site
closer to Baltimore and the use of the exist.
ing institution for mental cases,
For years it has been known that the
present Cheltenham school is in the wrong
location to attract a competent staff and to
provide the sort of training calculated to re-
store young delinquents as useful members
of society. The situation came to a head in
1943, following a particularly critical report
by the Maryland Commission on Juvenile De-
linquency, and resulted in a definite postwar
construction program to (1) build a com
pletely new institution for normal delin
quent Negro boys closer to Baltimore, and
(2) utilize the present Cheltenham for the
custody and training of defective Negro hoys.
The double-purpose Cheltenham plan was
a settled State policy, approved by the Legis.
lature. But when Governor Lane came into
office the plan was quietly dropped and the
lunds earmarked for the new school were
used for general budget purposes. Since
then there has been the usual run of reports
on shocking conditions at Cheltenham, in-
cluding one by the Governor himself, but
nothing has been done to implement the
established plan for the institution.
Probably the Cheltenham plan now needs,
revision to fit the use of the existing institu-
tion into the larger plans to reorganize the
mental institutions. But the great virtue of
the original Cheltenham plan still remains:
Maryland could get two badly needed in
stitutions for the price of one new one.
