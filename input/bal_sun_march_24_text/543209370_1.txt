Support of proposed legislation mak-
ang illegitimate children eligible for
State aid, and the marriage bill, re-
quiring a lapse of three days between
application and issuance of a license,
has been urged in resolutions adopted
by the executive committee of the
Baltimore Round Table of the Na-
tional Conference of Jews and Chris-
tians.
The resolutions, which have been
made public, are signed by the Rev.
Dr. Thomas Guthrie Speers, pastor of
the Brown Memorial Presbyterian
Church: the Rev. Dr. Edward L. Israel,
rabbi of the Har Sinai Congregation;
Vincent de Paul Fitzpatrick, editor of
the Baltimore Catholic Review, and
the Rev. A. W. Gottschall, secretary
of the Baltimore Round Table.
Social Legislation Studied
The preface to the resolution says:
"The Baltimore Round Table of the
National Conference of Jews and
Christians is deeply interested in
everything that concerns the reli-
gious and moral welfare of the citi.
zens of our State. We take no part in
partisan politics. But we have a very
deep common interest in social con-
ditions in our State because of the
effect of those conditions upon the
living standards, and the moral and
spiritual life of our fellow-citizens.
For this reason the executive commit-
tee of the Baltimore Round Table has
made a study of the social legislation
to be submitted to the present session
of the General Assembly.
"After a careful scrutiny of the va-
rious bills we find that while as indi-
viduals or as separate religious groups,
there are differences in our attitudes
toward certain of them, we can unite
whole-heartedly in recommending that
the members of the Baltimore Round
Table of Jews and Christians should
actively support the following":
1. The marriage bill previously men-
tioned.
2. Unemployment insurance, a bill
to expand present legislation to
cover all forms of business that
☐ employ four or more persons. We
agree in principle with this bill,
but state emphatically that we look
forward to the time when there
will be a complete coverage for
every employed person.
3. The status of illegitimate children.
For instance, making them eligible
to receive State aid.
4. State program for delinquent col-
ored boys, whereby the State ulti-
mately takes over and expands
Cheltenham.
5. Legislation for prison labor. State-
use program.
6. More funds for Board of Mental
Hygiene,
7. More funds for State tuberculosis
program.
8. More funds for State penal pro-
gram.
2. An appropriation of sufficient.
funds to finance the public-assist-
ance program.
10. In addition we suggest that, in
view of the damage to health and
morals caused by slums, the As-
sembly pass an enabling act which
would make it possible for the
State to take advantage of the
terms of a Federal housing law if
it should be passed by Congress.
"In our opinion these are the more
pressing needs of our State at the pres-
ent time. And we unite in urging the
passing of such legislation at this ses-
sion of the Assembly. Realizing the
financial stringency of the budget wé
believe it is the duty of the Assembly
to find equable and morally reputable
forms of taxation to make possible the
carrying out of such social legislation.'
