HOSE FLOGGING
OF BOY INMATES
NOT 'UNUSUAL!
Cheltenham Chief, How-
ever, Denies Youth Was
"Beaten Badly"
Answers Jones' Charges,
Says "None Of State-
ments Are True"
B. B. Sunderland, superintendent of
Cheltenham Reform School for Colored
Boys, yesterday admitted it was "noth-
ing unusual" for boys committed to
the institution to be beaten with a
rubber hose.
Mr. Sunderland was asked for com-
ment on charges made by Harry C.
Jones, State Employment Commis-
sioner, that 14-year-old Walter
Thomas, an inmate, had been beaten
severely by Andrew Beckwith, a cot-
tage master, with a cell padlock.
First Denies Jones' Charges,
Mr. Jones, in addition, charged that
John Beckwith, a brother of the cot-
tage superintendent, had "flogged"
Thomas with a piece of garden hose
two and a half feet long and an inch
in diameter before the boy had been
released from confinement at Chel-
tenham. Mr. Jones further alleged that
John Beckwith had admitted the flog-
ging.
Answering Mr. Jones' charges, Mr.
Sunderland first said that "none of the
statements are true."
"There is nothing wrong with the
boy," he said. "The mother had a
visit with him this morning and she
was perfectly satisfied. We don't beat
down here."
As To Beating With Hose
Then he was asked:
"But what about the charge that
Thomas was beaten with a rubber
hose by John Beckwith?"
"There is no question about not us-
ing a hose," Mr. Sunderland replied.
"You mean to say that he was not
beaten with a hose?" he was asked.
"No," Mr. Sunderland replied. "I
didn't say that at all. I didn't say that
at all."
"Then he was beaten with a hose?"
"Nothing Unusual," He Saya
"That's nothing unusual. Don't for-
get we get what you Baltimore city
fellows are done with. The main ques-
tion is to be fair to those boys."
Questioned further about the Thomas
boy, Mr. Sunderland said he was "not
beaten badly" and repeated that "noth-
ing was the matter with him."
He said that Andrew Beckwith, the
man charged with beating the Thomas
boy with a cell padlock, and who was
suspended from his duties after a per-
sonal investigation conducted by Mr.
Jones, had "taken a vacation for a day
or two."
"He needed it," Mr. Sunderland said.
"He's a hard worker."
Mother Appealed To Governor
The Thomas boy is the son of a
domestic employed in Baltimore. The
mother appealed some days ago to
Governor Nice to find out whether
her son was "dead or alive."
The Governor referred the matter
to Mr. Jones.
Mr. Jones said the officials at Chel-
tenham, with whom he has been hav-
ing a dispute about the capabilities

Then Mr. Jones charged that the
Thomas boy first had been put in a

Declared Ordered Locked Up
"On or about Tuesday, July 26, the

"The boy was taken by the assistant
superintendent, Mr. Carroll, to cottage
No. 1, over which a cottage master

in the absence of Beckwith, 'locked'
this boy in a barred cell.
The Padlock Incident
"On the next day, Thursday, July
28, another boy named Elwood Waters
was locked in the same cell. It ap-
pears that on Thursday evening the
two boys got to playing with the bed
in the cell and Andrew Beckwith,
cottage master, who in the meantime
had returned to duty, is alleged to
have entered the cell with the cell
padlock in his hand.
"He appears to have gone after both
