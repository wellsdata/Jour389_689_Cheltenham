LETTERS
to the
EDITOR
The Problem Of Cheltenham
■ TO THE EDITOR OF THE SUN-Sir: I have
just read in The Sun the excellent edi-
torial on conditions at Cheltenham. I
heartily agree with the suggestion that
the Governor should arrange for a thor-
ough investigation of the conduct of this
reform school for colored boys, and if
the court should decide that as the law
now stands there cannot be the proper
improvement, then the Governor should
prepare the way for changing the law
in the next session of the State Legisla-
ture.
In the letter from the Rev. George T.
Bragg, Jr., in this same issue, voters are
urged to support in the coming election
candidates who will stand squarely for
justice in this matter. This appeal should
be taken to heart.
Besides the obligation of public officials
to look after the welfare of our State
institutions, the main responsibility lies
with the private citizens. If they were
more interested and more coöperative,
we might have more reason to be proud
of our institutions.
As a member of the Interracial Com-
mittee of the Baltimore Branch of the
Women's International League for Peace
and Freedom and of the Committee on
Juvenile Delinquency of the Baltimore
Urban League, I have been gathering
information about the reform school for
colored boys at Cheltenham and the in-
dustrial school for colored girls at Glen
Burnie.
In visiting these places I have found
that the staff and the management of
the school at Glen Burnie is far su-
perior to that at Cheltenham. This dif-
ference is due in large measure, I be-
lieve, to the personal attention of an
efficient board and to the unselfish co-
operation of a large number of Negro
and white women.
When the public becomes as concerned
about Cheltenham as many have been
about the Glen Burnie establishment, it
will be possible to have changes there
that will tend toward the turning of
delinquent Negro boys into good citi-
zens.
Baltimore, Aug. 9.'
ESTHER J. CROOKS.
