State Commission Also
Asks All-Negro Board
For Cheltenham
Repeal of the "Jim Crow" law.
appointment of an all-Negro board
of managers for the Cheltenham
School for Boys and a change in
its own name were recommended
yesterday to Governor Lane.
The recommendations were made
by the Commission to Study Prob-
lems Affecting the Colored Popu-
lation.
The second of the proposals al-
ready is on the way toward being
placed in effect. Members of the
Cheltenham School's present board
recently submitted their resigna-
tions, recommending to Governor
Lane as they did so, the appoint-
ment of an all-Negro board.
The Governor is understood to
be preparing now for appointment
of such a board.
Makes Other Suggestions
The three recommendations.
were contained in letter of
transmittal of the commission's
first public report since March,
1943. The commission, first ap-
pointed by Governor O'Conor and
later established permanently by
the. Legislature, has been in exist-
ence since 1942. Joseph P. Healy is
chairman,
Besides the specific recommenda-
tions, the commission also pointed
up needs and made other sugges-
tions in its review of employment,
housing, health and welfare, law
enforcement, transportation, educa-
tion and other fields where prob-
lems were found to exist for the
Negro population.
The report itself does not discuss
the "Jim Crow" law or set forth the
commission's reasons for proposing
a change from the mixed board now
managing the training school for
Negro boys at Cheltenham.
New Title Suggested
The commission's third recom.

title-to "Commission on Racial

In its letter of transmittal, the
commission said the report showed
the State was making "progress in
the intelligent, sympathetic and
thorough understanding" of mi
nority problems, but noted that
"the complete solution of the prob-
lem will await the distant future."
"We have sought to analyze and
determine in so far as possible the
social and economic factors which
are the cause and basis of these
problems, and to suggest means of
solution
deemed practicable
within the framework of the tra-
ditions and policies of the State,"
the commission stated.
Evidences Of Improvement
The report is divided into sec-
tions, some merely setting forth the
problems found, others showing
progress or lack of advancement
and still others containing sugges-
tions for future action.
In its study of employment, the
commission found three main evi-
dences of improvement: An in-
crease in the absolute number of
Negro employés; employment by
industries and companies which
never before used Negro workers,
and promotions within industries!
and companies which formerly em-)!
ployed Negroes only in limited
capacities.
It found also, however, that the
Negro since the war had lost some
of his gains in industrial and agri-
cultural employment made in the
war period.
2 Licensed Electricians
Specific complaint was made that
properly trained Negro applicants
for plumbers licenses have met an
"insurmountable barrier" in Balti-
more city and that admission to the
electrical vocation presents the
same obstacles.
There are in the State, according
to the report, only two licensed
Negro electricians, one at Cam-
bridge and one in Baltimore city,
and only one licensed "plumber
and steam-itter," at Pocomoke
city.
As to housing, the commission
was less optimistic. It found resist-
ance against movement of Negroes
to new areas to establish homes
"as strong as ever" and com-
mented:
"There still seems to have been
but little progress made with this
toughest of Negroes' problems."
Overcrowding Deplored
Deploring overcrowding, it rec-
ommended that existing facilities
"at least be brought up to a mini-
mum standard by strict enforce-
ment of Health Department regu-
lations" and said "undeveloped
jareas in and adjacent to the city
of Baltimore must be made avail-
able for Negro housing to take care
of the present overcrowded situa-
tion and for future development."
"The three present inspectors of
the Health Department cannot
possibly enforce the rooming house,
housing and trailer camp rules,"
the commission said.

"The gross death rate among
Negroes emphasizes the social
consequence of discriminatory

Training For Doctors
It found need for adequate facili-
ties for training of Negro doctors,
training opportunities for Negro
nurses, sufficient beds for the
mentally ill at Crownsville and
relief from the "overcrowded"
condition at the Henryton Tuber-
culosis Sanitarium.
In 1946 and 1947, it noted, "the
death rate for tuberculosis of the
colored race was nearly four
times that of the white population,
the rate for pneumonia over twice
that for the white; the rate for
syphilis nearly ten times that for
the white."
The commission found the "im-
proved relationship existing be-
tween the police and the large sec-
tions of the Negro community,"
particularly in Baltimore city, one
of the "most tangible indications of
interracial progress in the State."
Police Co-Operation
"The Police Department under
Commissioner Atkinson has been
unusually co-operative in inves
tigating alleged abuses of police
authority and general complaints
of brutality and discourtesy," it
said.
"More Negroes have been inte-
grated into the department in the
city and in four of the county seats
throughout the State. Negro offi
cers are beginning to be rated on
the basis of merit and ability and
promotions made accordingly."

Newspapers Approached
Newspapers throughout the State
have been approached in an at-
tempt to "change attitudes of news
reporting, race labeling, etc.," the
commission continued.
It noted "general complaints
among Negroes that some news-
papers, in reporting crime, un-
necessarily describe the defendant
as a Negro" and said, "Negro lead-
ers are inclined to think and feel
the use of 'Negro' or 'colored' in
such cases is seldom necessary."
Discussing transportation, the
commission said the "numerous"
complaints about taxicab facilities
avialable to the Negro population
were "justified and well-founded,"
but added that discriminatory prac-
tices by drivers had been "some-
what alleviated."
It reviewed educational pro-
grams for Negroes in the State,
going into the vocational training
picture in Baltimore at some
length.
