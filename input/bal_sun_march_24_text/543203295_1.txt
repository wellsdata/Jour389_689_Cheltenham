JONES ORDERS
26 OUSTED AT
CHELTENHAM
Declares Nearly Half Of
Staff Unfit For Pres-
ent Duties
Bars Them From Merit
System Unless Over-
ruled By Court
Harry C. Jones, State Employment
Commissioner, yesterday ordered the
board of the Cheltenham School for
Boys to dismiss twenty-six of the fifty-
seven members of its staff.
Most of these twenty-six are "physi-
cally or otherwise unfit" for any duty
in the State service, and all are unfit
for their present duties, Mr. Jones
said.
He served a point-blank ultimatum
on the board that he would not accept
these employes in the State merit
system unless he is forced to do so by.
a court order.
Refuses To Approve Pay Rolls
He added that unless the board
promises to cooperate with him in a
wholesale reorganization of the per-
sonnel, he will not approve any pay
roll for the institution subsequent to
that of March 31.
As an essential part of this reorgani-
zation, he insisted that the board of
managers immediately terminate the
employment of four of its own mem-
bers on the institution's staff, that it
obtain an experienced executive for
the post of superintendent, and that
it cooperate in obtaining qualified per-
sons for several other key positions..
These recommendations were con-
tained in a report which the State
Employment Commissioner delivered
to board members and which will be
considered by them at a meeting to
be held at Cheltenham this afternoon.
Follows 3-Month Inquiry
This report was the result of a
three-month investigation of the in-
stitution's personnel which Mr. Jones
conducted for the purpose of classify-
ing employes preparatory to their in-
corporation into the State merit
system.
Investigation of the fifty-seven em-
ployes, case by case, convinced the
State Employment Commissioner, he
said, that he could not "assume the
responsibility" of "blanketing" them
into the classified service.
The 1937 General Assembly, in the
act which extended State control over
the 67-year-old Negro reformatory,
provided that the present personnel
be accepted into the State service
"without need of special examination
as to fitness."
:
Interprets Language Of Act
Mr. Jones told the board of man-
agers that he construed this wording
to "apply only to employes who are
physically and otherwise fit to per-
form the duties to which they are
assigned."
He added that he did not believe
the General Assembly intended that
the reorganization of Cheltenham as
a State institution should be delayed
"until after the death of practically
one-half of the present employes."
These employes, he said, the "em-
ployment commissioner has found to
be physically, mentally and otherwise
unfit for present duties, and, in all but
two or three cases, unfit for any duty.
in the State service.".
Finds 22 Classified
Mr. Jones found twenty-two of the
institution's employes qualified for
classification in the State service and
ordered nine others "not classified":
pending efforts to find work they!!
could do. The others, he said, should
be dismissed.
In a letter to Joseph H. Blandford,
president of the board, he explained
that all except the twenty-two em-
ployes to whom classifications were
granted should become temporary.em-
ployes, and either must be trans-
ferred to duties they can perform or
must be released as rapidly as com-
petent replacements can be made.
In his report, Mr. Jones placed par-
ticular cimphasis upon the division of
responsibility in the management of
the reform school which resulted from
the superintendent having as salaried
employes several members of his em-
ploying board.
Board Members Employed
Those board members serving as
employes, he said, are:
MR. BLANDFORD.
DR. WILLIAM H. GIBBONS.
REV. FREDERICK DOUGLAS.
JOHN E. McCORMACK..
"As board members, these employes
are able to vote on their own com-
pensation and duties, and are respon-
sible to the board only for the manner
in which their duties are performed,"
the employment commissioner said.
"The superintendent . naturally
is not in a position to issue orders to
the members of his own board, who
are in effect his employers, and, as he
thus lacks any disciplinary or admin-
istrative authority over thei: actions,
it is impossible for him to maintain
the standards necessary for the proper
administrative conduct of the institu-
tion."

present acting superintendent of Chel-

to a subordinate position and that a
new superintendent "equipped with
the necessary educational and techni-:

Instead of having a member of the
board serve as executive secretary, he

be employed to serve under the super-

A drastic revision of personnel in
the supervision of inmates will be
necessary, Mr. Jones said, finding thir-
teen employes out of seventeen in this
department incompetent. The depart-
ment head, John S. Beckwith, he
found "competent and satisfactory."
Hits Education Facilities
The employment commissioner said
that Cheltenham was "totally unpre-
pared" for its proper educational
functions. There is no vocational
instruction, he said, except in shoe
cobbling.
He recommended that the State
Board of Education be asked to survey
the situation and draw up plans for a
proper course of academic and voca-
tional studies.
Medical duties, Mr. Jones said, at
present are exercised by a member of
the board who lives some distance from
the institution. He recomanended that
an institutional physician be employed
who would give half his time daily to
the school and would be on call at
other times.
The revision of the parole depart-
ment, now headed by an aged Negro
minister, also a member of the board,
is badly needed, Mr. Jones found. He
recommended that the parole work be
centralized at the institution under the
supervision of the superintendent, and
that the Baltimore parole office be
abolished.
Demands Licensed Engineer
Cheltenham's heating plant, now
operated by unlicensed engineers,
must be put in charge of at least a
licensed shift engineer and a licensed
steam fireman, he recommended, in
order that there may be a licensed
engineer on duty at all times.
Mr. Jones said the board has an im-
portant service to perform in the in-
duction of Cheltenham into the ranks
of accredited State institutions, and
that "it will be most unfortunate and
a great disappointment to the critics
of Cheltenham as a private institution
if any minor consideration, such as
matters connected with unfit person-
nel, should delay or impede its ac-
complishment."
He also called attention to tthe fact
