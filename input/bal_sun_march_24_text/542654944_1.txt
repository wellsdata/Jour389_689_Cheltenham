BOARD SEEKS
NEW SITE FOR
CHELTENHAM
New All-Negro Group
Contends Institution Is
Too Far From City
In its first official recommenda-
tion, the new board of managers of
the Cheltenham School for Boys
yesterday advocated moving the
school to a site nearer Baltimore.
In discussion preceding their
action, members of the new all-
Negro board expressed the hope
that, in any move to a new site, the
name of the institution and the less
pleasant aspects of its past history
"be left behind."
The board immediately wired
Governor Lane of its recommen-
dation.

Remoteness Cited
✓ A formal report will be made to
the Governor as quickly as pos-
sible, according to Edward N. Wil-
son, registrar of Morgan State
College, who is secretary to the
board.
Several reasons were given for
the desire to move the Cheltenham
School from its present site in
Prince Georges county. Most of
these reasons dealt with the disad-
vantages of the remoteness of the
school from Baltimore.
: The decision that a move would
be advisable followed a meeting
which the board of managers held
at the school with John B. Funk,
chief State engineer.

Supervision Program
Also considered by the board was
a report from officials of the
school that 214 former inmates are
at present on parole and badly
need an adequate supervision
program.,
The fact that Cheltenham is 55.
miles from Baltimore makes the
task of supervision difficult and at
times impossible-and the situation
is especially serious since 90 per
cent of the boys who are sent to
the school are from Baltimore, the
board was informed.
Dr. White and the Rev. Mr. Col-
bert reported they felt that a new
school probably would remedy the
deficiencies at the present institu-
tion in facilities for vocational and
industrial training.

Programs Hindered
If the school is close to Baltimore
when an adequate program of voca-
tional instruction is put into effect,
several board members asserted, it
would be easier to place boys in
jobs in industry and supervise their
progress on the way toward re-
habilitation.
Another disadvantage of the pres-
ent location, according to the sub-
committee report, is the fact that
the distance from Baltimore hinders
development of adequate sports or
"cultural" programs.
Explaining how this has affected
the school, Mr. Wilson said:
"If they want to have a band con-
cert, it turns out that they can't get
an audience.
"If they organize a sports team,
they are too far away to get op
ponents."
