NEGRO FACILITY
TO BE BUILT
State To Provide Structure
For Normal Delinquents
Governor O'Conor said yester-
day the State would proceed with
plans for a new institution for
normal delinquent Negroes, who
now are committed to the Chelten-
ham School for Boys with defective
youths, as soon as a tentative loca-
tion and plans for buildings are
submitted by the State Department
of Public Welfare.
He said what appeared, in a re-
cent report of his Commission on
Post-War Reconstruction and De-
velopment, to be disapproval of this
plan and its replacement by one
calling merely for the improvement
of existing facilities at Cheltenham
was due to the failure of the Wel-
fare Department to submit plans
or a suggested location.
He said he had expressed satis-
faction with the commission's re-
port, including a program to im-
prove the present Cheltenham
School near Brandywine, but to
retain both normal and defective
inmates in the same institution,
because he realized the commission
at present could "go no further"
without a basis for plans.
Became Law In 1943
The Governor pointed out that
a recommendation by the Mary-
land Commission on Juvenile De-
linquency to provide a new institu-
tion for normal delinquents and to
make the present Cheltenham
School an institution exclusively
for defective boys was approved by
him and later was made law in 1043.
"This fact alone leaves no lati-
tude for change or disapproval of
the plan to erect new facilities for
normal boys," he said.
Last May, the Governor recalled,
he had instructed the Department
of Welfare to draw up plans for
whatever buildings it deemed
necessary to accommodate normal
youths now at Cheltenham and to
seek a possible location for the
proposed new facilities,
Was To Have Been Basis
This information was to have
been submitted to the Commission
on Post-War Reconstruction and
Development as a basis for the
portion of its report this year con-
cerning Cheltenham, the Gov-
ernor added.
Governor O'Conor emphasized
he had "gone on record publicly"
in the past as favoring the separa-
tion of normal and defective Negro
delinquents, as provided in the
law passed last year.
"We have gone as far as we can
without' plans being submitted for
new facilities," he said.
