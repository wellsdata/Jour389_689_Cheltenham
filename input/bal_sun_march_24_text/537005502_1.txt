LOST, BUT LIVES HERE
Young Negro Is Unhappy; Justice
Packard Is Perplexed.
Although claiming to have lived in Bal-
timore for the last five years, William H.
Wardell, colored, 16 years old, who was
before Justice Packard, in the Juvenile
Court, yesterday on the charge of vagrancy,
was unable to tell where he had lived. He
was lost, totally lost; more completely lost,
the authorities say, than any boy of his
size they have ever seen-if his story was
true.
Several nights ago he was found sleep-
ing on the softest side of a pine board in
one of the lumber yards near President
Street Station.
."Where do you live?" he was asked.
"Wy - Weraa - Werth street," he an-
swered.
"What number?"
"Five hundred block."
"Then go back there."
Then he said that he was lost.
Five feet 6 inches in height, broad and
with the marks of health scattered every-
where over his round figure and fat face,
he looked unusually well equipped by na-
ture to take his place in the day's work
and do a man's part. There was no sign
of mental weakness. But it was too clear
for discussion that he was lost. Justice
Packard tried to convince him to the cou-
trary yesterday, but to no purpose. Sug-
gestions that the boy be advertised in the
newspapers were considered, but the lack
of some distinctive feature to mark him
made this useless and Justice Packard is
wondering what to do with him.
Paroled Negro Gets Six Months.
:
John E. Brown, colored, was sent to
Cheltenham yesterday by Acting Judge
Packard, of the Juvenile Court, on the
charge of stealing: 50 copies of THE SUN
and other articles, the property of Louis
Fuhrer. Brown is 8 years old and was ar-
rested-on Wednesday for being disorderly
on the street. It developed at the hearing
that he was charged with stealing three
bottles of soda water and was paroled for
six months. Yesterday morning he was ar-
rested on the charge of stealing the papers.
