Criticizes Parents
The most serious case was that
of four young Negro boys who ad-
mitted trying to enter an Amity
st. tavern to steal whisky, which
they intended to sell. They were
frightened away by the woman who
lived over the tavern.
"We all took chances on the lock
with a piece of iron we found,"
one of the boys testified.

Another told how the attack on
the tavern lock came about.
"We gathered in a shed," said the
boy. "Piggy said: 'Oh, we have to
get into the bar tonight.' We went
to the tavern, and then we put in

A mother of one of the boys,
questioned by the judge, said: "I
wouldn't get up out of bed to fool
with that boy. I'm tired of his steal-
ing out. Let him go."
Big Baby
The father of another said: "We
have trouble with him all the
time. He is always in misdemeanors
and I have high blood pressure.
The doctor told me not to exert my-
self, so I let him go."
"It's your responsibility," the
judge said, "to see that this baby is
in bed. Instead, you act like a big
baby yourself."
The judge found the four boys
delinquent and continued the pro-
bation under which each already
was bound. One is to be examined
by a psychiatrist. The parents of
two will be arraigned.
Ready For Cheltenham
An 11-year-old boy whose mother
testified he stays out until mid-
night or 1 A. M., was quite willing
to go to Cheltenham. He already
was under probation.
"Did you bring your suitcase
along?" the judge asked.
"No," said the boy.
"You know where you'll go, don't
you?"
"Cheltenham."
Advised To Quit Job
The probation officer broke in:
"William isn't sure whether he
wants to stay home or go to Chel-
tenham."
When his mother said he goes to
Sunday school, the boy corrected:
"I don't go at all."
When the mother testified that
she and her husband-the boy's
stepfather-both work, the judge
suggested that she stay home.
"I'm not going to send him to
Cheltenham," the judge said. "I'm
telling you to have this boy in off
the streets after dark. We're liv-
ing in a civilized community and
we're not going to have babies
roaming the streets all night. Stop
feeling sorry for yourself. You're
not the only woman in Baltimore!
who has five children."
Slept In Parked Cars
A boy who lives with his grand-
uncle admitted taking $10 of the
old man's money and leaving home
for a week. But, after spending it
he served as a bowling-alley pin
boy until he could pay it back.
Then he went home. He was placed
on probation.
was
Another 11-year-old who
picked up in a restaurant yester-
day at 5 A. M., said he slept in
parked automobiles since Saturday.
He already was under probation
and the judge committed him to
Cheltenham pending further in-
vestigation of the case.
One 12-year-old Negro girl was
among those arraigned yesterday.
She was charged with spending al
night with a 14-year-old boy whose
parents had gone away and left
him alone in the house. Police-
woman White said the girl has had
improper relations with men and
boys since the age of 9 years.
Not Unusual
"It's strange," the judge re-
marked, "that a 14-year-old boy
would be on a premises himself
with his mother away."
"Oh, no," the policewoman said,
"that's not unusual, but this girl
must be removed from that neigh-
borhood."
She was committed to the Mary-
land Training School for Colored
Girls.
Other Cases
Other cases involved two boys
who took 50 cents from another
boy; a boy who tried to enter a
locked house to obtain bottles for
which he would recover deposits;
a 10-year-old boy who took a $45
wrist watch from a neighbor's
dresser and sold it for 5 cents to
another boy, and a boy who took
$20 from a box belonging to his
parents and returned it in lieu of
the $20 he would have given them
had he worked last week instead
of going to the movies every day.
All were put on probation except
an 8-year-old in the 50-cent case,
who was released in his mother's
custody.
