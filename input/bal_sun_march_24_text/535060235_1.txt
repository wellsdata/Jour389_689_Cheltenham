Cheltenham A Model Institution,
He Says.
TO THE EDITOR OF THE SUN-Sir: "E.
J. K.'s" remarks about the Cheltenham
Reforniatory for Colored Boys are from
hearsay, not from personal observation.
Anyone acquainted with the institution
and its superintendent knows full well
that the boys get plenty to eat, are taken
care of and have medical attention when
slek. Boys are sent to the institution in
poor health. but leave it healthy and
strong. I am not connected in any way
with this school,
CHARLES H. SEGUIN,
Baltimore, March 24.
Rev. Mr. Zeigler Approves The Idea
Of Agricultural High Schools.
TO THE EDITOR OF THE SUS-Sir: I
wish to give most unqualified indorsement
to the suggestion of Mr. S. M. Engle in to-
day's Sunpaper that the State estab'ish
county agricultural high schools instead of
running on in a half-hearted effort to build
a second-rate agricultural university. This
larger question the State is not yet ready
for, either from a financial polut of view
or the further point of view of public sen-
timent. Meanwhile, the agricultural high
school is needed, can be readily financed
and can find its proper and particular use-
fulness in this larger scheme. To my mind,
any plan to State universitize, according to
the present talk, is to make a growing
tumor of what-is-now but an excrescence.
Oscar WOODWARD ZEIGLER.
Baltimore, March 25.
