MAY CLOSE PLANT
AT CHELTENHAM
Hungerford Refuses To Class
Broom Factory As Shel-
tered Workshop
APPEAL MAY BE TAKEN
Action Would Result In Annual
-Loss Of $5,000 And Result In
Idleness Of Forty
The broom factory at the House of
Reformation for Colored Boys at
Cheltenham may be closed as a re-
sult of a refusal yesterday by Arthur
E. Hungerford, NRA Compliance Di-
rector, to allow the factory to be
classed as a "sheltered workshop,"
thereby removing it from the author-
ity of that industry's code.
Should Mr. Hungerford's decision be
upheld by the National Sheltered.
Workshop Committee at Washington,
the factory will be closed with a loss
of $5,000 per year to the institution
and the idleness of about forty inmates.
Conditions Under Fire
Cheltenham, which is a private in-
stitution receiving aid from the State,
has been under fire recently because
of alleged conditions at the institu-
tion. A plan also has been suggested
whereby the State would take over
the institution.
As far back as 1923 the Baltimore
Federation of Labor complained about
the broom factory, declaring it reduced
the Negro boys to a "form of peon-
age." Several months ago the Child
Welfare League of America, in a re-
port, condemned the health standards,
diet, dormitories, clothing and virtu-
ally all other living conditions at the
home.
Revenue Cited
The broom factory, which A. S.
Ripple & Bros. established at the insti-
tution more than twenty-seven years
ago, at present pays into the institu-
tion 50 cents per day for every boy
who works in the factory.
In denying the broom concern the
right to classify 'ts plant as a sheltered
workshop, Mr. Hungerford told the
representatives of the home and the
company that they could take the mat-
ter to the National Sheltered Work-
shop Committee for a final ruling.
Mr. Hungerford explained that a
sheltered workshop was one operated
for the benefit of handicapped workers,
such as the Goodwill Industries, Inc.
"The broom factory at Cheltenham
is not a sheltered workshop," Mr.
Hungerford said, "since the institu-
tion never has applied to be classed
as such, and, in my opinion, the fac-
tory is a member of the broom indus-
try, and must observe the code pro-
visions of that industry."
Robert F. Roberts, president of the
board of managers of the institution,
who attended yesterday's meeting with
Enoch Harlan, vice-president of the
board, was optimistic about the re-
sult of the appeal to be made to
Washington.
He admitted, however, that the clos-
ing of the shop "would be a hard-
ship."
