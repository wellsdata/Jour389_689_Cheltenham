21 THREATEN
TO RESIGN AT
CHELTENHAM
White Employes To Act
If Segregation Policy
Is Abolished

Ruling To, Be Effective
When "Administra-
tively Practicable"

Twenty-one white employes of
the Cheltenham School for Boys
have informed Howard C. Hill, act.
ing president of the board of man
agers of the institution for Negroes,
that they plan to resign if the
board's program abolishing restric-
tions segregating Negro employes
from white is put into effect.
The board of managers voted sev-
eral weeks ago to provide for joint
dining and housing facilities for
both whites and Negroes.
Mr. Hill said it was not the in-
tention of the board to put the pro-
posal into effect immediately, but
that it was to be effected as soon
as Vance E. Thomas, superintend-
ent, "found it administratively
practicable."

Effective April 20
Following the board's announce-
ment of its plan, Joseph H. Bland-
ford, president, tendered his resig-
nation to Governor O'Conor and
the latter said yesterday. "there ap-
parently is nothing else to do but
accept it," although he has not
formally done so.
Six of the group of white classi-
fied State employes, who informed
Mr. Hill of their intention to re-
sign, set April 20 as the date for
their resignation unless the reso-
lution of the board was rescinded.
The remaining fifteen stated the
time of their resignation would de.
pend on their ability "to work out
a solution to our own individual
problems by means of retirement,
transfer to other State departments
or to other employment."
With the exception of the con-
clusions, the letters of the two
groups, were identical.
The text of the identical part fol-
lows:
"The present conflict concerning
the prospects of interracial living
for the employes at Cheltenham
School for Boys has created a most
unfortunate situation.
We are anx-
ious to present to the board of
managers our thoughts and convic-
tions concerning this matter.
"The recent resolutions approved
by the board of managers are abso-
lutely administratively
imprac-
ticable.
The board has placed the
superintendent in an impossible
situation.
They have, by the action
of one meeting, destroyed all that
has been accomplished by the pres-
ent administration and staff for the
pact two years

Welfare Jeopardized
"They have seriously jeopardized
the welfare of the 325 boys at the
school.
If the board does not take
steps immediately to instruct the
superintendent to continue with
his policy developed during the
last two years, even more serious
consequences are imminent.
"We should like to make it clear
to the board that we are anxious
to continue serving in our present
capacities for the continued wel-
fare of the institution provided
there is no further attempt to im-
pose interracialism.
"It is not for the best interests
of the Cheltenham School for Boys
to arbitrarily declare interracial-
ism.
We cannot subscribe to such a
policy.
We feel these convictions
so strongly that we shall be forced
to offer our resignations."

To Confer With Thomas
Mr. Hill said he was going to
confer with Mr. Thomas to deter-
mine whether the situation was
"imminent or urgent" enough to
call a special meeting of the board
or wait until next regular meeting
date, the first Thursday in May, to
act on the protests.

Founded in 1879, Cheltenham,
which is located at Crownsville,
Anne Arundel county, was private-
ly operated until 1937, when the
State assumed control through an
act of the Legislature.

Blandford Made Head
Mr. Blandford was named presi-
dent of the board at that time.
He
was in Richmond, Va., at the time
the board acted to abolish the segre-
gation policy and last Saturday sent
his resignation to the Governor.
The controversy came about after
twenty-three conscientious objec-
tors had been obtained from a
morthwestern State to work at the
institution.
There were seventeen:
white and six colored men.
The conscientious objectors re-
sented the segration of white and
colored and their protests resulted:
in the board's action.
