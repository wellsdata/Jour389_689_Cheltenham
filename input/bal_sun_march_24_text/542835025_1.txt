Legislative Group Hears
Debate On Municipal
Employe Organizing
Severe penalties, "comparable to
those imposed for treason," were
recommended yesterday before the
Legislative Council for persons
who organize in an association "for
the purpose for preventing or ob-
structing any of the normal opera-
tions of government until their
demands are met."
The proposal was among several
put forward against unionization
of public employés by Willis R.
Jones, attorney, who, with Walter
IH. Buck, represented two taxpay.
ers in a recent suit against Balti-
more city and a teamsters' union
local with which the city had en-
tered into a contract.
Contract With Chauffeurs
A contract between the city and
the Municipal Chauffeurs, Helpers
and Garage Employés Local Union
No. 825 now is in effect.
This and
earlier contracts, as well as ne-
gotiations leading up to the con.
tracts, were the basis of much of
yesterday's discussion.
Mr. Jones also proposed:
•
That legislation be enacted to
prevent any State, county or
municipal agency of government
from deducting union dues from
the earnings of public employés
and from paying such dues to any
union or association of employés.
That public employés of any
such agency of government be
forbidden to belong to any union
or association of employés which
advocates resort to strikes or
work stoppages or threats of
strikes or work stoppages against
any such agency of government
to force compliance with their
demands.
That any public employé who
engages or participates in any
strike or work stoppage to pre-
vent the orderly functioning of
any agency of government shall
forfeit this right to be employed
by any agency of government
within this State and be subject
to prosecution for disloyal and
subversive activities against the
government.
Mr. Jones was interrupted in the
carly part of his remarks to the
Council when he asserted that
the government of Baltimore city
was "to a very large extent under
the control of one man," identified
the man as Harry Cohen, president
of the Teamsters Joint Council
No. 62. and said: "If Mr. Cohen
wants to fix municipal wages, he
should run for public office."
Senator Kimble Objects

jected to the interjection of "per-

Sobeloff's Assertion
Mr. Cohen had pointed out that
there was "no collective bargaining
agreement with any other political
subdivision in Maryland," and said
that "Baltimore is big enough to
hold its own potato, no matter. how
hot."
Mr. Sobeloff asserted that it was
better to negotiate with a union be-
fore a strike was called than after.
Grievances are settled by collective
bargaining in the last analysis, he
said.
Taking up Mr. Jones's recom-
mendations to prohibit unioniza-
tion of government workers, he
said adoption of the provision pre-
venting organization for the pur-
pose of obstructing government
operations until demands are met.
punishable by "severe penalties,"
would make the legislators the
"laughing stock of people ex-
perienced in legislation."
"Voluntary" Checkoff System
He defended the city's "volun-
tary" checkoff system-upheld by
the Court of Appeals-as an ex-
tension of a privilege accorded
such organizations as the Red
Cross, and the Classified Munici-
pal Employés Association.
The
union pays the city the estimated
cost of collecting, he said.
With regard to outlawing strikes
among government employés, he
pointed out that the city's contract
with the Teamsters Union con-
tained a no-strike clause and said
the proper way to proceed was to
establish sensible and decent labor
relations.
The union, he said, had been
willing, because of the decent atti-
tude shown, to renounce strikes
and agree to have the city engineer
the final arbiter for all grievances.
One Of "Mildest" Agreements
Mr. Cohen described the agree-
ment between the union and city
as "one of the mildest that could
possibly be written or accepted by
a labor organization" and said the
union "recognized its lawful and
dutiful obligations to Baltimore
city."
During the union's regime, since
June, 1941, he said, there has been
one "unauthorized work stoppage"
of twenty minutes in one district of
the city and a strike of less than a
day's duration when Mayor Jackson
delayed in signing a contract.
Wages of the union workers have
risen from 40 cents an hour in 1941
to approximately 80 cents at pres-
ent-still about 10 cents below the
level in industry, he told the
Council.
"Humiliating Language"
Mr. Buck, appearing against or-
ganization of government workers,
cited a resolution of the Board of
Estimates in February, 1944, in
which it was stated the "annual
crisis and strike" had been avoided
by "making slight concessions in
wages and working conditions" to
the union.
This he characterized as the
"most humiliating language any
public officials ever permitted to
come out in this State before."
Also testifying before the coun-
cil were Jack C. Flaherty, execu-
tive secretary of the Baltimore In-
dustrial Union Council, and Car-
lyle Barton, attorney, representing
the teamsters local union.
Dr. Preston Presents
Memorandum OnSchool
A memorandum of legislation:
which will be needed when the
Cheltenham School for Boys is
turned over to the State Board of
Mental Hygiene for an institution
for defective delinquent Negro
boys was presented yesterday to the
Legislative Council by Dr. George
H. Preston, commissioner of men-
tal hygiene.
The training school, in Prince
Georges county, is occupied at pres-
ent by delinquent Negro youths
for whom construction of a new
school has been authorized and is
under consideration.
Dr. Preston's memorandum calls
for establishment of a board of
managers, consisting of six to nine
members, which would operate the
institution under the supervision
of the Board of Mental Hygiene.
All-Negro Staff Urged
Its members would be appointed
by the Governor for staggered
terms of six years, and "should
include persons familiar with the
problems of feeblemindedness, de-
linquency, education, medicine and
institutional administration."
The superintendent would be a
physician experienced in the care
and treatment of defective delin-
quents, and all staff appointments
subject to the merit system.
Dr. Preston said his board
recommended that the board of
managers and all employés of the
institution be selected from com-
petent Negroes.
Per-Patient Charges
Annual maintenance costs were
estimated at $180,000. In addition,
extensive changes in buildings to
make dormitories more secure, pro-
vide a large fenced recreation yard
and an adequate dining room and
kitchen would cost "at least $200,-
000," it was estimated.
The memorandum further states
that each county should pay the
State comptroller $180 a year for
each patient in the institution com-
mitted from that county, and the
same for Baltimore city.
