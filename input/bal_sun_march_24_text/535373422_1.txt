Baltimoreans At Spring Resorts
On The Boardwalk
ATLANTIC CITY, April 8.
None sense three days of rain short-
ened the week and in another sense
lengthened it.
Still, to those fortu-
nate enough to be quartered in the tur-
reted and pinnacled towers that border the
beach front, the downpour made little dif.
ference.

While it was not possible on those days
to parade the boardwalk in a motor coat,
still the great parlors, reception halls and
exchanges in the hotels offered a fine
promenade.
Last Sunday was delightful, and if the
promise of the last day or two is kept,
tomorrow will be another wonderful day.
So few, if any, of the visitors in the crowds
that now are here have any reason to
complain.
As has been the custom for some years
the premier Mask and Wig performance is
set for the night before Easter in one of
the boardwalk playhouses.
Nothing could show more vividly how
easy it is to lose one's self here than the
discovery that the 13-year-old boy, Bernard
Rogers, missing from Philadelphia, has
been tending ponies on the beach front
opposite the IHotel Chalfonte for 10 days,
while the police of every city in the land
hunted for him in vain.
Thousands saw
the boy every day; he wore the silver
spectacles be had always worn and went
under his own name, and yet nobody rec-
ognized him in that week and a half.
Edward McLean, of Washington; Miss
Amy I. Dupont, of Wilmington; Miss Clara
Belle Welsh and George Rohlig are among
those who have made early box reserva-
tion for the Horse Show, to be held on the
Million-Dollar Pier April 19, 20, 21 and 22.
There are many visitors from Baltimore,
some in almost all of the hotels.
Mrs. Henry Warfield Ransom has taken
a Ventnor cottage.
Her daughter is to be
married in the fall, it will be recalled, to
Benson Mann, Jr., of Philadelphia.
Another new cottager in the Chelsea dis-
trict is the daughter of the late Senator
Elkins, of West Virginia, Mrs. Oliphant.
Mrs. Harry Lee Taft, of Chicago, who
married a nephew of the President, has
apartments at the Marlborough-Blenheim
and is to remain until after Easter.
Mrs.
Taft has been entertained in Baltimore.
Mr. and Mrs. Frank II. Callaway have
been much around the exchange in Hotel
Chalfonte during the rainy days.
Mrs. Cal-
laway wore once an attractive house gown
of wistaria silk.
Mrs. John W. Marshall is in the Balti-
more colony at the Hotel Chelsea.
Mr. and Mrs. Parker Cook arrived in
the rainy weather, but are to remain for
the Easter parade.
Mr. and Mrs. D. H. Emory and Miss
Sarah S. Emory are at the Hotel Dennis.
Mr. and Mrs. Henry Clay Matthews are
among the recent arrivals at the resort.
Mrs. Leonard Reeder and Miss Edith
Reeder were on the Boardwalk in a rolling
chair one of the clear days following the
rainy spell.
Mr. and Mrs. Thomas A. Whelan have
arrived.
Mrs. Whelan says it, is their in-
tention to remain over Easter.
Mr. and Mrs. D. H. Wheatley are now at
the Ponce de Leon.
Mr. and Mrs. Augustin M. Hopper were
in the rolling-chair procession together.
Mrs. Hopper has been ill and is here to
get the benefit of the sea air.
George Pleggemeyer is also conveles-
cing.
Mrs. Pleggemeyer is here with her
husband.

the band.concerts at the Million-Dollar
Pler.
