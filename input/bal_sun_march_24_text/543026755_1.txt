CHELTENHAM SHOWS SOME
AWWARENESS OF ITS PROBLEM
The appointment of Claude D. Jones,
now superintendent of the National Train-
ing School for Boys at Washington, to be
director of education at the Cheltenham
School for Negroes, seems to be a good
nove. The appointee was on a list of three
certified to the board by Harry C. Jones,
State Employment Commissioner. More
than that, he has had experience in the sort
of work he is employed to do, both at
Washington and, earlier, at Denver. These
facts apparently indicate that the appoint-
ment is not political..
As to the desirability of having a trained
man in that post there can be no question.
The special committee which made a survey
of Cheltenham strongly recommended this
move, for reasons that are obvious. The
director of education in such a place must
be more than an ordinary teacher, because
he is handling more than ordinarily difficult
pupils. Boys who are easy to deal with
don't go to Cheltenham.
At the same time, if the school is to
serve a social purpose useful enough to
make it worth its cost, the educational side
of its work should be strongly developed.
The institution's excuse for existence is the
hope that it may prevent problem children
from becoming habitual criminals, and all
experience goes to show that mental and
physical training plays an enormously im-
portant part in realizing that hope. To the
extent that appointment of a trained direc-
tor of education shows awareness of the
existence of that phase of the problem on
the part of the governing authorities, the
appointment is encouraging.
