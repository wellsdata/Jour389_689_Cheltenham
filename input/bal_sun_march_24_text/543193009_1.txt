'We Are Still Alive" Is Its
Answer To Recent
Ultimatum
The board of managers of the Chel-
tenham School for Boys yesterday in-
formed Governor Nice it would not
resign nor comply with his demands.
The Governor had demanded that
board members accept the proposed
plan for reorganization of personnel,
put forward an acceptable plan of
their own, or resign.
Cites Promises Made
By implication, moreover, the board
charged Governor Nice with bad faith.
It called attention to promises made
on his behalf that the institution's
employes would be retained by the
State.
The board disclosed that its four
Negro members have resigned "be-
cause of the criticism that has been
heaped upon (them) by people who
do not understand."
Two Refuse To Accept Pay
It further informed the Governor
that of the four board members who
were drawing salaries as employes of
the institution, two were declining to
accept any further salaries, another
was ill and has been off the pay roll
for several months, and the fourth
was one of the members resigning.
The board communicated with the
Governor in an eight-page letter bear-
ing the signature of Joseph H. Bland-
ford, its president. The letter was pre-
pared after a meeting of the board
held at Cheltenham Thursday, and a
copy was given out in Baltimore after
the original had been delivered to
the Executive Mansion late yesterday.
Long Controversy Indicated
This letter was the latest develop-
ment in the fight that has been raging
over the personnel situation at the
Negro reform school since Harry C.
Jones, State Employment Commis-
sioner, announced he could not accept
twenty-six employes into the State
classified service:
It was the first definite indication by
the Cheltenham board that it would
battle to keep its employes on the
State pay roll, and apparently fore-
shadowed a long-drawnout contro-
versy.
Friendly Suit Instituted
Mr. Blandford told the Governor the
board has instituted a friendly suit to
test Mr. Jones' authority to refuse to
pay the employes classified as unfit,
and would await a court ruling on this
before taking other action.
Referring to the Governor's ultima-
tum, Mr. Blandford quoted the follow-
ing section from the statute governing
Cheltenham:
At the expiration of their respec-
tive terins, the Governor shall ap-
point successors
for the term of
three years, and it shall be the duty
of the Governor to fill vacancies for
the balance of the respective terms,
caused by death, resignation, refusal
to act, or removal from the State,
"Still Alive," Members Say
He then added:
"We respectfully submit to you that
we are still alive, have no intention
whatsoever of resigning, are disposed
to continue to act, and none of us ex-
pect to remove ourselves from the
State of Maryland.
"An adjudication of the question
involved in the now pending man-
damus suit will help clarify the situ-
ation. In the meantime, we will con-
tinue to function and carry on the
affairs of Cheltenham, making such
changes as are possible."
Negro Members Quit
The four Negro members of the
board who resigned were:
The Hey, Frederick Douglass, whe
also was the chief parole officer of
the Southern Maryland institution.
Willard W. Allen.
Joseph Briscoe.
Dr. George McDonald.
Mr. Blandford said the board "unani-
mously regrets" the resignation of the
Rev. Mr. Douglass and that he had
done his job well.
He added:
"The writer, and I am sure every
other white member of the board,
knows that these four colored men are
the cream of the colored race in Balti-
more and have been most helpful to
Cheltenham.
"Two of them have served on the
executive committee with the writer
and one other white member for many
months, and these have been particu-
larly efficient and helpful in every
way."
