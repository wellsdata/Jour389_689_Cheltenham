IN NEGRO SCHOOLS
State Interracial Body Urges
Added Educational Facili-
ties In Report
LETTER SENT TO NICE
Commission Recommends Labor
Probe, Repeal Of Segregation
Laws For Public Carriers
Asserting that intelligence. health,
morality and industry are prime fac-
tors in the development of the Negro
as well as any other group-these
being included in the term "equal
opportunity"-the report of the In-
terracial Commission of Maryland to
Governor Nice and through him to the
General Assembly was made public
yesterday.
The report recommended, among
other things that:
Negro teachers and supervisors get
the same salaries as other teachers
and supervisors.
The Princess Anne Academy-East-
ern Shore branch of the Univer-
sity of Maryland ultimately should
have a four-year course, the first
two years being liberalized to
make them more advantageous to
those wishing to pursue other than
agricultural subjects.
Morgan College get increased sup-
port as the only standard college
for Negroes in the State.
A preventorium for Negro children
exposed to a tubercular environ-
ment be established in some part of
the State where climatic conditions
are favorable.
Laws prescribing regulations for the
separation of white and Negro
passengers on public carriers be
repealed.
Additional features at the House of
Reformation for Negro boys at
Cheltenham be extended to include
appropriate industrial training, and
recreational features there be in-
creased.
Labor Probe Asked
In a letter to the Governor that ac-
companied the report, the commission
consisting both of white and Negro
members emphasized that "suitable
provision should be made for a search-
ing survey of labor conditions as re-
lated to the Negroes of the State."
"No racial or social group of Ameri-
can citizens will long remain con-
tented if denied a fair and equitable
share in the education, social and
economic benefits due the people gen-
erally," the letter continued.
"All efforts that deny the steady
flow of these benefits are doomed to
ultimate defeat. The 'golden rule' is
not merely a tenet of religion, but
an inexorable law of social progress.
If checked or hindered for a time in
its application, it ultimately succeeds
through the weakness and folly of
those who deny its truth.
Urges "Fearless Honesty"
"Recent events clearly show that
this State is not exempt from the
most cruel manifestations of racial
antipathy. There seems to be no cure
for these social outbreaks, but the
cultivation of fearless honesty in deal-
ing with interracial relations. A dis-
crimination which begins by denying
the daily rights, public and private,
may end by denying the right of life
itself."
In 1920, it was pointed out in the
report, there were only four county
high schools for Negro pupils of
Maryland in addition to the high-
school work then offered at the Bowie
Normal School. There are now twenty-
seven high schools, Howard county
being the only county that does not
provide high-school training for its
Negro youth. Baltimore county pro-
vides for such high-school work in
the Baltimore city system.
Equal Costs Cited
"If the colored teachers of Maryland
are to make long continued prepara-
tion for efficient professional service,"
the report said, "they must see the
reward for their sacrifice in reason-
able remuneration. No human being
can remain satisfied with less. When
colored teachers make special prepara-
tion by advanced study, it costs them
as much as similar preparation costs
other teachers."
The State appropriated annually
$276,750 to eight colleges open to white
students and $23,400 to Morgan College.
The report stated that "with the Ne-
groes constituting one-sixth of the
population of the State, they receive
less than one-twelfth of the sum ap-
propriated in aid. We respectfully sub-
mit that this is not a fair and equitable
division of State funds."
