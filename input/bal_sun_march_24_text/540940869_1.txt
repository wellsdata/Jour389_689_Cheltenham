GRAND JURY
HITS ORDER ON
CHELTENHAM
Prince George's Body
Urges Action Of Board
Be Nullified
Recommends Dismissal
Of 19 Objectors And 6
Negro Employes
Immediate nullification of the
order to abolish race segregation in
the staff dining and housing facili-
ties at the Cheltenham School for
Boys and prompt dismissal from the
institution of nineteen conscien-
tious objectors and six regular
Negro employes have been recom-
mended to the school's board of
managers by the April grand jury
of Prince George's county.
1
In its report to the judges of the
Circuit Court the grand jury
charged that "a radical group com-
posed of Negro and white conscien-
tious objectors to military service,
who comparatively recently have
been detailed to the institution for
public service, and certain of the
regular Negro employes are de-
manding that the policy of social
interracialism adopted by the board
be put into effect," and that they
"appear to be primarily responsi-
ble" for the "deplorable situation"
found in the jury's investigation.
Cites "Chaotic Condition"
"This action by the board of
managers appears to have resulted
in a chaotic condition within the
institution," the report said.
It continued that, in addition to
practically all white employes, "a
certain group of Negro employes
who appear to definitely want race
segregation continued, have taken
the definite position that they will
sever their connections if any at-
tempt is made to put the board's in-
structions to the superintendent
into effect."
A third recommendation of the
grand jury was that "in the future
all vacancies on the board of man-
agers and in administrative posi-
tions on the staff be filled with per-
sonnel very carefully chosen to
reasonably insure the continued
successful operation of the institu-
tion in line with established prece-
dents."
:
Hill Reserves Comment
Howard C. Hill, acting chairman
of the board of managers, yester-
day said he had not seen the grand
jury report and would have no com-
ment to make until he has read it
fully. Mr. Hill is acting in the place
of Joseph H. Blandford, who re-
signed the chairmanship as a result
of the board's action against segre-
gation. Mr. Blandford was absent
from the meeting on March 25

The grand jury report, copies of
which were ordered sent to Gov-
ernor O'Conor and each member of
the board of managers, said:
"The conflicting peremptory de-
mands of the two groups referred
to have resulted in a situation
which is not only very serious but
is potentially dangerous, and it ap-
pears obvious that an immediate
solution of the problem is necessary
and urgent.
Called Repulsive
"Aside from what may be the
immediate result of the board's
action within the institution itself
it is the feeling of this body that
any policy of social interracialism
there would be very definitely re
pulsive to the great mass of both
white and Negro citizens of the
State, not be for the best interests
of the inmates themselves and
would defeat the very purpose of
the institution's existence, namely,
to prepare and equip Negro youth
of Maryland for future employment
in gainful occupations under white
supervision in a country where
most certainly for generations to
come general social interracialism
between the white and Negro races
probably will not be tolerated."
The grand jury said it was satis-
fied that the "recommendation con-
cerning the removal of certain per-
sonnel can be followed without any
serious result in so far as the op-
eration of the institution imme-
diately following such removal is
concerned, and we sincerely selieve
that all recommendations made are
for the best interests of the institu-
tion itself, its inmates and all
others concerned or interested."
