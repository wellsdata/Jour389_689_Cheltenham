Jurist Was Of National
Reputation And Wide
Activities
Judge Joseph N. Ulman, of the
Supreme Bench of Baltimore, a
jurist and penologist of national
reputation and former president of
the Prison Industries Reorganiza-
tion Board by appointment of Pres-
ident Roosevelt, died suddenly yes-
terday morning at his home, 2615
Talbot road, Windsor Hills. Death
was due to a heart attack.
Judge Ulman was also a member
of the American Law Institute, an
organization of especial importance
in the legal profession devoted to
research in the law; a leader in ac-
tivities of the Baltimore Urban
League, which he served many
times as president; a member of
the board of the Cheltenham School
for Boys; a former member of the
Alumni Council of the Johns Hop-
kins University, and a leader in
Jewish activities, local and national.
In Apparent Good Health
The jurist apparently was in per-
fect health when he awakened yes-
terday morning. The day before
he took his usual long walk and
last week attended to his court
duties and to other interests.
As he was preparing for break-
fast, Mrs. Ulman noticed that her
husband did not look so well as
usual, although he made no com-
plaint. Dr. Samuel Wolman was
summoned -but Judge Ulman was
dead when he arrived.
News of the judge's death spread
quickly and many of his colleagues
on the bench called at his home
during the afternoon. Because of
Judge Ulman's energy and appar-
ent health his death came as a de-
cided shock.
Governor And Mayor Shocked
Both Governor O'Conor and
Mayor Jackson said they were
shocked by Judge Ulman's death.
"I am shocked beyond expression
since it was only Friday that Judge
Ulman spent several hours at my
Annapolis office going over matters
of State importance with me," Gov-
ernor O'Conor said.
"Maryland' has suffered an irre-
parable loss because Judge Ulman
not only was an exceptional and
eminent jurist, but also because
for many years he has been an out-
standing leader in humanitarian
movements in our State.
Missed Train To Make Plea
"His was an unflagging interest
in what he considered just and in
the cause for right. Indicative of
this was a plea he made to me Fri-
da in the interest of a convict
seeking parole. He was so intense,
so interested, that he missed his
train to Baltimore.
"Baltimore and Maryland have
lost a truly great citizen."
Mayor Jackson recalled that
Judge Ulman only a week ago pre-
sided at a large meeting dealing
with Jewish humanitarianism.
"It was indicative of the man, an
honored citizen of our city, a jurist
of highest ideals, of courage and
vision," the Mayor said. "In his
death Baltimore suffers a loss it
can ill afford,"
Born Here in 1878
The son of the late Nathan and
Dina Oppenheim Ulman, he was
born in this city on August 9, 1878.
His father was a member of the
distilling firm of Ulman, Boykin,
and Company.
He attended Baltimore's public
schools and Marston's University
School before he entered the Johns
Hopkins University, where he re-
ceived his bachelor of arts degree
in 1898. He later received his mas-
ter of arts and law degrees at
Columbia University.
He was admitted to the Maryland
Bar in 1901 and started his law
practice as an associate of Samuel
J. Harman. In 1902 he became a
member of the law firm of Harman,
Knapp, Ulman and Tucker, which
later became the firm of Knapp.
Ulman and Tucker.
Appointed By Governor Ritchie
He rotired from the arm when
he was appointed to the Supreme
Bench by the late Governor Ritchie
in 1924 to succeed the late Judge
Charles W. Heuisler, who had
reached retirement age.
From 1908 to 1928 Judge Ulman
was a member of the faculty of the
University of Maryland Law School
He served as president of the
Prisoners' Aid Association from
1910 to 1912. It was in this capacity
that he attracted especial public
attention and established an activi-
ty on which he was to center his
attention for more than a quarter
of a century.
During a period of widespread
agitation over the management of
the Maryland Penitentiary, Judge
Ulman urged former Governor
Phillips Lee Goldsborough to ap-
point a commission to study condi-
tions in the State's penal institu-
tions, probation, parole and kindred
subjects. A legislative bill author-
izing the commission was passed

Meanwhile, Judge Ulman served
as treasurer of the Montrose School
for Girls from 1920 to 1924; presi-
dent of the Hebrew Benevolent So-

1928; a member of the executive
committee of the Community Fund

Urban League of Baltimore from
1931 to 1934; vice-president of the
Baltimore branch of the American
Jewish Congress from 1937 to 1941;
a consultant of the Public Works
Administration beginning in 1938.
Wide Activities
He also was a director of the
National Probation Association, the
National Urban League, the Legal
Aid Bureau of Baltimore; a mem-
ber of the advisory commission on
criminal justice of the American
Law Institute; member from 1938
to 1940 of the committee on edu-
cation and practice of criminal law
of the American Bar Association;
member of the American Socio-
logical Society, of the Maryland
Commission on Prison Labor and
of the Phi Beta Kappa, Phi Alpha
and Omicron Delta Kappa fraterni-
ties. He was a former president
of the Johns Hopkins University
Alumni Association.
In 1933 Judge Ulman wrote a
book that attracted national atten.
tion, "A Judge Takes the Stand."
In the volume he talked about jus-
tice in Maryland and about spe-
cific cases he had tried. In the same
year, he amplified sections of the
book in a speech before the Ameri-
can Prison Association convening in
Atlantic City.
It was at this meeting that he
told the delegates that the coun-
try's penal system "would be
ludicrous if it were not so tragic."
And in 1933 he urged Governor
Ritchie to sponsor a strong anti-
lynch law.
On Federal Committees
The following year Judge Ulman
was named chairman of a newly
formed prison-labor committee of
the American Prison Congress and
chairman of a subcommittee of the
National Industrial Relations
Board. Also in 1934. President
Roosevelt appointed Judge Ulman
head of a committee to make a
study of prison labor and the jurist
was excused from court duties
until the committee's report was
compiled.
Meanwhile, Judge Ulman was ap-
pointed by the late Governor Nice
to a State committee to investigate
idleness in Maryland prisons. The
following year, 1935, President
Roosevelt again called on Judge Ul-
man, appointing him chairman of
the newly created Prison Indus-
tries Reorganization Board, which
sought to ease competition between
prison-made goods and those of
private manufacturers. He resigned
from this board in 1936.
Urges Revision Of Law
In 1937, addressing a meeting of
Federal judges, prosecutors and
lawyers of the Fourth Judicial Cir-
cuit, he said a radical revision of
the criminal law was urgently
needed. Two years later, at a meet-
ing of the International Association
of Chiefs of Police in San Fran-
cisco, Judge Ulman submitted a
program for administration of
criminal justice to afford the maxi-
mum protection of society and give
an opportunity for rehabilitation of
the offender.
In recent years his attention to
juvenile crime and delinquency be-
came a major part of his life.
As late as last month he insisted
that Baltimore ought to have a
juvenile court in which cases could
be heard in decent privacy.
He was the author of many
magazine articles and pamphlets
dealing with criminal law and as-
sociated subjects, and was an asso-
ciate editor of Criminal Law and
Criminology.
Married In 1903
Judge Ulman married Miss Ella
Guggenheimer, daughter of Isaac
Guggenheimer, of the printing and
stationery firm of Guggenheimer
and eil, on June 23, 1903.
Fond of the outdoors, he had as
hobbies tennis, canoeing, walking
and fishing. He maintained a sum-
mer lodge in Maine, where he in-
dulged in outdoor sports every
summer for the past thirty years.
Judge Ulman never was without his
r'pe. He carried it, always one with
a curved stem, wherever he went.
He is survived, besides his wife,
by two children, Joseplı N. Ulman,
Jr., of Detroit, and Miss Eleanor
Ulman, and two grandchildren, Bar-
bara and Barry Ulman.
Funeral services will be held this
afternoon at 3.30 o'clock at his home.
They will be conducted by the Rev.
Dr. Charles Rubenstein, former
rabbi of Har Sinai Congregation.
The active pallbearers will be
Albert D. Hutzler, Sidney L. Ny-
burg, Victor S. Jelenko, Major
Manfred S. Guttmacher, Dr. Alan
F. Guttmacher and Sidney Hol-
lander.
Members of the Supreme Bench
will act as honorary pallbearers.
