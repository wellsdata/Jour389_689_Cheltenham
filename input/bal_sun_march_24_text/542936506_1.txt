50 BOYS UNDER 12.
AT CHELTENHAM
Some As Young As 7, Superin-
tendent Says
Juvenile authorities throughout
the State are committing delin-
quent Negro boys under 12 to
Cheltenham School for Colored
Boys, in violation of the institu-
tion's new intake policies, because
of a critical shortage of foster
homes, Vance E. Thomas, superin-
tendent, asserted yesterday.
Although the new intake rules,
established last January by the
State Department of Public Wel-
fare, provide that only Negro boys
12 or over shall be committed, Mr.
Thomas said some as young as 7
are being admitted.
Fifty of the 300 boys at the insti-
tution are under 12, he asserted.
Problem Outlined To Mayor
"The tendency over the last year
and a half has been to commit
younger boys," he added. "This is
in violation of the new intake
policies, but the practice will have
to continue until more foster homes
are found and home-finding
agencies are enlarged."
Mr. Thomas outlined this prob-
lem, to Mayor McKeldin, who
visited the institution Thursday,
accompanied by T. J. S. Waxter
and Francis. A. Davis, director and
chairman, respectively, of the Balti-
more Department of Public Wel-
fare, and G. Cheston Carey, active
in child-welfare work in the State.
Mr. McKeldin said he visited
Cheltenham because a large num-
ber of Baltimore boys were sent
there, and he wanted to see at first-
hand the care they received.
Program Criticized,
Approximately 80 per cent. of
the boys at the school have been
committed by the Baltimore Divi-
sion of Juvenile Causes, Mr.
Thomas said, the terms of commit-
ment averaging from 15 months to
two years. The city pays $180 a
year for each boy it commits.
The visitors noted that the boys
were given only tablespoons with
which to eat, 'and suggested that
knives and forks be supplied. They
expressed surprise at the inade-
quate sanitary facilities and the
"dungeonlike kitchen" in the base-
ment of the school's administra-
tion building.
Mr. Thomas. pointed out, how-
ever, that a 'law enacted by the
1943 session of the Legislature
called for construction of a new
Cheltenham after the war and de-
clared it would be useless to make
expensive improvements of the an-
tiquated buildings now in use.
Drop In 'Feeble-Minded
The superintendent said there
has been a slight decrease in the
number of feeble-minded delin-
quents committed to Cheltenham
since the State Department of Pub-
lic Welfare assumed control of the
State's four training schools last
June 1. "Borderline cases," with
I. Q.'s ranging between 60 and 70,
still are being admitted in consid-
erable numbers, however.
Under the new intake rules,
which permit municipal authorities
to order temporary commitments
not to exceed 45 days, 83 boys have
been admitted temporarily since
January 1. Of these, 43 have been
released on probation and 22 com-
mitted permanently.
