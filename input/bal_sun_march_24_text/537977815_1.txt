STILL IN NEED OF REFORM
The transfer of Cheltenham to the State
was undertaken largely with the idea that it
would put an end to major abuses. The school
had been a target of criticism for years.
It was thought that if the State took the
institution over it would be possible quickly
to transform it into a reasonably effective
agency for dealing with young Negroes gone
wrong.
Now, nearly a year and a half from the
time the institution came under the ægis of
the State, it is still necessary for the Employ-
ment Commissioner to bring forward charges
growing out of the alleged beating of a 14-
year-old boy accused of stealing a few cigars.
If the commissioner's charges are even par-
tially true, the conclusion is inescapable that
the benefits which were expected to flow from
the State's assumption of responsibility have
not materialized. If such a thing as he de-
scribes can still happen at Cheltenham, the
institution is still far from being the sort of
place to which the State can send delinquent
Negro boys in the hope of making them over
and turning them out as decent citizens.
The trouble seems to lie in the fact that,
when the State took the school over, it was
persuaded also to take its employés into
the merit system without examination. When
the Employment Commissioner held exami-
nations for purposes of classification, he found
that large numbers of those on the pay rolls
at Cheltenham could not come up even to
the minimum requirements of the merit sys-
tem. He refused to classify them, or to ap-
prove pay rolls on which these employés
were listed. The Cheltenham board, however,
which had also been taken over by the State,
backed up the employés, and the case is still
in the courts.
Whatever the legal merits of the commis-
sioner's contentions, his charges in the present
case reinforce the point that Cheltenham is in
perative need of a good house cleaning
