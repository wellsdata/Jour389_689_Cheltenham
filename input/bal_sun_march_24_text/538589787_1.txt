CHELTENHAM'S NEEDS
Governor O'Conor has made public a long
scries of proposals for the improvement of
conditions at Cheltenham School for Negro
Boys. The nature of the proposals makes it
clear that the unnamed committee which
worked with the board of managers in sur-
veying conditions at the school has consid-
ered not merely the physical aspect of
things but the nature of the training offered
and the quality of the personnel which gives
that training.
Cheltenham is one of the oldest and the
most difficult of the State's correctional prob-
lems. The dissatisfactions and open scandals
which it has produced are almost number-
less. Bitter condemnation has been visited
upon it by one grand jury after another. In
private hands it was unendurable; now, for
two years the property of the State, it still
arouses suspicion and dissatisfaction.
The proposals made public by the Gov-
ernor sound well in theory. But adequate
buildings and a staff of improved education
and character are not all that Cheltenham
has needed. It has needed, especially, a dif-
ferent sort of management; it has needed a
new approach and new standards among its
trustees. The proposals put forward by the
Governor do not touch on this essential. Most
Marylanders will hope that silence on this
point does not mean that it has not received
the consideration it needs.
