O'CONOR AIMS
FOR LOW TAXES
Preparing To Draw Budget
For Next Two Years
By LOUIS J. O'DONNELL
By constitutional mandate, Gov-
ernor O'Conor and his fiscal ad-
visers are about to embark on a
job that requires them to estimate
what the per capita income of
Marylanders will be, not in 1945,
but in 1946 and 1947-the prepara-
tion of the State budget for that
biennium.
Under the budget provisions of
the Constitution, the Governor not
only must prepare a schedule of
appropriations for all State gov-
ernmental operations for those
two years, but he is required to
base these allotments of funds on
his estimate of what the revenues
for that period will be.

Only one thing is certain at this
point in the preparation of the
biennial appropriations bill for sub-
mission to the General Assembly
within 20 days of its convening in
Annapolis next January 3 - that
there will be substantial increases

Governor O'Conor, he disclosed
yesterday, is approaching the task
before him with certain definite
objectives in mind, which include:
Avoidance of any new State
taxes.
Holding the State tax rate on
real and personal property at its
present figure of 12 cents per
$100, if possible.
Steering clear of any attempt
to resolve uncertainties in the
long-range cost of governmental
operation by generous recourse
to contingent funds.
In short, the Governor plans to
construct a budget as nearly as pos-
sible along normal lines, he said
yesterday, and recall the Legisla.
ture in special session if and when
conditions warrant or require a re-
vision of appropriations or reve-
nues as the post-war economic pic-
ture develops.
Salary Speeds Legislators
In this connection, the Governor
pointed out that the State's legis
lators now are on an annual salary
and can be called into special ses-
sion at comparatively little extra
expense, and furthermore that the
new method of remuneration ap-
parently encourages them to handle
the business before them in ex
traordinary session with dispatch.
He cited the four-day special ses-
sion on soldier-voting legislation
last March.
The Governor's disclosure that
he wishes to avoid any new taxes,
land hold the real estate levy at its
present figure, indicates that Mary-
landers may expect to resume pay-
ing the full two per cent. Income
tax in the next budgetary period,
which starts July 1, 1945.
Reduced By Proclamation
Under special powers granted it
by the Legislature, the Board of
Public Works by proclamation re-
duced income-tax payments in 1943
and 1944-on 1942 and 1943 in-
come-although the levy remains
at a two per cent. rate on the stat-
ute books.
In 1943 income-tax payers were
permitted to deduct one third of
the amount due after computation
of the levy by the Board of Public
Works proclamation. In 1944 they
had to pay only one half of the
amount due under a two per cent.
levy.
Maryland began the current
fiscal year-1945-on July 1 with
a surplus of $8,961,000, of which
$2,000,000 was earmarked as "work-
ing capital."
Governor Must Decide
A substantial amount of the sur-
plus will be needed, in the way of
a transfer into the debt fund, to
keep the real and personal property
tax at the current figure of 12 cents
per $100. In order to hold it at 12
cents this year it was necessary
to add $450,000 from surplus to
revenues from taxes on real estate
and personal property to fulfill
interest and retirement obligations
on the bonded indebtedness.
As a matter of policy the Gov-
ernor will have to decide whether
to make further heavy drafts on
surplus funds for general govern-
mental operation in order to con
tinue kick-backs to income-tax
payers, or begin to exact the full
two per cent. tax.
With payments halved by the
Board of Public Works proclama-
tion, the levy on 1943 incomes pro-
duced $6,400,000, or roughly 25 per
cent. of the total "general fund"
revenues of $24,000,000 appro-
priated for general governmental
operations.
"General Fund" Category
It is estimated by Walter N. Kirk-
man, the director of budget and
procurement, that the yield of the
levy on 1944 incomes-at the half-
rate figure-would produce about
the same amount.
It is in the "general fund" reve-
nue category that the budgeteers
are up against it in preparing esti-
mates as a base for the 1946-1947
budget. These revenues-the in-
come tax, taxes on beer and liquor,
racing and levies on the gross re-
ceipts of utilities are reflected by
the per capita income.
The tax on real and personal
property is dedicated totally to the
debt retirement fund and can be
estimated with a relative degree of
accuracy, dependent solely on per-
centage of the levy collected.
Dedicated Funds
The dedicated funds, such as
revenues from the gasoline tax,
motor-vehicle licenses, etc., while
they affect the departments and
agencies which draw their support
from them and govern the amount
to be expended on the State's high-
ways, are not reflected in the budg-
et for general governmental pur-
poses.
Mr. Kirkman, whose job it is to
prepare revenue estimates for sub-
mission to the Governor, said
yesterday that he is "not ready to
predict" what the State can expect
in 1946 and 1947.
He does know, however, that the
individual budget requests of the
State's departments and institu-
tions will make urgent demands for
replacement of equipment that has
worn out during the war and for
replacement of automotive equip-
ment.
More Funds For Welfare
It seems likely, according to Mr.
Kirkman, that the Department of
Public Welfare will have to be
granted additional funds on a con-
tingent basis to be prepared against
increasing unemployment after the
war.
Mr. Kirkman also believes the
general cost of operating institu-
tions will be greater than during
the present budgetary period be
cause of increases in the cost of
textiles, food and fuel.

Two Correctional Institutions
There are no more mental pa-
tients than there were a year ago,
the institutions have the buildings
and beds, but are at present short
on help. It is the considered opin-
ion of those in charge of the insti-
tutions that a three shift schedule
must be instituted for personnel
to enable them to recruit attend-
ants and "get the kind of people
they want."
The State is committed to
build-and, of course, maintain-
two new correctional institutions
a new Cheltenham and an insti-
tution for white boys of a type
between a reformatory and
prison.
Teachers To Seek Raise.
Due to an increase in their case-
load of bed-fast tuberculosis pa-
tients, the tuberculosis sanatoria
are expected to ask an increase of
$250,000 in their appropriation.
Except for minor adjustments,
the salary schedule of State em-
ployes in general, now fixed by the
State salary standards law, is ex-
pected to undergo no change.
The State's schoolteachers, how-
ever, are expected to seek increases
in their salary schedules. They are
not included in the salary stand-
ards law.
