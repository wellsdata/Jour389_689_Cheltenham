The Cheltenham Appointment
TO THE EDITOR OF THE SUN-Sir: If the
editorials in your issues of August 6 and 7
are intended as an implied criticism of the
State employment commissioner in certify-
ing Mr. Calvin L. Kiah as eligible for ap-
pointment as superintendent of Cheltenham
School for Boys, then it is not justified.
Under the law that placed the training
schools under the jurisdiction of the Board
of Public Welfare it was specifically provided
that that board "shall prescribe the minimum
qualifications" for the personnel of the train-
ing schools of which Cheltenham is one. The
State employment commissioner had no au-
thority to amend, change or alter the qualifi-
cations so prescribed, and the examining
board, created for the purpose, after person-
- al interview and oral examination, found that
Mr. Kiah did possess the minimum qualifica-
tions for the position of superintendent of
Cheltenham School for Boys with a passing
mark of 90.04 per cent. In these circum-
stances the State employment commissioner
had no alternative other than to certify Mr.
Kiah for appointment, and, as the law also
provides that a qualified veteran "shall be
certified for appointment, employment, rein-
statement or re-employment before non-
veterans," and Mr. Kiah, being a qualified
veteran, he went to the head of the eligible
list.
W. D. OWENS.
State Employment Commissioner.
Baltimore, Aug. 16.
No criticism of the State employment
commissioner was intended. -THE EDITOR,
