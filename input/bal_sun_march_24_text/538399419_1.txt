Washington And Freder-

By GEORGE C. DORSCH
Staff Correspondent of The Sun
Hagerstown, Md., May 9-Farm-
ers of the section of Maryland
hugging the Mason-Dixon Line,
where highways follow roller-
coaster patterns over some of the
richest land in the State, have a
suggestion that they think might
about solve their problems result-
ing from the draft and from the
grand exodus of farm labor to de-
fense industries in this city and
elsewhere.
They would supplement the little
labor available for planting and
harvesting with conscientious ob-
jectors and with inmates of penal
institutions. Of course, there would
always be the women of the farm
to give a hand where and when
they could.
Insist This Should Be Done
There have been no conferences
among the farmers, nor have they
called in any authorities for round-
table talks on the subject. Yet the
farmers of Washingtor county and
those of the upper reaches of Fred-
erick county, far from being de-
jected, insist that this should be
done.
They point out that the Mary-
land State Penal Farm at Roxbury
is only a short distance from Ha-
gerstown and that a great number
of the Washington county farms
are within easy transportation dis-
tance of the institution. Moreover,
some farmers further pointed out
that coupled with a statement of
Maryland officials recently that
vegetables would be canned at the
penal farm was the announcement
that this would keep only a frac
tion of the inmates busy.
Objectors Trimming Fences
It was recalled that some South-
ern Maryland farmers had sug-
gested that boys at Cheltenham
(correctional institution for col-
ored boys) be farmed out in that
section where farm labor is at a
premium.
W. K. Marker, operator of a
large farm near Ringgold, ex-
pressed more eloquently and elabo-
rately what other farmers said.
"Look over there at a crowd of
conscientious objectors trimming
bushes and making fences when
folks in the city are expecting us to
grow and harvest the food they
and the military will eat," he said.

one man to a farm would be a
great help. And some more could
be taken from their camps and

Mr. Marker has ninety acres of
tillable land and only a son, who
may be drafted, to help him. If the
son is called, Mr. Marker will have
nobody to help him get his wheat
and other commodities in barns.
The call for farm hands races
through. Washington county as else-
where in the State.
|
Horse Selling Revived
From the slopes of South Moun-
tain where extensive acres of raspe.
berries stand like regiments of
green- coated soldiers, to the State
line there is a demand for the help
which, it is asserted, has left to
find work either at the Fairchild
aviation plants outside Hagerstown
or for employment at larger wages
in, Baltimore, Washington, Edge-
wood and Aberdeen.
And, while the Washington
county farmers are worrying what
to do, there is developing in the
top stretch of Frederick county-at
Thurmont, to be exact-a renais-
sance of the business which once
made that section of Maryland
particularly prominent. It is horse
selling.
Imported From All Over Nation
Horses are being shipped in from
all parts of the country-even as
far away as Minnesota. And the
customers are coming from many
places looking for horses to sup-
plant their motor-driven wagons
and private machines.
But, while this progresses, the
farmers carry on their daily tasks
with hope that something in the
form of aid will show up. What?
They won't even guess, but they
can hope. Wheat is the pay crop of
Washington county and, according
to M. D. Moore, 'county agent,
yields about 800,000 bushels a year.
Raspberries also are of prime
importance. This year there are
about 800 acres of berries planted.
Stops Planting Extra Trees
Between the berry and the apple
country are the Fairchild plants.
The newest, still incomplete,
spreads out over wide areas ad-
jacent to the city airport. Here
hundreds of former farm hands
come daily to earn, in many in-
stances, in a week what they for-
merly earned on the farm in a
month. And there are many
others who once planted and har-
vested Washington county soil who
motor daily to work at defense
plants in near-by Waynesboro,
Pennsylvania.
"Apple Dan" Martin, who has
ten apple orchards scattered
through this part of the State, or
about 300 acres of apple land with
thirty trees to the acre, has usually
shipped between 60,000 and 70,000
bushels of apples a year. Now he
has stopped planting additional
trees. And the trees need at least
eight sprayings.
"From April 1 to July 1 they
should be sprayed every week or
ten days," Mr. Martin said. "I have
two big sprayers for the purpose,
and we paid men on a weekly basis,
ranging from $18 to $30, and they
still walked out. I've got six men
for spraying, but I don't know
when they'll go.
Have To Let Them Drop And Rot
"Then at the harvest time, any-
where from September to Novem-
ber, the apples have to be picked
and packed. We've paid 35 cents an
hour for sixty hours.
"If the draft and defense indus-
tries continue to take the help we
have-other apple growers and my-
self-we'll just have to let the
apples drop and rot. And I have,
for example, 8,000 crates and trucks
to handle them.
"I suggest that inmates of the
State Penal Farm at Roxbury be
sent into the orchards. We could
use them. Now it is a community
idea. Everybody helping everybody
else. And we'll have to do the best
we can in this fashion and not be
sure of the results."
"We'll Help Each Other Out"
Benjamin Risser has a farm of
150 tillable acres, of which forty
are in wheat and ten in barley. He
indorsed Mr. Martin's statement
about community work.
His fertile fields, incidentally,
are across the road from the air-
port and within view of the new
Fairchild plant, which has attracted
farm hands who otherwise would
be working for him.
"I have a thrashing machine and
we'll help each other out," said Mr.
Risser. "Nothing is ever too bad
that it couldn't be worse, and we'll
all go along together."
That theme is sounded in nearly
every section of Washington
county. There is a close-snit neigh-
borliness about the people in this
part of Maryland.
