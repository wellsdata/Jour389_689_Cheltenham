COUNCIL HEARS
OF CHILD NEEDS
Waxter Asks Early Building At
Social Agencies Meeting
Maryland should put into effect
"as soon as possible" an act of the
1943 General Assembly directing
the construction of new institutions
for defective children, and the es-
tablishment of a new training
school for delinquent Negro boys
now sent to Cheltenham, Thomas
J. S. Waxter, head of the Municipal
Department of Welfare, told the
Council of Social Agencies yester-
day.
The recommendation followed a
similar demand made Tuesday by
the advisory committee of the State
Board of Mental Hygiene, which is
studying the problem of adequate
care for feeble-minded and delin-
quent children.
Council Of Social Agencies
Mr. Waxter, addressing. the
eighth annual meeting of the Coun-
cil of Social Agencies at the Lords
Baltimore Hotel, added that Chel--
tenham should be given a com-
pletely Negro staff and should be
"programed to. rehabilitate chil-
dren and not simply to provide
custody for certain children who
represent a problem to the public
peace."
The director went on to state
that there are practically no insti-
tutional facilities for the dependent
Negro child, although there are
numerous such establishments for
the white child.
Additional Facilities Needed
"The Community Fund might
well consider expanding the facili-
ties at the small Negro institution
known as the Bragg House," he de-
clared, "Consideration should also
be given to the need for convales-
cent care of Negro children to
parallel the care now given white
children in Happy Hills. Additional
facilities are urgently needed also
for feeble-minded children."
The speaker suggested that the
next Legislature "might well con-
sider the establishment of a 'Youth
Authority' to provide for young
people between the ages of 16 and
10."
Mr. Waxter linked the problem
of juvenile delinquency with that
of housing, declaring, "many chil-
dren, both white and Negro, in
Baltimore, live under conditions
which almost inevitably will affect
adversely the growth of a whole-
some adult personality.
Protection Of Values
"It has been pointed out time
and again that the city must erad-
icate its slum areas so as to pro-
tect property values. Human values
demand the same action. The effort
must be made to insure decent
housing to all citizens so that chil-
dren may be raised to be normal,
happy, industrious adults."
Maintaining the national income
at a high level will be the "most
vexing problem of the post-war
period," Mr. Waxter said. "This
means work for the able-bodied at
compensatory wages. The burden
of this will fall predominantly
upon private enterprise.... Be-
hind private enterprise, the nation
may, again, have to assume the re-
sponsibility of a works program.
Hopes For No More WPA
"It is to be hoped that if such
a necessity arises such devices as
the WPA, with the so-called 'sub-
sistence wage' will not be resorted
to, and that employment will be
given on work which is socially de-
sirable and at accepted rates of
pay.
"Following financial security in
importance may be an adjustment
of the difficulties which are so dy-
namic at the present time between
the dominant white majority of
the people who live in the city and
the 20 per cent. minority who are
Negroes. The solution of this prob-
lem rests in the future. It will, how-
ever, require the best that the lead-
ers in both groups have in the way
of understanding and tolerance.
"There is no royal road to its
solution, nor any master plan. It
will have to be worked out by an
innumerable series of adjustments
spread over a long period of years.
This problem should be faced
frankly with the facts placed before
us on the table without in any way
minimizing its inherent diffi-
culties."
