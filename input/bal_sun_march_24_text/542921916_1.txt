STATE FUNDS
RECOMMENDED
FOR J.H.U. UNIT
Public Works Board Backs
Postwar Project For
Engineering School
The allocation of State funds for
planning the construction of two
buildings for the engineering school
at the Johns Hopkins University was
recommended to the Board of Pub.
lic Works yesterday by the Mary-
land Commission on Postwar Recon-
struction and Development.
Commission members said it
is understood that Johns Hopkins
officials also will ask the Legisla-
ture to appropriate funds for the
construction, estimated to cost
$1,250,000.

First Of Its Type
It was noted at the commission
meeting that the recommendation
was the first made by the group for
the use of State planning funds on
any but a strictly public institution.
Acting on reports by the commit-
Lee on public activities, the commis-
sion also voted approval of con-
struction at an estimated cost of
$4,274,000 for the University of
Maryland; the establishment of a
new Maryland Training School for
Colored Boys, at an estimated cost
of $1,600,000; buildings at Morgan
State College, at an estimated cost
of $957,000; at the Maryland Train-
ing School for Boys, $166,500; at
Montrose School for Girls, $161,820;
for the Maryland State police,
$290,500, and at the Women's
Prison, $21,500.

Laboratory And Classroom
E. Brooke Lee, chairman of the
committee on public activities, said
the committee's action on the Johns
Hopkins plans was taken at a meet-
ing called at the request of Gov-
ernor O'Conor. The university was
represented at the meeting by Dr.
Isaiah Bowman, president; Dean
W. B. Kouwenhoven, of the School
of Engineering, and Dr. A. G. Chris-
Cie, professor of mechanical en-
gineering.
The proposed buildings, are a
(Continued on Page 8, Column 2)
laboratory and classroom building
at an estimated cost of $500,000,
and a laboratory for industrial re-
search, estimated at $750,000..
Mr. Lee said he previously had
discussed the question with Wil-
liam C. Walsh, Attorney General,
and had been advised that the
project could be legally financed
by legislative action, "probably
along the same lines that were fol-
lowed when the 1912 session
made $600,000 available for the
construction of the present build-
ings of the engineering school" at
the university.
Mr. Lee also pointed out that
the State is now appropriating $93,-
000 a year toward the operating
cost of the engineering school and
that the State is receiving scholar-
ships to the school in return.

Cites Number Of Graduates
Dr. Bowman told the committee
that the average number of engi-
neering school graduates for 1939,
1940 and 1941 from both Johns
Hopkins and the University of
Maryland was 106 a year, which, he
said, represented a 50 per cent de-
ficiency in the number of engineers
annually graduated in Maryland as
compared to such graduation in the
nation.
Dr. Bowman emphasized "in con-
siderable detail," Mr. Lee said,
"the importance of Johns Hopkins
University work in industrial re-
search both in connection with
the prosecution of the present war
and in connection with improved
industrial processes."
He said the details could not be
made public because of war
secrecy.

Plans For College Park
The plans for the University of
Maryland include a building for
engineering, chemistry and physics
to cost $750,000, to be built at Col.
lege Park. Other College Park
buildings include general class-
rooms, an agriculture building, a
building for girls' activities, three
girls' dormitories, two men's dor-
mitories, and additions and im-
provements to the plant at an esti-
mated cost of $3,111,000.
Approved for the University of
Maryland in Baltimore are a psy-
chopathic hospital, additions to the
dental building and nurses' home,
a recreation hall, and completion
of the twelfth floor of the Univer-
sity Hospital, at a cost of $695,000.
New University of Maryland con-
struction at Princess Anne would
include a men's dormitory, eight
faculty cottages and one faculty
apartment house, a dining hall and
kitchen, reconditioning of the agri-
culture building, an athletic field
and stands and other improvements
at a cost of $468,000.

For Normal Colored Boys
The proposed Maryland Training
School for Colored Boys would be
for normal boys now sent to the
Cheltenham School for Boys from
juvenile courts. Cheltenham would
be continued for the mentally de-
ficient.
The proposed location of the
school is near Catonsville, Mr. Lee
said. It would house only 243 boys,
but unlike Cheltenham, where
some boys remain for several
years, it is believed that each boy
sent to the new school would re-
main not more than three years.
It would be under the super-
vision of the Department of Public
Welfare, while Cheltenham would
be under the Department of Mental
Hygiene.

Morgan Proposals
The Morgan proposals include a
classroom building, a dining hall
for 350 persons, a women's dormi-
tory, a men's dormitory, an audi-
torium and quarters for the col-
lege staff.
The committee said the college
is crowded beyond capacity and
that many more students, some of
them now in the armed forces, are
expected to enroll after the war.
The Maryland Training School
for Boys construction includes liv-
ing quarters for the staff and an
addition to the power plant.
The Montrose School projects
are an addition to the vocational
building, a dormitory and a school
addition.
For the Maryland State Police
department, the committee recom-
mended a new headquarters and
training school at Pikesville, build-
ings at Waldorf, Cumberland and
Frederick.

new canning and storage building
