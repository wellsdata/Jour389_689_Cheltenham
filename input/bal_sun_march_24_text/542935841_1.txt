YOUTH DETENTION
HOMES CROWDED

No Facilities For Mentally De-
fective Children

There is no State-operated place
in Maryland to care for or detain a
juvenile delinquent.

There is no State-operated place
to care for or detain a mentally de
fective juvenile.

There is no State-operated place
to care for a neglected child.

These facts were revealed yester
day by the State Department of
Public Welfare; the State Board of
Mental Hygiene and the Baltimore
Council of Social Agencies.

Room For Six More Girls
Of the four juvenile correctional
institutions in the State only one,
the Maryland Training School for
Colored Girls, is not filled to or
beyond capacity, and this institu-
tion has room for exactly six more
girls.

Cheltenham School, for colored
boys, which has a current operat-
ing capacity of 240, now houses
270 boys, at least 50 of whom are
mental defectives, who, officials
agree, should not be there at all.
There is no segregation of these
mental cases from the other boys.

Montrose School for Girls is
filled to its capacity of 125, and
there is a "limited waiting list."
There is "a very small percentage"
of mental defectives in this school,
and there is no provision for their
segregation from the other girls.

Overcrowding
Maryland Training School for
Boys has a current operating ca-
pacity of 240, but there are 286
boys crowded in the institution. At
the present time there are no men-
tal defectives.

Until three months ago feeble-
minded children, along with de-
linquents, were committed to the
four correctional institutions be-
cause there was no room for them
elsewhere.

There still is no room for these
mental defectives, but they no
longer are being committed to the
correctional institutions. They are
not being committed anywhere.

Nothing Could Be Done
Dr. George H. Preston, of the
State Board of Mental Hygiene,
was asked yesterday what could be
done if the need arose during the
day to take care of a mentally
defective child.

"Absolutely nothing," he replied.

Crownsville State Hospital for
mentally defective Negro men and
women, boys and girls, from city
and State, has 190 beds "constantly
full," Dr. Preston said.

"Beyond that there are no other
provisions for feeble-minded Negro
children," he said.

"Beyond Capacity"
There are 1,200 beds at Rose-
wood, the institution for mentally
defective white children, both boys
and girls, from the city and coun-
ties, and it is "crowded beyond ca-
pacity," Dr. Preston said.

"There is literally nothing which
can be done at present to expand
facilities.

"The problem is, how are we
going to divide existing facilities
which cannot be expanded?" Dr.
Preston said.

"No Other Place"
In this connection, Dr. Preston
said the advisory committee at
Rosewood will meet next Monday
to confer with Dwight H. Ferguson,
director of institutions of the State
Department of Public Welfare.

This may result in the resump-
tion of the practice of sending men-
tally defective children to the four
correctional institutions, it was in-
dicated.

"I know it is wrong for the cor-
rectional institutions to take mental
defectives," Dr. Preston said, "but
There is no other place."

Big Waiting List"
Right now there is a big waiting
list of more than 100 neglected
and dependent children, while ap-
proximately 700 children are being
cared for in less than 300 foster
homes, according to the Council
of Social Agencies.

To assist the State Department of
Public Welfare in its plan to find
places for delinquent or neglected
children the Council of Social
Agencies plans to conduct a drive
in Baltimore and Baltimore county
to find 300 additional foster homes
with accommodations for 400 chil-
dren.

In order not to conflict with a
Red Cross campaign, the drive for
the foster homes will not begin
until April 1.
