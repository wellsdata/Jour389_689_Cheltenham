Arthur GuiteRMAN wrote the
libretto for the new all-American
opera, with music by Walter
Damrosch, which was produced
recently. It is the classic story of
"The Man Without a Country."
Guiterman is one of America's
most popular writers of "light"
verse, but underneath the rip-
pling rhythms, the clever rhymes
and the humor there is often a
kindly but deep philosophy. Не
likes especially to point a satiric
finger at faddists, posers, snobs,
and pretenders generally.
His most famous poem,
"Strictly Germ-Proof," was a
rollicking bit about an "antisep-
tic baby and a prophylactic
pup." This poem has been read
around the world and translated
into Esperanto.
Six people have liked it so well
that they have claimed it as their
own. One man sold it, as his
work, to the "New York World"
two years after it had appeared
in "Woman's Home Compan-
ion," and was so determined to
prove that he was the author
that he forged "affidavits."
On page 12 of this issue you will
find some delightful little verses
written by Guiterman especially
for THIS WEEK.
ARTHUR GUITERMAN
Ten years ago a novel called
"The Hounds of Spring" was
heading best sellers in the United
States. It was written by a girl
of 23 who had been expelled from
Cheltenham for pawning a pair
of stockings and invited to leave
Oxford because she wouldn't
study. Sylvia Thompson was gay,
beautiful, feminine, with a taste
for dancing, traveling, ice cream,
seventeenth-century furniture
and paintings by Whistler.
Since this first successful novel
she has written eight more, and
become one of England's most
distinguished women novelists.
In an early issue we shall publish
a short story by her:
