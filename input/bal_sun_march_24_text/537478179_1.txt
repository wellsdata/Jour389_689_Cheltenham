BLANDFORD
QUITS POST AT
CHELTENHAM
Resigns Following Board's
Decision To Abolish
Race Segregation
Managers Voted To Pro
vide Joint Housing
For Employes
Joseph H. Blandford yesterday
tendered his resignation as presi
dent of the board of managers of
the Cheltenham School for Boys
following the board's decision to
abolish all restrictions segregat.
ing the institution's Negro em
ployes from the white."
In a letter to Governor O'Conor,
Mr. Blandford stated his resigna-
tion would become effective imme-
diately, as he "could not go along
with the board members who voted
to establish an interracial situa-
tion among the employes of the in-
stitution."
The board voted to provide joint
dining and housing facilities for the
Negro and white workers at a meet-
ing held ten days ago.
Away At Conference
Mr. Blandford was away at a con-
ference at Richmond, Va., and al-
though he had authorized the meet-
ing to be held in his absence, he
said yesterday he did not know the
employe question would be dis-
cussed.
Howard C. Hill, vice-president of
the board, presided at the meeting
attended by the nine other mem-
bers.
"I give credit to the board mem-
bers for their full sincerity," Mr.
Blandford stated, "but I think they
are wrong.
At least their views are
different from mine and I cannot
go along with them."
Unhappy Over Decision
The text of Mr. Blandford's let-
ter to the Governor, dated April
10, is as follows:
"I am very unhappy over the de-
cision of the board of the school
to establish an interracial situation
among the employes of that institu-
tion.
"I feel as do practically all white
people, as well as the great mass of

26 Years With School
"Indeed, it is my firm conviction
after twenty-six years connection
with this institution, together with
a lifetime in that part of the State
which has the largest proportion of
Negroes to whites, that the princi-
pal of interracialism is revolting
to the senses of all our white people
and is not desire1 by the Negroes
themselves.
"Since assuming the presidency
of the board of managers of the
Cheltenham School for Boys in
1937, ou policy has been to em-
ploy Negroes in positions of close
contact with the inmates-such as
cottage masters, school teachers,
etc.-leaving administrative and of-
fice personnel in the hands of the
whites, with separate eating, sleep.
ing and recreational facilities for
the two races.
Apparent Satisfaction
"This arrangenient has apparent-
ly worked to the satisfaction of all
concerned, and I know of no reason
why it could not have been contin-
ued indefinitely with beneficial re-
sults to the inmates. In other words,
we have had a bi-racial situation on
the employment level and segrega-
tion on the social level.
"I have frequently referred to
the fact that inasmuch as many of
the boys will in the future work
under white supervision, they
should become accustomed to such
supervision while at the school.
"During the entire period of my
incumbency as a member of this
board I have diligently applied my
self to the problem of obtaining so-
cial and economic rehabilitation for
these unfortunate boys, most of
whom are at Cheltenham only for
the reason that they were neglected
in early childhood or for some
cause got off to a bad start in life.
"Definite Progress"
:
"I feel that we have made defi-
nite progress in the direction of re-
leasing boys with a sufficient
amount of skill in such lines as
laundering, shoemaking, dairying,
canning, plumbing, vegetable grow-
ing and other activities, as to en
able them to render satisfactory
service to their employers.:
"Our main objectives have been
to instill in the boys a desire to
go straight and to give them the
ability to earn an honest living.
"During the past several months.
however, I have observed these
objectives losing ground and in
their place has come a definite
trend toward using Cheltenham as
a laboratory for testing out un-
wholesome experiments in social
and racial problems.
"Unalterably Opposed"
"I am unalterably opposed to
any attempt to divert Cheltenham
from the purpose for which it was,
and is, intended, or to waste State
funds in providing a small radical
group the opportunity to exploit
theories that are offensive to the
great mass of our citizens.
"I am therefore submitting my
resignation as a member of Chel-
tenham, to take effect today."
Mr. Blandford was named presi-
dent of the board when the State
assumed control of Cheltenham in
October, 1937. Founded in 1870, the
institution had been operated pri-
vately until that time.
