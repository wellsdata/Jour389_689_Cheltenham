By LOUIS J. O'DONNELL
[Annapolis Bureau of The Sun]
Annapolis, Jan. 12-The personnel
of Governor Nice's committee charged
with seeking sou ces of additional
State revenue was disclosed today.
Under the chairmanship of William
J. Casey, director of the Baltimore
Commission on Governmental Effici-
ency and Economy, the committee
will canvass the revenue field and
advise Governor Nice what sources
should be tapped to finance such leg-
islation as relief, old-age pensions
and unemployment insurance.
Appointment of Mr. Casey, who is
regarded as an expert on taxation and
fiscal matters, was announced some
months ago. He also will head a "su-
per-committee" to be composed of the
chairmen of various groups intrusted
by the Governor with the task of draft-
ing legislation for his social program.
Members Named
The members of the committee that
will function under Mr. Casey on
revenue questions include:
ADAM J. HAZLETT, president of the
Eastern Rolling Mills Company, a
director of the Baltimore Associa-
tion of Commerce and a member
of the Baltimore Emergency Relief
Commission,
OSCAR LESER, minority member of
the State Tax Commission and an
authority on taxation.
ALBERT D. HUTZLER, department store
president, former president of the
Associated Jewish Charities and of
the Baltimore Association of Com-
merce.
CHARLES C. WALLACE, City Solicitor in
the first administration of Mayor
William F. Broening.
ALLAN SAUERWEIN, director of the
Baltimore Association of Com-
merce and legal member of the
Baltimore NRA Compliance Board.
JAMES E. HOOPER, vice-president of
the W. E. Hooper Cotton Mills and
a director of the Baltimore Asso-
ciation of Commerce.
First Meeting Monday
Mr. Casey said last night that the
committee would hold its first meeting
Monday afternoon.
It will be, he said, an advisory body,
the function of which will be to iay
before Governor Nice reports on the
effect various forms of proposed new
taxes would have on the ecenomic sit-
uation and various industries.
"Our committee," said Mr. Casey,
"will cover the entire field, dealing
with the problem of where additional
revenue will come from and its gen-
eral effect on business. We do not ex.
pect to develop any legislation.
Looks Like "Rush Job"
"It looks like a rush job that must
be done in a hurry, and we'll do the
best we can with it.
"Of course, it is understood that we
cannot adopt any permanent reorga-
nization of the State revenues in this
emergency situation."
Mr. Casey said any widespread re-
adjustment of the Maryland system of
taxation would entail months of work.
The job before them, he pointed out,
must be completed in a brief space
of time, because the Legislature will
be in session only until April 1.
There will not be time, he said, for
many meetings of the committee and
he expects that the members will have
to devote much of their time to ac-
complish. their task in the period al-
lotted them.
Group Fully Qualified
The committee, he added, is to his
mind "most representative" and fully
qualified for the task entrusted to
them.
Governor Nice pointed out that the
personnel of the committee had been
selected from among men who previ-
ously had been making a study of the
taxation problem.
"They have been investigating and
giving the best of their thought and
talent to the task of helping to solve
the financial problems of the State of
Maryland," he said. "The very men-
tion of the names of these gentle-
men is in itself a guarantee that these
problems will receive thoughtful and
intelligent consideration, and that
their findings will be constructive,

Judges Discuss Report
On Negro Reformatory
Grand Jury Committee Suggests
Institution Be Taken Over
By State
Judges of the Criminal Court yes-
terday were considering a report filed
by a special committee of the retiring
grand jury which suggested that the
House of Reformation for Colored
Boys at Cheltenham be taken over by
the State.
Now supported almost entirely by
the State, Baltimore city and the
counties, the institution, according
to the report, is overcrowded and lacks
the proper medical facilities. The "rec-
reational and social expression and
development of the boys at Chelten-
ham seem non-existent," the report
says.
The report recommended that all
the boys receive training in "produc-
tive work," suggesting agriculture,
poultry raising, husbandry, small
fruit farming, landscape gardening and
manual training.
NAMED BY FIRE VOLUNTEERS
Karl A. Young Reelected At
Mount Rainier
Mount Rainier, Md., Jan. 12-Karl
A. Young has been reelected chief of
the Mount Rainier Volunteer Fire
Department.
Other officers elected were: William
S. Freeman, deputy chief; Bernard
Beall, captain; James Mezzanotte,
lieutenant; Simon Lebowitz, sergeant;
Harry Robertson, superintendent of
machinery; A. R. Gill, fire marshal;
Edward Reier, secretary, David Lang.
financial secretary and Julius Reineis,
treasurer.
