SCHOOLS SEEK

Maryland And Chelten-
ham Ask Authority To
Spend $6,000,000
| Maryland's two training schools
for boys are seeking authority to
spend nearly $6,000,000 for capital
improvements in the two-year
period beginning July 1. 1950.
The Maryland Training School
for Boys at Loch Raven has pro-
posed expenditure of $2.213.000 on
construction including a separate
group of buildings for younger
boys, a new cottage in the present
group, a gymnasium and a building
to house 70 staff members.
Plans of the Cheltenham School
for Boys primarily are for con-
struction of an entirely new train-
ing school for Negro boys at a new
location at an estimated cost of
$3,645,994 in the first two years of
construction.
Expenditure Breakdown
Its proposed expenditure of
$125.950 at the present institution
is described as "construction and
repairs to conform with instruc-
tions of fire and health depart-
ment," with $100.000 of the
amount allocated to the boiler
house and $20,000 toward building
a shed for cattle.
Taken together, the programs of
the four training schools, including
the Maryland Training School for
Colored Girls and the Montrose
School for Girls, present a total
proposed expenditure during the
biennium of $7,310,305. Each pro-
gram is to undergo review by the
State Planning Commission.
The Maryland Training School
for Colored Girls wants permission
to spend $830,011 including $237,-
068 for a central heating plant,
$200.000 for two new cottages for
girls, $150,000 for a reception cen-
ter and infirmary, $136.418 for an
addition to the administration
building. $65.000 for a sewage con-
nection and $25.000 for a superin-
tendent's home.

Montrose has proposed expendi-
tures of $113.100 for a staff build-
ing, $128.000 for a dormitory for
25 girls, $211,900 for a school build-

Tor a swimming pool and $33.700
for furnishings, or a total outlay
of $496,300.

Treatment Of Children
"There should be a definite type
of treatment as applied to children
13 years of age and under as against
that used for 14 years and up," the
institution's statement said.
"This is sound in light of the
whole educational philosophy of
America as well as the normal
behavior patterns of those 13 and
under and those above puberty.
"It is also felt that the Mary-
land Training School should be
planning to take care of all white
delinquents in the State of Mary-
land looking toward the closing of
St. Mary's (Industrial School.
"In line with this and even if
the population of both institutions
is kept at a possible minimum as
against past years, we will need
to look toward a population of 400
in the near future with a possible
increase of 40 to 50 in later years.
Eight Cottages Needed
"With the treatment of children
in units of not more than 30 for
those boys of high school and in
advanced age, and 25 with the
younger children who require more
individual education, and after
consulting with Mr. Funk (John
B. Funk, chief engineer of the
State), the board felt that a unit
consisting of eight cottages, with
proper educational building with
an auditorium for church and
chapel, should be established for
the junior group.
"These figures indicate at least
five boys in each unit more than is
generally advocated in better-run
delinquent schools.
"When we stop to consider that
in the State only three children are
recommended for boarding home
care (no more than five) and that
these children are not delinquents
in any way, but are just boarders.
it is beyond the conception of good
treatment procedure to expect a
couple to handle more than 25 boys.
"We feel that we should have
fewer boys in the cottages but the
cost of this to the State would be
too great."
For New Negro Institution
The Cheltenham board of man-
agers placed itself on record some
months ago as favoring construc-
tion of a new Negro institution
closer to Baltimore city.
Estimates of initial costs include
purchase of site, $150,000; ten cot-
tages for 25 boys, $1.000.000;
superintendent's residence. $25,000;
staff quarters, $91,200; gymnasium,
$300,000; boiler house, $165,000;
vocational building. $400,000: aca-
demic building. $215,000; culinary
department, $343,700; administra
tion building, $132,000; farm and
auxiliary buildings. $39.500; equip-
ment, $195.000; sewage disposal,
$175.000, and garages, $30,000.
Other expenditures estimated in
connection with the new institu-
tion are landscaping, $84,840; utili-
tfes, $117.108, and architectural
and engineering fees, $182.046.
Four-Year Expenditures
Additional classrooms for $155,-
1000, and auditorium for $200.000
and other improvements costing
$355,000 would be carried out in

Commenting on the programs of
the four institutions, J. Milton Pat-
terson, director of the State De-
partment of Public Welfare, which
oversees their operation, told the
Planning Commission:
"We believe these plans repre-
sent concretely a State plan for a
responsible service for children
and equal opportunity for all chil-
dren receiving the service of the
training schools.
Cheltenham Needs Vital
"We cannot say that any one
plan, except that of Cheltenham.
takes precedence. Cheltenham needs
immediate repairs and improve-
ments requested to provide for the
safety and health of children. It
cannot provide a training program
without what is included in the
building request for the next two
years.
"You will note that the Chelten-
ham board, with regard for the ulti-
mate costs to the State, has re-
duced expenditures on the present
plant to provide only the minimum
essentials for health and safety."
Mr. Patterson's letter pointed out
that the department was "con-
cerned" about what the planning at
the institutions amounted to at
present-day costs. It added: "How-
ever, we believe that the expendi-
tures now will ultimately repre-
sent a saving in money to the
State."
