Directors of the House of Reforma-
tion for Colored Boys at Cheltenham
have ratified an agreement by which
control of the institution will be turned
over to the State, Morris A. Rome, a
member of the Board of State Aid and
Charities, announced yesterday.
Mr. Rome and Enoch Harlan, acting
president of the institution, were ap-
pointed Monday as a committee to
"see what could be done toward hav-
ing the State take control" of Chel-
tenham.
They held a conference Wednesday,
drawing up a tentative agreement
which, Mr. Rome said, was satisfactory
to the Board of State Aid and Chari-
ties. When the agreement was pre-
sented to the Cheltenham board yes-
terday, favorable action was taken.
Mr. Rome said he had been informed
by Mr. Harian,
"Full Accord"
"We are in full accord now," Mr.
Rome said. "We have been trying to
accomplish this for fifteen years, and
now all that remains is the mechanical
procedure of drafting a bill to be
presented to the Legislature next
month."
Under the agreement, according to
Mr. Rome, Cheltenham will turn over
to the State its entire property, repre-
senting an investment of approximate-
ly $500,000, and the State will control
and maintain the institution.
Conditions at Cheltenham have been
investigated a number of times by
grand juries, committees of the Board
of State Aid and Charities and other
groups interested in social problems
and the care taken of delinquent
Negro youths.
At a meeting of the Board of State
Aid and Charities Monday, Governor
Nice criticized conditions at the in-
stitution as "deplorable," although he
said an improvement had been noted
in the last year or two.
Privately Owned
Cheltenham is a privately owned in-
stitution supported by a State appro
priation of $20,000 a year and payments
of $180 a year from the counties anc
city for each boy committed. Balti-
more, it was said, contributes about
$60,000 annually.
The board of directors of Chelten-
ham consists of a self-perpetuating
group of twelve persons and four oth-
ers, two appointed by the State and
two by the city.
At present, there are approximately
450 Negro boys confined there.
A bill authorizing the State to take
over control of the institution was
introduced in the Legislature in 1935
but after being passed by the Senate
it was defcated in the House of Dele-
gates. At that time, Cheltenham di.
rectors were opposed to relinquishing
control.
