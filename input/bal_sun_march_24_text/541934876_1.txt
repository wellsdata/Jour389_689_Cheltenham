COURT WARNS
DELINQUENTS
Tells Juveniles They Face
Terms In Training Schools
Judge Charles E. Moylan In the
Division of Juvenile Causes yester-!
day announced that because so
many juvenile delinquents released
on probation are becoming repeat
offenders, he will have to resort
to more frequent commitments of
such offenders to training schools.
Judge Moylan announced his new
policy after sentencing four Negro
boys, ranging in age from 8 to 12,
to the Cheltenham School for Boys.
The judge disclosed that a training
program has been developed for
younger delinquents.
Twelve Burglaries In One Month
He said the boys were involved
in twelve burglaries in one month.
Some of the offenses were com-
mitted Immediately after the boys
bad been arrested on similar
charges, but released in care of
their parents, the judge pointed
out.
One of the boys, who is 8, has
been before the Court eight times,
while a 9-year-old boy was making
his ninth court appearance, the
judge pointed out. Two 12-year-old
boys had been before the court
seven and four times, respectively.
2,293 Delinquency Cases In 1948
"The probation officers and the
presentatives of the Department
of Public Welfare recommended
that the boys be committed to the
training school, despite their youth,
as there are no alternate facilities
in the community equipped to deal
with young delinquents of this
type," the judge asserted.
"During 1948, of 2,293 delin-
quency cases, 592, or about 25 per
cent, involved youngsters under 12
years of age; ten of them were
brought to court on four separate
occasions charged with delin-
quency; eight appeared in court
on five occasions during the year,
and one of them on seven different
occasions.

Urged By Welfare Agencies
"This condition grows out of
the Court's reluctance to commit
children in this age bracket to the
training school. As a result of this
reluctance, there were practically
none of this age from Baltimore
city in the training schools of Mary-
land during 1946, 1947 and 1948."
The judge said welfare agencies
were unable to cope with the in-
crease in serious delinquencies and
have urged commitment as the only
possible alternative.
After conferences between social
agencies, welfare organizations
and the Court, a training program
has been devised in the training
schools for younger delinquents,
the judge said.
