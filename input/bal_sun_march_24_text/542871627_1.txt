REFORM SCHOOL
BACKERS LOSE
Cheltenham Site Supporters
Denied New Council Hearing
Refusing to accept disclaimers of
racial prejudice in the opposition
to the locating of Cheltenham
School for Boys near Westport in
South Baltimore, supporters of the
site yesterday requested and were
denied a new hearing on a Council
resolution condemning the State's
selection of the South Baltimore
tract.
The resolution, introduced by
Councilmen Freburger and Kot-
mair, and Councilwoman Bailey, all
of the Sixth district, was adopted
following a noisy hearing of about
500 South Baltimore residents who
indulged in frequent applause, foot-
stamping, whistles and occasional
catcalls, depending on the nature of
the testimony.
Galleries Warned
It was not until near the end of
the hearing that Councilman Bon-
nett, chairman of the Committee on
Buildings and Building Regula-
tions, warned by galleries that their
behavior was not in keeping with
procedures in Council hearings.
Selection of the site, which has
been purchased outright by the
State, was defended by Thomas B.
Sprague, president of the Chelten-
ham Board of Managers, and J.
Milton Patterson State director of
public welfare.
Joseph P. Healy, chairman of the
Governor's Commission to Study
Problems Affecting the Negroes,
urged the Council committee to
treat the question "the same as
though you were considering the
proposed location of a white insti-
tution."
"If now or later you are going
to permit training schools in the
city, don't discriminate against
this Negro institution," he said.
There were protests from the
sponsors of the resolution and from
some of the objecting citizens.
Shouting In Galleries
"If you were here," Councilmar
Freburger told Mr. Healy, "repre
senting a brick yard, you'd have no
better chance of getting this land
rezoned than Cheltenham has."
Whistles, shouts and stamps of
approval broke out in the galleries.
The Rev. Louis F. Hoffman, pas
tor of the Lakeland Presbyterian
Church, supporting the resolution
against the location of the school,
said he hated "to see the race ques-
tion injected into the discussion."
There was more applause.
But Leo V. Miller, a member of
the National Association for the Ad-
vancement of Colored People and
a member of the board of managers
lat Crownsville State College,
charged that "90 per cent of the
protestants to this location are
thinking in terms of color."
There were whistles of derision.
"The institution," he said, "could
never harm the beauty of the neigh-
borhood" because the "State is
careful of its architecture."
'Precedent Will Be Set'
Del Rasin, Negro, a member of
the Baltimore Community Youth
Centers, told the committee that
"every site in Maryland with 100
acres or more-large enough for
the proposed new Cheltenham-is
in a predominantly white area. We
will have the same opposition on all
sites. The precedent will be set
here today."
Mrs. Lillie May Jackson, presi-
dent of the Baltimore Branch of the
National Association for Colored
People, urged the committee to
"give us a chance to present the
other side.".
"I have a son in Germany and
another in Saipan because Hitler
couldn't be reasoned with," she
said. "Let us be reasonable here."
"It's going to be hard," said the
Rev. James Gray, who said he rep-
resented 165 Negro Baptist min-
sters, "to locate the school near
Baltimore because sometimes it's
lifficult even to get a house to live
n unless you white folks agree to
[it."
The proponents of the site said
they were not prepared for a com-
plete argument on the question yes-
terday. Mr. Rasin asked that a later
date to be set for the hearing. He
said he disagreed with a statement
by. Councilman Kotmair that Cher-
ry Hill Negro residents were op-
posed to locating the training
school in their vicinity.
Freburger Speaks
"I think they should be allowed
to speak for themselves," he said,
Councilman Freburger told the
committee the people of the area
are against changing any residen-
tial zones in the Sixth district "re-
gardless of the purpose."
"I am not satisfied," he said, "for
any State or Federal body or any
other to come into the corporate
limits of Baltimore city and set up
an institution that violates the code
of Baltimore city in respect to zon-
ing. If this thing is allowed, we may
as well throw the Commission on
City-Plan, with all its plans, out the
window."
"We'll fight to the last ditch
against it," said Councilman Kot-
mair.
Emmett Batson, a resident of the
district, said the location of the
institution on the 115 acres of open
land "eliminates the possibility of
$1,500,000 in taxable basis."
James K. Cullen, United States.
Commissioner, told the committee:
"I can't imagine anyone interested
in the development of Baltimore
city taking any position other than
that Cheltenham should be located
in a rural district."
Meade At Hearing
Hugh H. Meade, an Assistant
Attorney General, representing the
Board of Public Works and the
Cheltenham board, said he was
"not here to oppose the resolution."
"That is entirely a matter for
the Council and then it will be up
to the Board of Public Works to
decide what action it will take."
Mr. Sprague said the site was
selected because it "is close to the
city of Baltimore and has all trans-
portation and utility facilities."
He said an act of the Legislature
requires the moving of the insti-
tution for delinquent boys from its
present location in Prince Georges
county. The existing institution will
be used for mentally deficient boys.
Councilman Kotmair asked Mr.
Sprague. how many boys escaped
from. Cheltenham in the past two
years.
"Some place it as high as 700,"
he replied. "There has been only
one escape so far this year. There
were 31 in December."
