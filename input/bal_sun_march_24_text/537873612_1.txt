BYRD IS NAMED
TO BOARD OF
CHELTENHAM
Nice Appoints 4 Others
Besides President Of
U. Of Md.
Governor Holds Parley In
Effort To Iron Out Re-
cent Difficulties
To the multifarious duties of Dr.
H. C. Byrd, president of the Univer-
sity of Maryland and potent yet myş-
terious figure in the State Democratic
political picture, another was added
yesterday.
His name was placed by Governor
Nice on the roster of the turbulent
board of managers of the Cheltenham
School for Boys.
Dr. Byrd's appointment, with four
others, to membership on the board
was announced by the Governor short-
ly before a conference was held at the
Government House in Annapolis in an
effort to solve the difficulties that have
arisen since the Negro reform school
was taken over by the State.
The five appointees fill vacancies
caused by expired terms on the six-
teen-member board, of which the Gov-
ernor is ex officio a member.
Four Other Appointees
The others named by the Governor
are:
JOHN L. BERRY, Negro, graduate of
Howard University, former teacher
at the Douglass High School in
Baltimore, former member of the
grand jury and active in civic af-
fairs of his race.
THE REV. ALBERT J. MITCHELL, Negro,
graduate of Clark University and
Gammon Theological Seminary,
superintendent of the North Balti-
more district of the Afro Methodist
Episcopal Church and active in the
raising of funds for Morgan College
and other Negro educational insti-
tutions.
MISE IDA R. CUMMINGS, Negro, Mor-
gan College graduate, sister of the
late City Councilman, Harry S.
Cummings, and a retired school-
teacher.
MISS BERTHA W. Pпостов, Negro pub-
lie school teacher, president of the
women's auxiliary of Morgan Col-
lege and a social worker.
Present At Conference
Those who Inter conferred with the
Governor on the squabble between the
State and the board of managers were
Harry C. Jones, State Employment
Commissioner; M. Hampton Magruder,
Collector of Internal Revenue and a
member of the board; Joseph H.
Blandford, president of the board, and
Howard C. Hill, member.
Sitting with them also were Morris
A. Rome, member of the Board of
State Aid and Charities, and W. H.
Blakeman, State Budget Director.
A score of employes of the board at
the reform institution were pro-
nounced unfit for such employment by
the State Employment Commissioner
and when the State withheld pay to
Lorce their elimination, with support
of a majority of the board, mandamus
proceedings were instituted to obtain
the funds.
Nice Gives Ultimatum
The Governor stepped in with an
ultimatum to the board to accept plans
for reorganization, offer an acceptable
substitute plan or resign.
They declined to resign, but Mr. Ma-
gruder, representing the board, and
the State Employment Commissioner
were named at yesterday's conference
as a committee to seek a suitable com-
promise solution.
The Governor said the conferees had
"an agreeable talk," but that he had
stood on his request for a reorganiza-
tion or for resignations of a majority
of the board, and this group had stood
on its denial.
"Some Progress" Reported
However, he added that he believed
"some progress" had been made.
The five new members on the board
will not affect its attitude, the Gov-
ernor added, since the five who retired
were aligned against the majority in
opposition to the State's demands.
