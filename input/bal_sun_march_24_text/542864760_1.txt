Need A Ladder?
David Hobbs, 31, was locked out
of his room at 32 North Chester
street by his landlady, so he went
in quest of a ladder with which to
get up to the second floor. Not a
man to avoid the easiest way out of
a predicament, Hobbs pulled a fire
alarm from the corner of Fair-
mount avenue and Chester street.
When firemen arrived and found
no fire, Battalion Chief W. S. Leid-
lich saw Hobbs standing near the
box. Questioned, Hobbs told the
fire chief of being locked out and
said that he wanted to "borrow"
a ladder from the Fire Department
so he could get up to the second
floor of the house.
The chief informed Hobbs that
no ladders were available, but that
alternate quarters at the city's ex-
pense could be furnished. Hobbs
was taken to the station house and
lodged in a cell. The next day he
was charged with sounding a false
fire alarm.

Baltimorc, Md., Sunday, October 28, 1945
Navy personnel, from seamen
first class, to four-stripers, and their
families invaded the town last week
as thousands arrived for the Navy
Day celebration. Downtown Balti-
more took on the traditional Sat-
urday night air of hustle and bustle
as the lads in the bell-bottomed
trousers rushed for eating places,
night spots, movie houses, roller
skating rinks, bowling alleys, bur-
lesque houses, photograph gal-
leries, penny arcades, and all of the
so-called playhouses that attract
the gob out for an evening of
amusement.

Most of the men were from the
eleven vessels that arrived on Wed-
nesday and Thursday for the big
day, yesterday. The huge carrier
Randolph, the heavy cruiser Can,-
berra, three destroyers, one de-
stroyer escort, four submarines and
one APD boat described as a light-
attack transport steamed into har-
bor and cleared the decks for the
public inspection on Friday, yes-
terday and today.
The carrier Randolph's pinup
girl, Joan Lilley, a Baltimore girl,
was formally introduced to the
ship's crew last night at a ban-
quet. The week end was a busy
one for her, as she kept a date with
3,000 doting gobs at the Navy
League's Saturday night's formal
ship's party and at a special pinup
dance.

Navy Day itself started off with
a public award ceremony at which
Commander Slade Cutter, former
football star at the Naval Academy
and now commanding officer of the
submarine Requin, preserited med-
als and letters of commendation
to thirteen crew members from the
submarines Requin and Flying
Fish.
A colorful parade from the 5th
Regiment Armory to Memorial
Plaza via Howard and Baltimore
streets followed the award cere-
mony.

self-styled "phantom burglar."
Setting an all-time record for
the number of charges made
against any one defendant at any
one time, Maenhoudt is being held
in $53,000 bail which actually re-
quires the posting of more than
$100,000 in property.

against Maenhoudt were on posts
he was working as a patrolman

show that in some he himself took
the reports on them, interviewing
the victims and ostensibly aiding
in the investigations.

Skeletons Crop Up
The remains of eight bodies, ap-
parently from old Colonial "pot-
ter's field," have been found thus
far in Frederick, Md., by city
workmen installing a sanitary
sewer in Abrecht place between
Carroll parkway and West Patrick
street. In only a few cases
has there been much left of the
wooden caskets in which the bodies
were buried, evidently before the
turn of the Eighteenth Century.
Occasionally a skull or section of a
skull rolls out into the ditch, but
in a number of cases the bones
have crumbled into dust.
Workmen are not enjoying the
Job. One of them remarked: "I've
had enough of this."

Stadium News
The plan for the $5,000,000 me-
morial stadium was submitted to
the City Council Monday. The proj-
ect would provide all sorts of activi-
ties, ranging from football and
swimming meets to religious ex-
ercises and political conventions,
as well as a 100,000-person seating
capacity and an aluminum-alloy
roof supported by air pressure.
The plan was submitted by the
Baltimore War Memorial Stadium
Committee, of which Charles P.
McCormick is the chairman. A
brochure that accompanied the
plan explained it was "conceived
and developed by Mr. Glenn L.
Martin, chairman of the research
and survey committee, Stadium
Committee, and his assistant, Mr.
Jan Porel, who was called upon to
act as an expert consultant."
It explained that the stadium
could be used for a circus,, base-
ball, football, music festivals,
political conventions, prize fights,
rodeos, beauty contests, religious
services, ice carnvals, automobile
shows, ice hockey and other events.
There also would be a trophy room,
a gymnasium, a youth activities
meeting room, handball courts, a
rollar-skating rink, a room for band
practice, a pistol range, a cafeteria,
a little theater, a swimming pool, a
bowling alley, a lost-and-found de-
partment, two lounges, a grand con-
course, ticket and information
booths, a first-aid station, guide
headquarters, a gift shop, tele-
graph office, flower shop; bus, air-
line and taxicab office, a coffee
shop and a sports equipment shop.
