In Survey
Censuring the health standards,
diet, dormitories, clothing and vir-
tually all other living conditions at
the House of Reformation for Colored
Boys at Cheltenham, the Child Wel-
fare League of America, Inc., has
recommended that the State take it
over.
The league's report on the institu-
tion, based on a study made last
year, came to light last week in con-
nection with a petition filed in court
for the release of one of its inmates.
Executive director of the league,
which has offices at 130 East Twenty-
second street, New York city, is C. C.
Carstens. The secretary is Kenneth
L. Messenger.
Criticized By Grand Juries
Frequently criticized by grand juries
and other investigating bodies, the
institution most recently came under
fire in the report of the retiring Sep-
tember term grand jury, and Gover-
nor Nice subsequently reiterated his
campaign assertion that the State
should take charge of the reformatory.
The Child Welfare League's report
cited health problems equaled by
"few, if any, institutions for children
in this country"; termed the diet
"seriously inadequate"; called dormi-
tory conditions "hazardous" and
otherwise assailed the institution's
facilities and methods.
Referring to health conditions, the
child welfare report said:
"Few, if any, institutions for chil-
dren in this country have so many se-
rious health problems and so little of
the health service needed for meeting
these problems."
Tuberculosis Cited
There is an obvious presence of
Huberculosis at the school, inadequate
diet and an absence of routine health
examinations, according to the report,
which continues:
"There is no recognition of the spe-
cial nutritional needs of boys under-
weight. Those feeding the children
have not been informed of those un-
derweight, anæmic or otherwise in
need of special attention. Even for
boys in fairly good health the diet is
seriously inadequate.
"It has been customary to separate
the milk and sell nearly all the cream.
The boys have only the milk which
has passed through the separator. The
boys receive no butter to make up
For this deficiency.
"The vegetables used most are po-
catoes, beans and others which are
bulky. There is some use of green
vegetables, but not nearly enough in
proportion to the bread, cereal and
starchy vegetables. There also is too
much reliance upon tea and coffee as
Deverages."
Segregation Urged
The report urged the segregation of
boys with tuberculosis and feeble-
ninded boys and suggested for the
Latter group the establishment of farm
colonies, each of which would pro-
vide residence and training for a group
ranging from twelve to twenty in
number.
As the staff of the institution deals
with delinquency, feeble-mindedness,
Hisease, serious neglect and other
problems, "it is obvious that the House
of Reformation has been filling some-

Larger Medical Staff Urged
A larger medical staff was urged,
with a physician in half-time attend-
ance, at least, and a dentist in full-
time attendance. The report asserted
the services of a psychiatrist were es-
sential and that nurses should be ob-
tained and the infirmary enlarged.
The survey of the institution in ref-
erence to living conditions said:
"In dormitories conditions are haz-
ardous. Too many boys are in each
room, the ventilation, for which there
are plenty of windows, is too limited
because the bedding is unsuitable and
Insufficient, and, therefore, windows
are kept closed more than is desirable.
Cross infection is inescapable in such
quarters, especially when respiratory
diseases are present.
Life In Danger From Fire
"The lack of suitable exits and fire
escapes places life in danger from fire.
The wooden ladder type escapes
(nailed to the outside of the buildings)
are unsuitable. Moreover, it has been
customary to nail shut the windows
leading to these escapes."
The clothing worn by the boys is
about as meager as it could be, the
report continued, pointing out that
the expenditure for clothing was about
$7.6% per capita a year, as compared
with $20 to $30 per capital per annum
at other extremely economical insti-
tutions.
In reference to education and work.
the report showed there were almost
no provisions for teaching boys voca-
tions which they could follow after
leaving Cheltenham.
Broom Shop Demoralizing
The broom shop, it Laid, was de-
moralizing rather than educational.
and its operation was accompanied
with serious health hazards.
"The piece-work which is done by
most of the boys in this shop," the
report asserts. "requires too rigid
a
work discipline at monotonous
tasks ... a binder may earn 10 cents
a dozen for any brooms bound in
excess of his daily quota of thirty-
nine. Boys can earn 10 or 20 cents
a day, and some have earned a little
more than this.
"Whenever possible, heads of de-
partments should be employed who
have some ability as teachers. It is
probable that farming and dairying
always should form the largest part
of the vocational training efforts of
the institution."
Punishment Discussed
Concerning punishment of the boys
at the school, the report said much
of it seemed designed to prevent es-
capes, but, nevertheless, 112 boys
Lescaped in 1933.
"The conventional punishment for
escaping consists of thirty days con-
finement in a cell with a diet of bread
and water, supplemented by soup or
stew once or twice a week," accord-
ing to the report. "The cells are used
also in punishment for other offenses.
"The other severe form of punish-
ment consists of whipping.
times as many as twenty lashes are
given."
