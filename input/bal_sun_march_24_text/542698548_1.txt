| Judge Charles E. Moylan. presid-
ing in the Division for Juvenile
Causes, yesterday lodged a com-
plaint with Hamilton R. Atkinson,
police commissioner, that two 14-
year-old Negro boys had been
beaten with nightsticks by police
Thursday night after the youths
were arrested in connection with an
attempted burglary.
Judge Moylan in a telephone con-
versation also complained that the
youths, after being held at the
Northeastern Police Station over-
night, had not been given any food
and had been sent to the Court-
house without breakfast.
"We have just broken up cruelty
down at Cheltenham," the judge
asserted, "and I don't expect to put
up with cruelty cropping up in the
Baltimore police force.
Judge Notices Wound
"Im here to co-operate in keep-
ing down crime, but I don't like to
see cruelty to children."
The two boys had been before
the court for attempting to break
into a store in the 1800 block Ash-
land avenue, which is within a
short distance of the police station.
The youths had been sentenced
to Cheltenham School for Boys and
were leaving the courtroom when
Judge Moylan noticed that one of
the boys had an open wound on
his forehead and the other had a
wound and small cut on the side of
his head.
Made No Mention During Trial
The youths had made no mention
of police mistreatment during their
trial, Judge Moylan said. On fur-
ther questioning by the judge, the
boys said they had been beaten by
oflicers who arrested them as they
were hiding under steps near the
store.
Judge Moylan then called Patrol.
man Gordon Beere, one of the ar-
resting patrolmen, back to the trial
chambers and queried him about
the marks on the boys' heads.
The policeman said there had
been no fight and the arrest was
made without difficulty. He ex.
pressed the opinion that one of the

Wound On Forehead
The boy with the forehead wound
identified Patrolman Beere as the
policeman who beat him, while the
other youth said another patrolman
beat him.
"Well, I believe the boys," Judge
Moylan declared after the question-
ing had concluded. He then pointed
out that the boys had not com-
plained of mistreatment during the
trial and made the accusation only
after he noticed the wounds.
"These are just 14-year - old
boys," the judge told the patrol-
man. "You couldn't mistake them
for older persons. The policemen
had thrown flashlights on them
before they pulled them from their
Thiding place. The police admit
there was no struggle. This is a
case of a mistake of not using
brains, but using brute force and
nightsticks instead."
