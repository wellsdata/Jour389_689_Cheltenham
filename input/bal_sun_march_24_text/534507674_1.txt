Reformation of Colored Children-A
Visit to the House at Cheltenham. Tester-
day a number of citizens, Including Gov. Ham-
ilton and Mayor Latrobe, at the invitation of
Mr. Enoch Pratt, president, visited the House
of Reformation and Instruction for Colored
Children, located at Cheltenham, Prince
George's county, about 42 miles from Balti-
more, on the Pope's Creek Branch of the Balti-
more and Potomac ralkroad. A special train
left Calvert Station at 9.30 A. M., having on
board Mr. Enoth Pratt, president of the board
of managers; E. Stabler, Jr., secretary; W. E.
Woodyear, treasurer; Cyrus Blackburn, Alex.
P. Hill, B. F. Deford, Charles Markoll, Wm. S.
Whiteley and Charles T. Boehm, of the board
of managers; Messrs. Samuel M. Price, of Bal-
timore county; C. C. Magruder, Jr., of Prince
George's county; Edwin Blackburn and W. F.
Weber, of Baltimore city. Gov. Hamilton joined
the party at Marlboro' Station, he having taken
an early train from Annapolis. The visitors
were met at the station by Gen. John W. Horn,
superintendent, who had provided vehicles to
transport them up to the house, which is dis-
tant about threo-quarters of a mile. On ac-
count of the visit the inmates had been given a
holiday, and they were apparently enjoying
the bright sunshino in strolling about the spa-
cious grounds. Shortly after entering the build-
ing Mr. Pratt conducted the Governor, Mayor
and others to the chapel, in the upper story,
where a brass band, consisting of 13 inmatos, en-
tertained the visitors with martial music and
popular airs. The main building, as well as
the four family buildings, were thoroughly in-
spected, Governor Hamilton and Mayor Latrobe
expressing gratification at the constantly recur-
ring evidences of strict discipline, neatness and
order everywhere observable. The kitchen,
laundry and workshops were all visited, and
called forth the unanimous verdict that the
inmates were better cared for than nine-tenths of
their fellows outside of the institution. At pres-
ent there are 211 boys in the House, their ages
ranging from 8 to 20 years. There is plenty of
work found for them on the farm, (of 700 acres,)
in the workshops and domestic employment
about the House. Everything consumed by the
boys, except meats, is raised upon the place.
This year 70,000 heads of cabbage and 2,500
bushels of potatoes were parts of the crop.
Shoes and clothing, including caps worn by
the inmates, are manufactured by them, while
a large number are employed in caning chairs
for an establishment in Baltimore. Some of
the boys have made considerable progress in
studying in the schools, where the rudimentary
branches are taught by faithful teachers.
Specimens of writing were exhibited that
would do credit to white boys of similar age.
Governor Hamilton was much interested in the
farming operations, and in company with
Mr. Pratt and others inspected the cows, calves,
lambs and swine. At noon the bell sounded
the dinner call, when the boys occupying the
family buildings formed in companies and
marched in columns of twos to the main build-
ing and into the dining-hall, keeping step to
the cry of "hep, hep," from one of the larger
boys in each squad. Filing in, each boy took
his place at the table, and when all the tables
were surrounded heads were reverently bowed
while grace was said by all in unison, At a
signal all seated and began the meal, which
consisted of stewed meat and potatoes and
large slices of wholesome bread. In less than
two minutes scores of plates were emptied and
raised above the head of each voracious con-
sumer, signifying that, like Oliver Twist, they
wanted more. And more they received, some
of them getting as many as four large plate-
fuls of the eavory food.
Mr. W. F. Weber, architect of the buildings,
procured photographic views of them, also a
group picture of the visitors standing on the
front steps of the main building. After this
the company dined at the expense of the man-
agers of the institution. The special train re-
turned to the city shortly after 6 o'clock, bring-
ing all back safely. Gov. Hamilton returned to
Annapolis, leaving the special train at Odenton.

The Maryland State Grange commenced
its annual session yesterday at Raine's Hall.
H. O. Devries, of Howard county, is master, and
E. Hall of B., of Anne Arundel county, is sec-
retary. The membership comprises the mas
ters of the local granges throughout the State
and their wives, when they are members of the
Order of Patrons of Husbandry. Past mom-
bers and their wives are also members and
eligible to office, but have no vote. Most of the
members present yesterday, numbering about
100, from nearly all the counties of the State,
were accompanied by their wives. Among those
prosent were G. T. Moore, F. A. Tscheffely, John
McDonald, George R. Hays, of Montgomery
county; John W. Corey, Kent county; George
W. Smith, John S. Lakin, Frederick; Thomas
F. Shepherd and J. N. Chiswell, Carroil; George
Thomas, St. Mary's; James T. Earle and Dr. A.
E. Sudler, Queen Anne's; George Brooke, Н. М.
Murray, J. Baldwin, Anne Arundel; Daniel
Jenifer, Wm. B. Sands, George H. Merryman,
George H. Elder, S. C. Heird, Baltimore county:
Ferd. Pue and Jos. Barlow, Howard; J. W.
Knotts, Caroline; J. C. Bartell, Talbot; James
Pentland, Baltimore city; J. Willison, Allegany;
Thomas H. Bond. St. Mary's. The session was
mainly occupied in the reading of reports from
officers, the appointment of committees and
other routine work. The master's annual ad-
dress showed the order in the State to be in
good condition, both as to members, financial
condition and moral influence. The erange
will meet again to-day, and will probably con-
tinue in session throughout to-morrow. Among
the subjects likely to come up during the ses-
sion will be the feasibility of establishing a
general State produce exchange.
