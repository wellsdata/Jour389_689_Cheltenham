FORCES IN FULL CONTROL
Patronage Dispensed
Under Direction Of Gov-

No Change In Presiding
Officers; O'Conor Gives
Biennial Report
By LOUIS J. O'DONNELL
[Annapolis Bureau of The Sun]
Annapolis, Jan. 1--Encountering not
the slightest ripple of opposition, a
smooth-clicking administration ma-
chine carried the General Assembly
tonight over the opening-day hurdles
of its ninety-day biennial session.
Patronage allotments were signed,
and sealed in Governor O'Conor's office
and delivered to the Democratic caucus
for ratification only a few hours before
the executive mounted the rostrum in
the House of Delegates chamber to
give an accounting of his two усага
in office.
As for the presiding officers of the
Senate and the House, no change was
expected.
And there was none; Senator
Arthur H. Brice (Dem., Kent) retained
the Presidency of the Senate, and
Thomas E. Conlon (Dem., Fifth, Bal-
timore) again will preside in the
House.

Nothing Startling
There were a few indications-such
as in the appointment of Frank F. J.
Daily, long a follower of Mayor
Jackson, as chief clerk in the House
of
a further consolidation of the
O'Conor political forces but otherwise
nothing startling appeared on the sur-
face in the apportionment of jobs.
The list forwarded from the execu-
tive office and rubber-stamped by the
caucus was identified mainly, as the
handiwork of the Governor, Howard
Bruce, his political mentor; Clarence
W. Miles, his legislative field marshal,
and William Curran,
E. Brooke Lee Unnoticed
Nowhere at least openly-did Dele-
gate E. Brooke Lee (Dem., Montgom-
ery), former Speaker, Montgomery
county boss and generalissimo in the
Ritchie administrations, show up in
today's preliminary organization of the
Assembly.
He's back in the House
in place of a member who resigned.
So far as could be learned, he is not
slated to cut a big figure if the ad-
ministration has its way, but he was
being pointed out to those who are
comparatively strangers to the State
House corridors as the man who can
upset the best of apple carts if he has
a mind to do so.
The Governor didn't burden the
legislators-many of them still holi-
day-minded-with a recitation in ita
entirety of his 24,000-word report on
the operation of the State government
since he took over on January 11,
1939,
Only Hits High Spots
Instead, he skimmed through the
lengthy document and spent forty-
five minutes hitting its high spots,
emphasing the need for legislation
that will mesh the State's activities
with the national defense program.
Without making specific recom-
mendations, he pointed out that with-
in the State's borders are many im-
portant defense manufacturing and
armament plants for which the tracks
must be greased and around which
safeguards must be thrown.
The Governor appeared before the
legislators shortly before 9.30 P. M.
after both Houses had been through
the formalities of installing presiding
officers and heard their brief speeches
of acceptance.
Ten minutes after the Governor had
finished speaking, the State House was
almost deserted.

Adjourn Till 11 A. Μ.
Both Houses had been adjourned
until 11 o'clock tomorrow morning, at
which time the bills prepared by the
Legislative Council will be presented.
The Assembly will adjourn tomorrow
afternoon until Tuesday night, accord.
ing to current plans, at which time
committee assignments will be com-
pleted.
The Governor in his message indi-
rectly warned the legislators that the
administration-and the public-ex-
pects the Assembly to get down to
work without the usual dilly-dallying.
He reminded them that the State
Treasury's figures show that Maryland
is in the soundest financial position it
has ever enjoyed-with balance as
of September 30, 1940, of $6,151,955-and
that he will recommend that this good
fortune be passed on to the taxpayers.
In fact, he told the legislators that
he has practically completed the de-
tails of the fiscal recommendations he
will pass on to them with his proposed
(Continued on Page 4, Column 6)
(Continued from Page 1)
budget for 1942-43 and that they can
expect this program next week.
This early preparation of the budget,
the Governor advised the legislators,
is his contribution to a general effort
that should be made to forestall the
usual closing-hour rush.
Road Plan Is Ready
Affairs of the State Roads Commis-
sion are in such shape, the Governor
said, that he is prepared to put be-
fore the Assembly a most ambitious
highway program-an accomplishment
made possible by the fact that no gaso-
line-tax funds have been diverted by
his administration and none will be
diverted.
It is up to the Legislature, he added,
to forge a new link in State trans-
portation by ratifying the purchase of
the Claiborne Annapolis ferry-a
move that will mean lower rates.
In the first year of his administra-
tion, the Governor reported, 110.18
miles of road was built and, during
this year, 87.68 miles are under con-
struction or contract.
The Governor's summary of opera-
tions of various institutions was de-
tailed, designed primarily as a hand-
book for the legislators.
Projected Plans
In the case of some, however, the
Governor projected the plans of the
administration for improvement or
expansion.
For example, in the case of the
Cheltenham School for Boys,
the
State's institutional sore spot for years,
the Governor said that the fact that
further improvement in facilities and
operation is imperative must be faced
squarely by the administration and the
Legislature..
To meet this need, he informed the
Assembly, he has made a generous
provision in his budget.
He reviewed the progress of the
State Health Department; expansion
of hospitals for tuberculosis and
mentally afflicted and indicated that
appropriations for such purposes
would be increased rather than held
down in his forthcoming budget.
On the subject of conserving the
State's natural resources, the Governor
spoke briefly of the fact that the ad-
ministration's plan for a separate com-
mercial fishery commission was de-
feated at the polls in November and
then issued a plea for considered and
unbiased action on the problem during
this session.
Dismisses Sectionalism
In reaching a solution, the Governor
asked the legislators to "lay aside pre-
conceived notions and any sectional or
political viewpoints and be guided by
what is best for the future of the
State."
Maryland has been fortunate, the
Governor said in connection with the
State welfare program, in that ex-
penditures are being handled "capably
and properly" to the end that, as com.
pared with other States, the cost here
is relatively light.
He indicated that his recommenda-
tions on welfare appropriations must
await the fiscal proposals that will ac-
company his budget.
Praises Magistrate System
The trial magistrate syster that was
created by the legislators at the 1939
session, the Governor told them, has
worked so well that it can be said in
no two-year period heretofore have
such rapid advances been made in the
administration of justice in Maryland.
Dr. H. C. Byrd, president of the
University of Maryland, was paid ex-
ceptional tribute by the Governor in
his reference to that institution.
"Under the able leadership of Dr.
Byrd," the Governor's report asserted,
"the University of Maryland, and all
its related activities are being effici-
ently conducted and are offering
services of great value to the State."
"Very Noteworthy"
"Very noteworthy," said the Gov-
ernor, is the growth of the physical
plant at the university-a growth made
possible mainly by Dr. Byrd's per-
suative work with Federal fund-
dispensing agencies in Washington.
However, the Governor added that
"the educational advancement of the
university has keep pace with the ad-
vancement in physical facilities."
Fiscal aspects, often the subject
of great controversy in the past, of
the university were left for the budget
message.
In keeping with the new demands
That will be made on State agencies
by the national defense program, the
Governor informed the Assembly that
the Public Service Commission has
approved expansion plans of the pub-
lic utilities that will provide adequate
service and he revealed plans to train
members of the State police force for
specialized duties.
To Increase Police
Additions to the force that he will
recommend-and provide for in the
budget-the Governor said, will make
possible this special training of the
State policemen and increase their
usefulness as well as make more of
them available for patroling the high-
vays.
It was indicated, in his remarks on
The unemployment compensation di-
ision, that the Governor plans to ask
The Legislature to make changes in
The law that will simplyfy its adminis-
ration and relieve employers of some
f the mass of detail now necessary in
he keeping of records.
Reminds Membership
Paving the way for the approximate-
seventy measures that the Legisla-
ive Council will lay before the As-
embly as a whole with its recommen-
Lation for adoption, the Governor re-
minded the general membership of the
Legislature that the council had not
usurped any of the rights of the
legislators as a body.
The Governor said he desired to
"lay emphasis" on the fact that the
bills the council will persent are open
to the same discussion as any other
measure that may come before the
Assembly.
Cites Constitution
"The elected representatives of the
people, including every member of
the Senate and every member of the
House of Delegates," he said, "are the
legislative branch of the government.
"The Constitution provides for that,
and no provision by law (such as
creation of the council or otherwise
can deprive the members of their
rights.
"Accordingly, it will be the entire
membership of the General Assembly
that the public will look for final
decisions on all the matters under
consideration.
That the Senate and the
House will meet their responsibilities
I have no doubt."
