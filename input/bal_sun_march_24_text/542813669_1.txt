LANE ATTACKED
FOR FOOD CUTS
ssembly Told Of Needs In
State Youth Institutions
By ALBERT W. QUINN
[Annapolis Bureau of The Sun]
Annapolis, Feb. 27-Criticism of
Governor Lane's cuts in requests
for food allowances at two of the
State's training schools featured to-
day's joint session of House and
Senate committees for the study of
the 1948-1949 biennial budget.
E. L. Fletcher, superintendent of
the Maryland Training School for
Boys, also complained about the de-
Metion of a proposed new job-su-
pervisor of recreation at $1,900 for
the fiscal year of 1948 and $1,995
for 1949.
Mr. Fletcher said his school is the
only one of its kind in the State
without a recreation supervisor.
and that such an officer is needed
for the proper rehabilitation of the
boys committed to the institution.
Present recreation, he said, is
"makeshift," and supervised by
various teachers and others on their
own time.
Says Food Is Important Item
But food, he said, is the most im-
portant of all items for growing
hoys. He said his request would
have provided for 75 cents a day
for the feeding of the inmates of
the school-a figure established
some time ago by a dietician of the
State Health Department. A later
figure, he said, is 81 cents. The
Governor cut the item, Mr. Fletch-
er stated, to a figure that would
provide only 56 cents a day per
boy.
The total request for food in the
Maryland Training School budget
was for $92,500 for each year of the
biennium. The budget as submitted
to the Legislature calls for $55,700
for each year.
For the present biennium, the
appropriations were $49,275 for
1946 and $37.625 for 1947.
Weekly Food Needs Given
Mr. Fletcher said the needs for
the weekly feeding of each boy in
such an institution as his are:
Seven quarts of milk, four pounds
of potatoes, one half pound of
beans, two pounds of citrus fruit,
three pounds of yellow vegetables,
four pounds of all other vegetables,
two and one half pounds of meat,
poultry or fish, seven eggs, five
pounds of break cake, one and one
half pounds of fats and oils, three
fourths of a pound of sugar-at a
total cost today of about $5.70.
His figures of fiscal needs to pro-
vide the food, he said, take into
consideration the production on the
school's farm.
Addressing the joint committee,
Mr. Fletcher said: "In making that
cut, you have absolutely not studied
the food needs of boys."
"If you have been cut," replied
Senator Robert B. Kimble (R., Al-
legany), "you were cut by the Gov-
ernor, not by the committee."
21-Cents-A-Day Allowance
The entire requests for the school
were $430,463 for 1948 and $429,774
for 1949. The Governor reduced
these to $357,263 and $358,579.
Mrs. Muriel Johnson, superin-
tendent of the Maryland Training
School for Colored Girls, said when
she took over the institution a
year ago the food allowance was 21
cents a day, the girls had one dress
apiece and the school was nothing
more than a custodial institution.
- She requested $25,000 for each
year of the next biennium for food,
- which the Governor cut to $21,900
for each year. The appropriations
for the current biennium were
$10,898 for 1946 and $10,000 for
1947. Mrs. Johnson, however, said
she had to go to the Board of Public
-Works on several occasions for ad-
ditional funds in all branches of
maintenance. For 1947, to date, she
said, the school has received $38,-
000 additional.
Satisfied With Funds
The entire requests for the
school were $159,377 for 1948 and
$127,003 for 1949. They were cut
to $118,571 and $119,311,
Mrs. Edith P. Gardner, superin-
tendent of the Montrose School
for Girls, said the food allowance
of $15,000 a year about 46 cents
a day for each girl-is "quite satis-
factory. She pointed out, however,
that the school has a "very good
farm." Her appropriations for 1948
and 1949 are $153,578 and $151,163,
compared with requests of $168,-
297 and $163.702.
II. R. Harris, superintendent of
Cheltenham School for Boys, said
the entire allowance in the Gov-
ernor's budget of $53,965 for 1948
and $56,066 for 1949 is necessary
if the school is to be more than a
place of detention. He had re-
quested $61,585 for 1948 and
$62,166 for 1949.
Dwight Ferguson, director of
the Bureau of Child Welfare, said
the Governor's supplemental budg-
et will propose $190,000 for a
State-wide program of juvenile de-
tention. Because of a lack of such
facilities, he said, holding of juve-
nile delinquents in jail, pending
commitment to an institution, is a
"common practice"
