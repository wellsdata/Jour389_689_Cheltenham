Warned To Keep Silent
The next day Lucas again came to
the Ashland avenue home of one of
the Negro youths, showed them the
pistol he had displayed the night be-
fore and said he had shot a man early
that morning. After warning them to
keep silent about the matter, he left
the house, they asserted.
In the meantime police were work-
ing on the theory that Brilliant had
been shot accidentally, probably by
one of a group of Negroes engaged in
an argument. John T. Beatty, the cab
driver who took Brilliant to the hos-
pital, declared Brilliant had muttered,
"I've been shot three times by a
Negro."
At the hospital, however, it was
found only one bullet had struck the
youth, piercing his chest. Residents in
the neighborhood, however, reported
having heard three shots.
Friends Tell Of Threat
Thursday night, Lucas' friends said,
the Negro youth came to one of them
and threatened him because he "knew
too much." Yesterday afternoon the
youths decided to go to Capt. John
R. Scheuler of the Northeastern po-
lice district, and tell him their story.
Captain Scheuler immediately sent
a squad of officers to Lucas' home, but
the youth was not there. Later he was
arrested at the home of friends in the
1600 block of Milliman street. Still
later police searched Lucas' home
and found the pistol secreted in the
mattress.
Admits Owning Gun
Lucas, who Captain Scheuler said
had been an inmate of Cheltenham
several times, refused to admit the
shooting, despite several hours of
questioning by police. He did admit
ownership of the gun, however. Police
said they would have the weapon ex-
amined by ballistics experts today in
order that its markings may be com-
pared with the bullet removed from
Brilliant's body.
Brilliant graduated from Baltimore
City College in 1929, and was said to
be the sole support of a widowed
mother, five brothers and a sister. He
was employed as an accountant by an
oil company and lived at 2236 Orleans
street.
