Maryland and Baltimore city are
ready to go ahead with 98 public
works projects, at an estimated
cost of $16,404,474, on the day after
materials are released at the close
of the war with Japan, Henry P
Irr, chairman of the Maryland
Commission on Postwar Recon
struction and Development, an
nounced yesterday.
Bids could be advertised today
on these projects, for which plans
and specifications have been drawn
and approved, he said. They are
part of a total of 655 projects over
the State in varying stages of con-
sideration and preparation. involv-
ing an estimated expenditure of
$212,171,356, the chairman said.
Of the plans completed and
ready to be put into operation im-
mediately, twenty involve capital
state improvements, estimated at
$1,298,354; seventeen are under the
State Roads Commission, estimat-
ed at $7,894,730; 39 are sponsored
by the counties and municipalities
at an estimated cost of $1:687,090;
21 are Baltimore city projects esti-
mated at $4,589,200, and one is a
sanitary district plan estimated at
$25,000..
196 Being Completed
Mr. Irr said that an additional
196 plans, involving the spending
of $81,795,686, are now in process
of completion. Proposals have been
made, but no plans drawn, for 361
more projects to cost an estimated
$113,971,299, he said.
Among the capital state improve-
ments which can be started as soon
"as the green light is given" are an
$844,400 addition to Spring Grove
State Hospital; a $377,194 improve-
ment at the Cheltenham School for
Boys; a $20,000 piggery for the
Maryland State Penal Farm; a
$119,200 addition to the Mary-
land Training School for Boys at
Loch Raven; additions to Morgan
State College valued at $428,000,
and a $258,000 addition to the
Maryland Tuberculosis Sanatorium
at Sabillasville.
