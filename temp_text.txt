Enoch Pratt Library Case TO THE EDITOR OF THE SUN-Sir: May
I challenge the closing paragraphs of your edi- torial,. "A
New Challenge for Negroes," in which, in, regard to the
recent court decision admitting qualified colored
applicants to the Enoch Pratt Free Library training course,
you warn: "Now that the court ruling removes this racial
distinction they must not expect special consideration
because they are Negrocs must win their way on indi- vidual
merit." . Anyone familiar with the strenuous efforts which
colored citizens have had to make to gain admission to the
University of Maryland, which is supported by their taxes,
to be ac- cepted for employment on the police force, to be
employed as social workers, knows that there is no danger
of any colored citizen re- ceiving special consideration
because of his race. To an observer with a simple sense of
justice it is clear that the colored people have never
expected special consideration, only equal treatment. ...
As a practical excursion into the realities of the color
question, have your writers inter- view just a few of the
hundreds of skilled, business, and professional job
applicants among young colored citizens and listen to their
experiences. A colored citizen always has to be more
skilled, better qualified, able to - double the
qualifications_of a white citizen, to get a job, and then
in the majority of cases he is barred simply because of his
race.... JUANITA MITCHELL, Director, Citizens Committee for
Justice. Baltimore, Oct. 22. Palestine And The Future TO
THE EDITOR OF THE SUN-Sir: Allow me to thank you for the
very interesting and de- tailed reports by Faul W. Ward, of
your Wash- ington Bureau, about Palestine. To me, and
thousands of other readers, Palestine and its future is a
very dear and interesting topic. We could almost prophesy
what's in store for our world by the attitude that this
same world will assume toward the yearning of the rem-
nants of European Jewry to settle in Pales- tine. If the
leading nations will do justice to the Jews and return
Palestine to them-then they will also be just in their
international relations with one another and a finer and
nobler world will emerge from the chaos of World War II.
If, on the other hand, the great nations or their leaders
will break sacred pledges and.condemn a tortured people to
per- petual dispersion-then they lack a sense of justice
and will eventually abuse one another, and that will lead
to conflict which will put a finish to human civilization.
Thus the degree in which Jews are enabled to fulfill their
aspirations in Palestine serves, to me, as a barometer of
the conscience of mankind. And, whereas we could do little
to change the course of human destiny, we should at least
be well-informed. And such information. Paul W. Ward's
correspondence provides in a splendid manner. Baltimore,
Oct. 21. DR. JACK SHOBIN. Cheltenham School For Boys TO THE
EDITOR OF THE SUN-Sir: I have been instructed by the board
of managers of the Cheltenham School for Boys to express to
you our appreciation of the editorial of October 15. We
feel that you presented the facts in the situation most
accurately, clearly and persuasively. You may be interested
to know that the contractor has promised us the use of our
security unit within three weeks. While it is a makeshift
use of very poor present equip- ment, we think that it will
provide custody of chronic runaways and protect the
community. The only satisfactory answer to the prob- lem is
the building of the new school, which will furnish these
facilities at their best. Then we can really give the boys
a good training program while providing the custody that
will safeguard any community. President, Board of Managers,
Chelten- ham School for Boys. | Cheltenham, Md., Oct. 22.
F. REID ISAAC,
