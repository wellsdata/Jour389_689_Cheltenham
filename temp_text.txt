GRAND JURY REPORT-Protest Against the Increase of Perjury
and Other Matters.- The grand jury for the January, term,
Mr. R. Q. Taylor, foreman, made its report to Judge Duffy,
in the Criminal Court, on Satur- day, and was discharged
with the thanks of the court. The new grand jury, of which
Mr. David L. Bartlett is foreman, will be swora In today.
The report states that it has been in session 68 days, has
examined 2,620 wit- nesses, made 470 presentments and 435
Indiat- ments, dismissed 532 and continued seven cases, and
has ignored three indictinents, After reviewing the reports
of the various committees, the report says: "This grand
jury, in full accord with its predecessors, presents as
disgraceful to the city, and inimical to justice. the
accommodations provided for witnesses summoned before it.
The rooms are entirely Inadequate in space, are filthy in
appearance, offensive in odor, and such that decent men and
women cannot with self-respect wait in until called before
the grand jury. The grand jury suggests that a great deal
of per- jury might be prevented if the witnesses be- fore
it were sworn by the foreman or assist- ant foreman in the
presence of the body. Many of the witnesses appear to be
not at all impressed by any sense of obligation of an oath
taken in a perfunctory way in the office of the clerk of
the court. The possibly necessary charge for the clerk's
fees could be arranged by order of the court. "While the
grand jury recognizes that the reporting by the police of
the names of per sons entering or leaving on Sunday places
where liquor is sold has done much to pre- vent free
traffle in liquor on Sunday, and prevents much public
intoxication and dis- order, it must express its opinion
that other means are needed to break up this Sunday trade
and provide proof for convictions, and at the same time
stein the alarming flood of perjury that confronts each
successive grand jury when investigating violations of the
Sunday law. The dotective force of the city police should
be authorized and instructed frequently to visit on Sunday
the business places of the more noto- rious violators of
the law, and thus get proof sumelent for conviction. Many
of these, Sunday cases presented to the grand jury excito
reasonable suspicion that the names of persons visiting the
saloons and restaurants who do not drink are given by
interested parties to the police, so as to keep up the
appearance of police inspection and yet escape punishment.
Out of 387 Sunday cases presented by the police during this
term, with 1,307 witnesses, only 59 present- ments could be
found and 328 cases had to bo dismissed. It is said with
regret that in the bellef of the grand jury the majority of
the witnesses were guilty of perjury. "The grand jury is
convinced that the cre- ation of one or more police courts
having jurisdiction in all cases of petty larceny and
simple assault would insure more certain and speedy justice
and save areat,expense. Many offenses against person and
property go un- punished because of the time and trouble
en- tailed upon the parties aggrieved by attend- ing at the
station-house, grand jury and Criminal Court. "The grand
jury regrets that so few of the many rumored open

providing tor the punishment of conspiracies to Injura the
business or to intertere with the em- ployes of persons or
corporations is strongly urged. "There is good reason to
believe that many witnesses are tampered with both before
and after their appearance before the grand jury, by or
with the connivance or advice of attor- neys practicing in
the Criminal Court, though as yet no such suflicient proof
has been pre sented as would warrant indictinent. "The city
of Baltimore is to be congratu- lated on the condition and
character of its police force and marshal, as shown in the
good order and peaco preserved during the excitement
attending the car-drivers' strike, and also evidenced by
apparent honesty and intelligence manifested by those who
have appeared before the grand jury as witnesses."" The
